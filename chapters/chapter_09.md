## Chapter 9 -- The Lord Returns

My father came home with the same subtlety he'd left with, which is to say: none.

The column appeared on the Roseroad on a morning in late 283 AC, trailing dust and noise and banners in a spectacle that could be seen from Highgarden's highest towers a full hour before it arrived at the gates. Trumpets announced the approach because apparently walking through the door like a normal person was beneath the dignity of a man who'd spent the better part of a year eating outside a castle he'd failed to take.

I stood in the courtyard again. Same spot, or close to it. My mother beside me, Garlan on her other side, baby Loras in the arms of a nursemaid behind us. The household lined up in their finest, the way they had when the army marched out, because departures and arrivals were the two occasions that demanded spectacle and Highgarden was very good at spectacle.

Garlan was vibrating. Five years old and practically humming with excitement at the prospect of Father's return, bouncing on his toes the way children do when their bodies contain more energy than their frames can hold. He'd been asking "when is Father coming home?" every day for the last month, with the relentless consistency of a child who has grasped the concept of anticipation but not the concept of patience.

"Today," my mother told him, for the forty-third time. "Today."

The gates opened. The column filed in. And there was Mace.

Bigger. If that was possible, and apparently it was, because a year of feasting outside Storm's End while besieging a starving garrison had added to my father in ways that were visible even from across a courtyard. His armor gleamed -- fresh-polished for the homecoming, no doubt, because the journey home from a siege provided ample time for squires to apply elbow grease. His horse was fresh. His banners were clean. His smile was enormous.

He dismounted with a grace that belied his size, handed his reins to a squire, and swept Garlan off the ground one-armed, the way you'd pick up a particularly enthusiastic cat.

"My boy! Look how you've grown!"

Garlan screamed with delight. My mother stepped forward, and Mace pulled her in with his free arm, and for a moment the three of them were a knot of reunion that was genuine and warm and uncomplicated by the things I knew and they didn't.

Then he turned to me.

"Willas." His eyes were bright. Happy. A man home from war -- even a war that consisted primarily of sitting down -- carried a certain relief in his face, a loosening of tension that expressed itself as something close to joy. "You kept things running. I knew you would."

I hadn't kept things running. My mother had. But this wasn't the moment for accuracy.

"Welcome home, Father."

He ruffled my hair. I let him. Some performances require no effort at all, because the feeling behind them is real even when the gesture is absurd.

He was home. The war was over. And the act that was about to follow -- the feast, the boasts, the magnificent rewriting of history that Mace Tyrell was about to undertake -- would be one of the most educational evenings of my life.

***

The feast began at sundown and showed no signs of ending.

Highgarden's great hall was lit with enough candles to qualify as a fire hazard, the tables groaning under a spread that made the wartime economy feel like a distant memory. Roast boar, honeyed duck, fourteen varieties of cheese, bread so fresh the steam was still rising, fruits and pastries and a soup course that lasted three servings. The wine flowed with a generosity that bordered on assault.

The irony was so thick you could have spread it on the bread.

For a year, the household had rationed. Tightened. Managed. My mother had stretched supplies with quiet competence, Gormon had preserved and portioned with increasing creativity, and the entire machinery of Highgarden's domestic economy had operated under the gentle, persistent pressure of feeding a great household while its lord used the food budget to maintain a luxury siege.

And now that lord was home, and the first thing he did was throw a feast.

I couldn't fault the logic entirely. Feasts served a political function -- they demonstrated wealth, rewarded loyalty, and signaled that the good times were back. The bannermen who'd sent their men and their grain needed to see that the Lord of Highgarden was generous, prosperous, and firmly in control. The servants who'd endured the scarcity needed to see abundance restored. The household needed a release valve, and roast boar was as good a valve as any.

But still. The waste. My carefully preserved barrels of brined vegetables, sitting in the pantry unmolested while fresh produce piled onto every surface. The salt-cured meat I'd worked with Gormon to prepare, rendered redundant by the arrival of fresh stocks. The quiet, patient work of making less go further, swept aside by the tidal wave of excess that Mace Tyrell carried in his wake the way other men carried dust.

I ate my food. I watched my father perform.

***

The performance was magnificent.

Mace stood at the head of the table -- actually stood, pushing back his chair and rising to his full considerable height, a goblet in one hand and the other spread wide in the universal gesture of a man about to say something he considered important and that everyone else would consider long.

"My lords! My friends!" His voice filled the hall the way water fills a basin -- completely, immediately, leaving no room for anything else. "I return to you victorious!"

Victorious. The word landed with the confidence of a man who believed it absolutely.

"When I led the might of the Reach to Storm's End, the rebels trembled! When I set my siege, Stannis Baratheon cowered behind his walls! When I cut off his supply lines by land and Lord Redwyne blocked them by sea, the rebel garrison ate rats and boot leather while we feasted under the banners of House Tyrell!"

This part was true, technically. The siege had cut off supply lines. Stannis had been reduced to eating rats. Mace had indeed feasted. The factual accuracy of the individual elements was not in question. It was the assembly -- the *framing*, as The Book's section on rhetoric and persuasion would call it -- that transformed a military stalemate into a personal triumph.

"Storm's End would have fallen!" Mace declared, and the hall murmured agreement because that was what halls did when lords declared things. "Another month -- two at most -- and the gates would have opened! If the war hadn't ended when it did, I would have taken Storm's End and crushed the last of the rebellion with my own hand!"

The conditional tense. The most useful tense in the language of self-aggrandizement. I *would have*. If only. Given more time. The victory that existed in a hypothetical future where inconvenient reality hadn't intervened was always more glorious than the messy, ambiguous victory that actually happened.

What actually happened: Rhaegar Targaryen died on the Trident with Robert Baratheon's warhammer in his chest. The Lannisters sacked King's Landing. The Targaryen dynasty ended. And Mace Tyrell, who had been sitting outside Storm's End contributing nothing to the resolution of the war one way or the other, marched home and claimed that his siege had been a cornerstone of the Targaryen war effort.

He wasn't lying. Not exactly. He was doing something more interesting than lying -- he was constructing a narrative. And watching him do it, from my seat at the children's end of the table, between Garlan (who was feeding cheese to a dog under the table) and a bannerman's son whose name I'd already forgotten, I learned something valuable.

My father was not stupid.

This was a revision. I'd been operating under the assumption that Mace was well-meaning but dim, a large man with a small intellect, propelled through life by the momentum of wealth and birth. And he was those things, or some of them. But watching him work the hall -- the timing of his pauses, the way he modulated his voice for emphasis, the carefully curated eye contact with specific bannermen at specific moments -- I saw something else.

Mace was a performer. An excellent one. He wasn't constructing his narrative through ignorance; he was constructing it through *instinct*. He knew, on some level beneath conscious analysis, that a lord who returned from war needed a story, and the story needed to be a victory, and the victory needed to feature himself at the center, because a lord without a story was just a man, and a man without a victory was vulnerable.

This was political survival disguised as bragging.

Not sophisticated political survival. Not Olenna-level, gods no. But functional. Workable. The bannermen didn't need to believe every word -- they needed to have a narrative they could repeat, a version of events that made their lord look strong and their own service look meaningful. Mace was providing that narrative with the generosity of a man who had an unlimited supply and was happy to share.

I watched him raise his goblet and toast the Reach, and the hall roared, and I thought: *This is the lever.*

Not Mace's intelligence. Not his martial prowess. Not his political acumen. His *need*. Mace needed to feel important. Needed to be the hero of his own story. And if I could make my own work -- my reforms, my innovations, the slow transformation of the Reach that existed in my notebooks and my plans -- look like extensions of *his* vision, reflections of *his* greatness, achievements that enhanced *his* narrative...

Then he wouldn't just tolerate what I was doing. He'd champion it.

The insight settled into my mind with the satisfying click of a key finding its lock. I filed it away under "things I will use later" and returned to my food.

Garlan, under the table, was teaching the dog to beg. Some skills came naturally.

***

The political aftermath was swift and bloodless, because the Tyrells had always been good at bloodless things.

Robert Baratheon sat the Iron Throne. The Targaryens were dead or fled. And the lords who'd fought for the losing side needed to make their peace with the new order before the new order made peace for them, which historically involved a great deal less peace and a great deal more executions.

The Tyrells bent the knee. Of course they did. Bending the knee was practically a family tradition -- they'd done it for Aegon the Conqueror when the Gardener kings burned, and they did it now for Robert Baratheon with the same pragmatic grace. The Reach was too valuable to punish severely. You could burn a Tyrell, but you couldn't burn a harvest, and the realm needed Reach grain the way a body needs blood.

Grandmother explained this to me in her chambers, three days after the feast, with the brisk efficiency of a woman delivering a lecture she'd prepared in advance.

"We backed the losing side," she said, pouring wine with one hand and gesturing dismissively with the other. "This happens. The important thing is what we have that the winning side needs."

"Food," I said.

"Food." She smiled. It was not a warm smile. It was the smile of a woman who understood that survival was a negotiation and that the Tyrells held the better cards. "Robert Baratheon can hate us or he can feed his kingdom. He is, by all reports, not a complete imbecile. He'll choose food."

"And we get..."

"Pardoned. Our lands, our titles, our position -- all confirmed. In exchange for grain shipments to King's Landing and a general attitude of enthusiastic loyalty to the new regime." She sipped her wine. "Your father will manage the enthusiastic part. He's already composing a letter to Robert praising his courage at the Trident. The irony is not lost on me, and it is entirely lost on him."

I laughed. I couldn't help it. Grandmother had that effect -- her observations were so precisely aimed that laughter was the only reasonable response.

"The realm is reordering itself," she continued, and her voice shifted register. Lighter to heavier. The joke was over; the lesson had begun. "New king. New alliances. New opportunities for houses that position themselves correctly. The Lannisters gave Robert a throne and a wife. The Starks gave him a war. The Baratheons gave him a name. What do the Tyrells give him?"

"Grain."

"Grain. The most boring and most essential thing in the world." She fixed me with a look that I felt in my spine. "Remember that, Willas. When you are lord of Highgarden -- and you will be, gods willing and your father's arteries permitting -- remember that the power of this house has never been swords or dragons or cleverness. It has been the dirt. The fields. The endless, unglamorous, absolutely vital capacity to grow things and feed people. Everything else -- the knights, the banners, your father's tournament victories that he likes to embellish until they bear no resemblance to what actually occurred -- is decoration. The food is the foundation."

She was telling me something she thought I needed to hear. She didn't know I'd been thinking about nothing else for four years.

"I understand, Grandmother."

"Good." She set down her wine. "Now. Tell me about these preserved vegetables your man Gormon has been making. The ones that taste like vinegar and last through the Long Night."

I told her. Not everything -- never everything -- but enough. The brining. The salt-curing. The methods I'd "read about" in her books (a fiction she accepted with a raised eyebrow that acknowledged the fiction without challenging it). The results: food that lasted longer, wasted less, stretched further.

She listened. She asked three questions, each sharper than the last. And when I finished, she nodded once, decisively, and said, "Good. Now do more."

Not "well done." Not "how clever." *Do more.* The most Olenna thing she could have said. Approval expressed as a command.

I would. I was already planning to.

***

The household settled. The war receded into memory. The grain wagons resumed their normal schedules, the stores refilled, the servants stopped counting in whispers, and Highgarden returned to the comfortable abundance that was its natural state, the way a river returns to its banks after a flood.

Mace adjusted to peacetime with the seamless ease of a man for whom war and peace were essentially the same thing: a series of occasions to eat, drink, and tell stories about himself. He hosted visitors. He wrote letters. He maintained the fiction of his siege triumph with the tireless dedication of a man who had discovered that if you tell a story often enough, it becomes true, at least for the people who weren't there.

My mother, relieved of wartime responsibility, returned to the gentler rhythms of a lady of a great house. She did not, I noticed, relinquish control of the household accounts. Mace didn't ask for them back. Some changes, once made, were permanent.

Loras grew from a baby into a toddler with the kind of golden beauty that made strangers stop and stare. He was going to be trouble. I could tell already.

Garlan grew into his fifth year with an enthusiasm for everything that was exhausting and endearing in equal measure. He'd taken to following Ser Vortimer's squires around the training yard, mimicking their drills with a stick, and his form was -- I had to admit -- remarkably good for a child his age. Natural talent. The kind my father wished I had and I wished I could give him.

And then there was Margaery.

She'd been born during the war -- I'd been told, not present, as birth was not considered appropriate viewing for a seven-year-old heir, which was reasonable given the medical conditions. A girl. My mother's joy was deep and genuine. Mace's was only slightly performative -- he'd wanted another boy, but Mace's disappointments had the lifespan of soap bubbles, and within a week he was doting on his daughter with the same boundless, uncritical warmth he applied to everything he loved.

Margaery was perfect. Round, pink, possessed of a set of lungs that could wake the dead and probably had. She cried with conviction and slept with intensity and existed with the absolute certainty of an infant who had no doubt whatsoever that the world was arranged for her personal convenience.

I held her once, carefully, sitting in a chair because standing while holding a baby was an act of reckless courage I wasn't prepared for. She looked at me with eyes that didn't focus properly yet and made a sound that might have been contentment and might have been gas.

"Hello," I said. "I'm your brother. I apologize in advance for the family."

She blinked. Fair enough.

The Tyrells were now five. Six, counting Grandmother. Seven, counting Father. A family. Growing, sprawling, noisy, and mine.

***

I took stock on a quiet evening, alone in my room with my notebook and a candle and the kind of silence that only exists in a castle after the household has gone to bed and the stones themselves seem to hold their breath.

I was eight. Eight years old, with four years of careful, patient, invisible work behind me and -- if the gods or whatever governed this world were feeling generous -- decades more ahead.

What had I built?

I opened the notebook. Not the first one -- that was retired, its most sensitive pages long since burned, its surviving contents innocent enough to be found without consequence. This was the third notebook, and it was different. More structured. More ambitious. More dangerous, though the danger was still theoretical, because a child's notebook remained the safest hiding place in Westeros.

The first section: Agriculture.

Companion planting, confirmed and quietly spreading through Harmon's garden. He'd expanded the bean-and-wheat beds three times, and other gardeners had started copying his methods, the way innovations spread in a pre-literate society -- through observation, imitation, and the practical logic of "his crops look better than mine." Not dramatic. Not fast. But real.

Food preservation, upgraded. Gormon's pantry now routinely used the improved salt-curing and brining techniques, and the kitchen staff had adapted them with the innovations that practical people always contribute when they take ownership of a new method. One of the cooks had started smoking cheese with a technique I hadn't suggested, having extrapolated from the salt-curing principles to a related but distinct preservation method. This was the best possible outcome -- the knowledge was propagating, evolving, becoming something bigger than what I'd started.

The second section: Observations.

Detailed notes on Highgarden's economy, its supply chains, its labor patterns, its waste streams. How much grain moved through the castle in a month. How many cartloads went to market versus how many stayed in the stores. Where the bottlenecks were -- the mill that could only grind so much per day, the roads that turned to mud in autumn, the bridge over the Mander tributary that was too narrow for two wagons to pass and created delays that rippled through the entire transportation network.

Data. The boring, essential, revolutionary stuff.

The third section was new. I'd started it during the war and expanded it in the months since. I called it, in my private shorthand, *The Tree*.

It was a dependency chart. Not as clean or as formal as the project management diagrams in The Book's engineering sections, but the same basic concept: a map of what needed to happen before other things could happen. A visual representation of the order in which the world needed to change.

At the bottom, the roots: agricultural improvement. Better yields, better preservation, more efficient use of land and labor. This was Phase One. This was what I'd been doing.

Above that, the trunk: economic foundation. Trade networks, standardized measurements, basic record-keeping practices. The infrastructure that turned agricultural surplus into wealth. Phase Two. Coming soon.

Above that, the branches: technology. Better metalworking (you needed metal tools to build machines). Better woodworking (you needed structural knowledge to build mills and presses). Basic chemistry (you needed charcoal and solvents and acids for a hundred different processes). Phase Three. Years away.

And at the top, the crown: the printing press. The thing that would change everything. The technology that would make knowledge reproducible, portable, persistent. The machine that would turn one copy of an agricultural manual into a thousand copies, put them in the hands of every lord and steward and farmer in the Reach, and make the entire transformation self-sustaining.

The press was the goal. Everything else was prerequisite.

I traced the lines of The Tree with my finger, following the paths from root to crown, and felt something I hadn't expected.

Hope. The structural kind — not a wish but a calculation. The math worked. The path was viable. The only variable was time.

I had time. I was eight.

I closed the notebook, slid it under my mattress, and blew out the candle. In the dark, I could hear the wind in the gardens and the distant sound of the Mander, and somewhere above me — two corridors over and one floor up — my grandmother was probably awake too, turning over pieces of the same game.

The tree had roots. Time to grow the trunk.

Tomorrow I would ask my mother about the abandoned outbuilding near the southern garden. The one with the good chimney and the stone floor and the door that locked.

A workshop needed a space. And I had plans that wouldn't fit in a notebook much longer.
