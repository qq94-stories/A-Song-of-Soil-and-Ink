[H2]Chapter 13 — Distilled Spirits[/H2]

The test plots were in the ground. The first cycle of wheat and turnips and barley and clover was growing on Merrell's twenty acres, doing what plants do, which is to say: nothing visible, very slowly, while I stood at the edge of the fields willing them to grow faster and accomplishing exactly as much as you'd expect. Donal kept his records. Harmon checked the soil. The south fields did their work at the pace the south fields chose, and there was nothing I could do to hurry them along.

So I did what any restless engineer does when one project is in the waiting phase. I started another.

The idea came to me, as the best ideas often do, while watching my father drink.

Mace was holding court in the great hall on an evening that blurred into all the other evenings, a mid-week gathering of household knights and minor bannermen, the kind of social event that served as lubricant for Highgarden's political machinery and as excuse for Mace to tell his siege stories to a fresh audience. The wine was Arbor gold, as always, because Highgarden didn't serve anything less and Mace considered Dornish red a personal insult.

He was on his third goblet. The stories were getting better, by which I mean longer and less accurate. The Battle of Ashford, in which Mace had played a role roughly equivalent to that of a boulder in a landslide — present, large, and swept along by forces entirely beyond his control, was undergoing its weekly enhancement. By next month, he'd have personally unhorsed Robert Baratheon. By next year, he'd have done it twice.

But I wasn't watching the performance. I was watching the wine.

Good wine. Excellent wine, actually. The Arbor produced vintages that could compete with anything from Essos and that formed one of the Reach's most valuable exports. But wine was, at its core, a low-proof alcoholic beverage. Eight to fourteen percent ethanol by volume, depending on the grape, the fermentation, and the vintner's skill. The rest was water, sugar, tannins, and the complex organic chemistry that gave wine its flavor and its reputation and its ability to make my father increasingly creative with recent history.

Eight to fourteen percent. Nature's ceiling, imposed by the yeast itself, the microorganisms that converted sugar to alcohol were killed by their own product once the ethanol concentration reached a certain threshold. You couldn't ferment past roughly 15 percent. Biology wouldn't allow it.

But you could [I]distill[/I] past it. That was the whole point.

Distillation. The separation of a liquid mixture into its component parts through selective evaporation and condensation. Ethanol boiled at 78.4°C (173°F). Water boiled at 100 degrees. Heat a fermented liquid to somewhere between those two temperatures, and the ethanol evaporated first, rising as vapor while the water stayed behind. Capture that vapor. Cool it. Condense it back to liquid.

What you got was spirits. Concentrated alcohol, 40 to 60 percent by volume on a single pass, higher with redistillation. Stronger than wine. More durable than wine. Spirits didn't spoil, didn't sour, didn't turn to vinegar in the barrel the way wine did when the gods were feeling uncooperative. Spirits lasted forever, shipped anywhere, and commanded prices that made even Arbor gold look like tavern swill.

And nobody in Westeros was making them. Wine, yes. Beer, certainly. Mead, in the North. Fermented everything, everywhere, because fermentation was one of the oldest technologies in human history and every culture that had access to sugar and time had figured it out independently. But distillation, the step beyond fermentation, the technology that concentrated alcohol from a drink into a [I]substance[/I], was either unknown or so rare as to be invisible. The Book mentioned that the Free Cities had various spirits, and the Essosi had something called [I]firewine[/I], but the technology hadn't crossed the Narrow Sea in any systematic way.

Which meant it was mine to introduce. I sat in the great hall, watching my father drink wine, and I thought about copper pots and condensation coils and a revenue stream that would fund everything else I wanted to build.

[HR]

The coppersmith's name was Aldric.

Every castle of Highgarden's size had one. Copper was used for cooking pots, water pipes, decorative fixtures, roof flashing, and the hundred other applications where you needed a metal that was workable, corrosion-resistant, and didn't poison the food. Aldric had his own workshop adjacent to Tobho's forge, a smaller space that smelled of flux and metal polish instead of coal smoke, and he worked precisely, because copper cost too much to waste — though "quiet" was the last word anyone would use to describe him.

I'd spoken to Tobho first. The still was a coppersmith's project, and Tobho knew that, but he'd become my gateway to Highgarden's craft community, the man whose endorsement carried weight among the artisans. When Tobho said "the lad knows his business," it meant something. It opened doors.

"Aldric's your man," Tobho told me, wiping soot from his hands with a rag that redistributed the soot more than it removed it. "Best copperwork in the Reach. Maybe the best I've seen anywhere." He paused. "Talks your ear off, though. Fair warning."

This was Tobho's version of a glowing recommendation, and the warning turned out to be understatement. I found Aldric in his workshop, shaping a replacement panel for one of Highgarden's kitchen cauldrons. He was a lean man, maybe thirty, with quick hands and quicker mouth. He'd been humming when I walked in, and the humming stopped only long enough for him to register my presence before being replaced by words, a stream of them, as natural and continuous as water from a tap.

"Young lord Willas! Tobho said you'd be coming by. Something about a project, he said, wouldn't tell me what, which is typical of Tobho, the man treats words like gold coins, won't spend one if he can help it. Come in, come in, mind the flux pot, I've been resoldering the east wing drainpipes all morning and the place is a disaster. What can I do for you?"

This was, I would learn, how Aldric operated. He talked the way other craftsmen hammered: constantly, rhythmically, and with a surprising amount of precision buried in the volume. Half of what he said was filler. The other half was sharp observation delivered at the same pace as the filler, so you had to listen carefully or you'd miss the useful parts entirely.

"I have a project," I said, seizing a gap between sentences the way you'd jump onto a moving cart. "If you're interested."

"Interested! I'm always interested. Bored is the word for a coppersmith with nothing new to work on, and I've been bored since the last set of kitchen pots. Show me what you've got."

I laid the drawings on his workbench. I'd spent three evenings on them, translating The Book's diagrams of a simple pot still into something that looked like an ambitious child's design project. The key components were labeled in my best handwriting, which was adequate for an eight-year-old and terrible for a technical document, but the underlying engineering was sound.

A pot. A tapered neck rising from the pot, the [I]lyne arm[/I], though I didn't call it that because the term didn't exist here. A long coil of copper tubing descending from the lyne arm into a barrel of cold water. And a collection vessel at the end of the coil.

[IMG]https://raw.githubusercontent.com/qq94-stories/A-Song-of-Soil-and-Ink/main/images/published/IMAGE%2011.png[/IMG]

"It's a... cooker," I said. "For wine. You heat the wine in the pot, and the steam goes up through the neck and down through the coil, and the cold water around the coil turns the steam back into liquid. But the liquid that comes out is stronger than the wine that went in."

Aldric studied the drawing, and for the first time since I'd entered the workshop, he went quiet. Ten seconds. Maybe fifteen. He traced the joints with a finger, followed the curves, tapped the coil.

Then the dam broke.

"Right, so, this is a pot, obviously, with a neck rising off the top, tapered, that's good, and the neck feeds into a coil here, copper tubing I'd guess, wound into a spiral, which is going to be the fiddly part because copper work-hardens if you bend it cold so I'd need to anneal between each turn. What diameter on the tubing? Two fingers? And the coil, how wide, barrel-sized? Because if it's sitting in water for cooling, which I think is what this barrel here is for, the tighter the spiral the more surface contact you get, which means better cooling, which means—" He looked up. "What's it [I]for[/I]?"

"Heating wine," I said. "The steam rises through the neck, travels down the coil, and the cold water turns it back into liquid. Stronger liquid."

"Stronger wine. Huh." He was already back at the drawing. "The pot, how big are we thinking? Ten gallons? Twenty? Because the joint here between the pot and the neck, that's where you'll lose your steam if it's not sealed properly. Now, I could solder it, that'd hold, but for a first try I'd say luting paste, we use it on pipe joints all the time, flour-and-water base with a bit of ash mixed in, hardens up nicely when it heats. Permanent version I'd braze, but let's see if the thing works first before I commit the silver solder."

He hadn't asked me if he could build it. He'd skipped straight to how. I was starting to understand why Tobho had recommended him. Behind the torrent of words was a mind that worked fast, evaluated as it talked, and arrived at solutions while other men were still formulating questions.

"Materials will cost," he added, almost as an afterthought. "Copper's not cheap."

"I know." Grandmother's funding. The quiet, invisible support that had been enabling my projects since the garden beds. "I can cover the cost."

"Then I can build it. Give me a week."

[HR]

It took nine days. Aldric was meticulous, which I respected even as my patience frayed. Each section of the still was shaped, tested, adjusted, and reshaped until Aldric was satisfied — and Aldric was never satisfied on the first pass. The coil alone took two days: copper tubing, wound by hand around a wooden form, annealed between each bend to prevent cracking, then fitted into the barrel with the precision of a watch spring.

The finished still was a thing of beauty. Not in the aesthetic sense (it looked, frankly, like a copper octopus that had been involved in an industrial accident) but in the functional sense. Clean joints. Smooth curves. A pot that sat level, a neck that tapered evenly, a coil that spiraled without kinks or flat spots.

Aldric delivered it to my workshop on a morning when the light was good and my anticipation was barely contained. He set it on the work table with both hands, stepped back, and regarded it with the proprietary satisfaction of a craftsman surveying a finished piece.

"There she is. Nine days, fourteen annealing cycles on that coil, and I resoldered the neck joint twice because the first time it had a hair's width gap that would've driven me mad. Not that anyone else would've noticed, but I'd have known, and that's worse. If this does what you say it does, my lord, I want to know everything. How it works, why it works, what else you've got in that head of yours, because I've been making pots and pipes for ten years and this is the first interesting thing anyone's asked me to build."

"If it works," I said, "I'll tell you everything."

"It'll work. The engineering's sound." He tapped the coil. "I don't know what it [I]does[/I], but I know what it [I]is[/I], and what it is, is well-made." He grinned, waved once, and left still talking as he walked back toward his workshop, his voice trailing off across the yard.

And I was alone with a copper still and a jug of wine and the giddy, terrifying knowledge that I was about to create the Reach's first distilled spirit.

The science was simple. The practice was... instructive.

I set up the still on the brick base that Joram (the mason, my old friend from the kiln project) had built to my specifications: a small fire pit with a grate, sized to hold the pot at the correct height above the flames. The pot went on the grate. The wine went in the pot, three gallons of a middling Reach white that I'd liberated from the kitchen stores with Gormon's reluctant cooperation and a promise that the experiment would produce "something interesting."

The lid went on. The luting paste sealed the joint. The condenser barrel was filled with cold water drawn from the well that morning. The coil was connected to the lyne arm, its lower end positioned over a clean jug.

I lit the fire. Small at first. Controlled. The temperature needed to climb slowly; too fast and you'd overshoot, boiling the water along with the ethanol and defeating the entire purpose. The target was somewhere between 78-85°C (172-185°F). Above the boiling point of ethanol. Below the boiling point of water. A narrow window that required patience and attention and the willingness to stand next to a copper pot watching for the first sign of vapor.

Harmon was there. He'd been in from the beginning — the beans, the kiln, the charcoal — and he considered everything that followed to be partly his, which it was. He didn't understand what I was doing. "Cooking wine" was his description, delivered with the skepticism of a man who thought wine was perfectly fine uncooked, but he trusted the process because he trusted me, and trust was the foundation of everything.

The pot heated. The wine warmed. I could smell it, the sharp, sweet scent of warm alcohol cutting through the workshop's usual aroma of charcoal and oak and the faint metallic tang that Tobho's forge contributed to everything within a fifty-yard radius.

After perhaps twenty minutes, the lyne arm grew warm under my fingertips and a bead of condensation formed on the outside of the copper, the tube sweating as hot vapor met cooler metal. I was watching for it with an intensity that probably looked unhinged. The vapor traveled up the arm, over the curve, and down into the coil, where it met the cold water of the condenser barrel and—

A drop. A single, clear drop fell from the coil's outlet into the waiting jug.

Then another. Then a thin, steady stream, no thicker than a thread, of clear liquid that caught the light from the window and gleamed like something precious.

Because it was.

I waited. The stream continued, thin and steady, the still doing its work with the patient efficiency of simple physics:ethanol evaporating, rising, condensing, falling. No moving parts. No complex machinery. Just heat, copper, and cold water, combining to do what nature couldn't: separate alcohol from the liquid that had birthed it.

After an hour, the jug held perhaps a pint. I removed it from beneath the coil, replaced it with a second jug, and examined the first batch.

Clear. Almost colorless, with a faint golden tinge from the wine. I sniffed it.

The smell was immediate and absolute. Sharp, burning, aggressively alcoholic, the concentrated punch of ethanol at a concentration many times higher than anything fermented. My eyes watered. My nose objected. The parts of my brain that were eight years old suggested, firmly, that this was not for children.

I dipped a finger. Tasted it.

The first thing I registered was heat. Sensation, not temperature. The liquid burned across my tongue with an intensity that was startling even though I'd expected it. The flavor behind the burn was rough, raw, harsh, all the characteristics of a first distillation, which concentrated not just the ethanol but the fusel oils, the methanol traces, the harsh congeners that made raw spirits taste like punishment.

But underneath the harshness was [I]potential[/I]. The clean, strong, undeniable fact of concentrated alcohol. Maybe 40 percent ethanol by volume, I estimated. Rough, but real.

"Well?" Harmon asked, watching me with the expression of a man who was wondering if the lord's heir had just poisoned himself.

"It works." My voice came out slightly hoarse, which was the spirit's fault and not the emotion's, though the emotion was substantial. "It's rough. It needs refining. But it works."

"What [I]is[/I] it?"

I held up the jug. "Concentrated wine. The strong part of the wine, separated from the weak part. This is..." I searched for a word that existed in this world's vocabulary. "Spirits."

"Spirits." He peered into the jug, suspicious but unable to turn down something that smelled that strongly of adventure. "Can I try?"

"A small sip. It's much stronger than wine."

He sipped. His eyes widened. His face performed an extraordinary sequence of expressions — surprise, alarm, assessment, and then, slowly, grudging respect.

"Seven hells," he said. "That's... that's something."

Something. Yes. Something that was about to change the Reach's economy, though Harmon didn't need to know that yet.

The second distillation was the breakthrough. First-pass spirits were rough. I knew this from The Book, which explained in merciless detail the chemistry of distillation byproducts: methanol (toxic, boiled at 64.7°C (148°F), came over in the first cut, needed to be discarded), fusel alcohols (harsh, contributed to hangovers, concentrated in the later cuts), and the complex organic compounds that gave raw spirits their aggressive, unrefined character.

The solution was double distillation. Take the first-pass spirits, put them back in the pot, and distill again. The second pass concentrated the ethanol further, pushing toward 60 to 70 percent, while allowing more precise separation of the undesirable fractions. Discard the first trickle (the [I]heads[/I], rich in methanol). Collect the middle run (the [I]hearts[/I], the good stuff). Discard the late run (the [I]tails[/I], heavy with fusel oils).

Heads, hearts, tails. The distiller's trinity.

I ran the second distillation on a morning when the workshop was warm and the light was golden and I had the specific, focused calm that comes from doing something you understand completely and doing it right.

The heads went into a waste jug. Maybe the first cup, clear liquid that smelled sharper and more chemical than what followed. I sniffed, winced, and set it aside. Methanol. Toxic in quantity, useless for anything except possibly cleaning metal.

Then the hearts came. And they were different.

Clearer. Cleaner. The harshness of the first distillation had softened, the raw edge refined into something that was still unmistakably strong but no longer aggressive. The burn was there; it would always be there, at this concentration, but behind it was a smoothness, a depth, something that suggested the drink this could become with aging and care.

I tasted it. The heat bloomed across my tongue, through my palate, down my throat, and settled in my chest with a warmth that was almost pleasant. The flavor was clean, grain-sharp, slightly sweet, with a finish that lingered without punishing.

This was spirits. Real spirits, the kind that men would pay good money for, the kind that would keep in a barrel for years and improve with time, the kind that represented a concentration of value (more alcohol per barrel, more shelf life per gallon, more profit per shipment) that no wine or beer could match.

I poured a small measure into a cup. Held it up to the light. Clear as water. Strong as ambition.

The presentation to Mace was Olenna's idea, though she'd never have called it that. Grandmother didn't [I]tell[/I] me to present the spirits to my father. She suggested, with the razor-edged delicacy of a woman who understood the difference between a command and a manipulation, that my father might enjoy "sampling his heir's latest project."

"The boy's been making something in that workshop of his," she said at dinner, casually, to no one in particular, which meant to Mace specifically. "Something to drink, I'm told."

Mace's head swiveled. If there was one word guaranteed to capture my father's attention at a dining table, it was [I]drink[/I]. If there were two, they were [I]something[/I] and [I]drink[/I]. The combination hit his interest like a key hitting a lock.

"To drink?" His eyebrows rose. Curiosity and appetite, the twin engines of Mace Tyrell's personality, engaged simultaneously. "What kind of something?"

"I've been experimenting," I said, with the carefully measured enthusiasm of a boy presenting a school project. "With wine. Refining it. Making it... stronger."

"Stronger wine?" Mace leaned forward. His goblet, I noticed, was already empty. "How much stronger?"

"Why don't you try it, dear," my mother suggested, and I caught the glance between her and Grandmother, the quick, practiced look of two women who'd been managing Mace Tyrell between them for decades and who had coordinated this moment with the silent efficiency of a military operation.

I produced the bottle. I'd decanted the double-distilled spirits into a ceramic flask, because presentation mattered and a clean flask was more impressive than a jug that smelled like a workshop. I poured a small measure into a clean cup, a proper cup, not a goblet, because the serving size for spirits was different from wine and I didn't want my father to drink a gobletful and fall off his chair.

Mace took the cup. Sniffed it. His eyebrows climbed further.

"That's... vigorous."

He sipped, and the transformation was immediate. Mace Tyrell was a man who experienced flavors the way other people experienced weather, as large, obvious phenomena that affected his entire body. His eyes widened. His cheeks flushed. His mouth opened, closed, opened again.

"Gods," he said.

"It's called spirits," I said. "Distilled wine. The essence of the wine, concentrated. Stronger and longer-lasting."

"It's [I]magnificent[/I]." Mace took another sip. Larger this time. The flush deepened. "My boy made this? [I]My[/I]boy?"

The possessive carried an enormous weight of parental pride, and I felt it land with a complex mix of genuine warmth and calculated satisfaction. This was what I'd wanted: Mace delighted, Mace claiming ownership, Mace wrapping my innovation in the narrative of [I]my son, the clever one, look what he's done[/I].

"The lad's been working in that workshop for months," Harmon said. I'd positioned him at the table for exactly this purpose, the trusted servant confirming the heir's accomplishment, grounding the claim in testimony from someone Mace respected. "Built the apparatus himself. Well — had it built. Designed it, I should say. From those foreign books of his."

"Foreign books!" Mace beamed. "My mother's books, no doubt. Always filling the boy's head with ideas." He said this with such broad affection that it was impossible to tell whether he considered ideas a good thing or a bad thing, and the answer was probably both: good because they produced interesting drinks, bad because they were not swordsmanship.

"May I?" Grandmother extended her hand. I poured her a measure.

She sipped. Her face did not change. Olenna Tyrell's face changed only when she wanted it to, and at this moment she wanted it to be a mask of polite interest rather than the expression of a woman who had just calculated the commercial value of what she was tasting.

"Interesting," she said. The word was neutral. Her eyes were not.

"It lasts," I said, directing this to the table at large but meaning it for her. "In barrels. Without spoiling. It can travel, by road, by ship, without turning. And it's..." I paused, choosing the word. "Compact. More value in less volume."

"More [I]drink[/I] in less volume," Mace corrected happily, missing the economics and hitting the personal relevance with unerring precision. "My son the winemaker! No — what did you call it? Spirit-maker!" He raised his cup. "To Willas!And to spirits!"

The household raised their cups, because you raised your cups when the lord raised his, and several people drank things they had not expected to drink that evening and wore expressions that ranged from delighted to alarmed.

Mace poured himself another measure. Then another. He was, I realized with a mix of affection and calculation, going to be my most enthusiastic customer.

Grandmother found me in the workshop the next morning. I'd expected this; her appearance at the dinner had been too controlled, too carefully timed, for a woman who did nothing without purpose.

She closed the door. Examined the still, which I'd left set up on its brick base, with the appraising eye of a woman who evaluated everything by the same metric: [I]what is this worth to me?[/I]

"How much can you produce?" she asked. No preamble. No compliments.

"With this still? A gallon a day, maybe. Depends on the wine supply."

"And if you had a larger still?"

"More. Much more. The design scales. A bigger pot, a bigger coil, the same process." I paused. "Aldric could build a still twice this size in two weeks. Three times, if I redesigned the condenser."

"And the quality?"

"Improves with practice. I'm learning the cuts, which part of the run to keep and which to discard. And aging. Wine improves with age. Spirits should too, in the right barrels." Oak barrels. The Book was specific: charred oak, which imparted color, flavor, and smoothness to raw spirits over months and years. But I kept that to myself. One step at a time.

Olenna claimed my work stool, the only seat in the workshop, with the natural authority of a woman who had never waited for a chair in her life. She was quiet for a moment, and I could see her working through the implications faster than I'd expected.

"The Arbor produces the finest wines in Westeros," she said. "The Redwynes have held that trade for centuries. Your spirits don't compete with wine. They sit above it." She said this as a statement, not a question. She'd already seen what I'd spent days thinking through. "Different product. Different price. A barrel of this keeps forever, ships without spoiling, and takes less space than wine for more value."

I opened my mouth to make the case I'd rehearsed. Closed it. She'd just made it for me, better and faster.

"The question," she continued, tapping the still with one finger, "is the apparatus. Who else can build one?"

"Anyone with a coppersmith and the principle. The design isn't complicated."

"Then we don't sell the design. We don't teach the process. We sell the spirits." She looked at me. "You've thought of this."

"I have."

"Good. Because if you'd come to me proposing to share the method, I'd have been disappointed." She stood and walked a slow circle around the still. "The product is public. The method stays in Highgarden. Let the Arbor keep their wine. We'll sell what comes after wine, and they'll wonder how we make it, and wondering is good for business."

I'd been prepared to convince her of the monopoly strategy. I'd rehearsed arguments about intellectual property, first-mover advantage, the economics of scarcity. I didn't need any of them. Olenna had been running trade strategy since before my father was born. She didn't need a child to explain how to corner a market.

But I could offer her something she didn't have.

"There's one more thing," I said. "The spirits. At high concentration, much stronger than what I gave Father, strong enough to burn, they have a medical use."

She stopped circling. That got her attention in a way the business case hadn't, because the business case was territory she already owned. Medicine was new ground.

"Medical use?"

"Wound treatment. High-proof spirits kill the... the things that make wounds go bad." I chose my words with care. Germ theory was centuries away from this world's understanding. I couldn't say [I]bacteria[/I]. I couldn't say [I]antiseptic[/I]. But I could frame it the way I'd framed the bandages during the war: observation, not theory. [I]What works[/I], not [I]why it works[/I]. "Spirits strong enough to burn, applied to a wound, prevent corruption. I've read accounts from the Free Cities. Their surgeons use it."

The fictional Free Cities surgeons, joining the fictional Qohorik metallurgists and the fictional Volantene agronomists in my growing pantheon of convenient foreign experts.

"Wounds," Olenna said slowly. "Battlefield wounds?"

"Any wounds. Cuts. Burns. Surgical wounds, if the maesters can ever be persuaded."

She was quiet again. Longer this time. I watched her rearrange things behind those dark eyes, slotting the medical angle into the commercial framework she'd already built, finding where the pieces fit together.

"So your little copper pot produces a luxury drink that funds the operation [I]and[/I] a wound treatment that saves lives. And you give the medicine to our own healers."

"At cost," I said, and then stopped, because I realized I didn't actually know how to distribute it without drawing the wrong kind of attention.

"At cost," she repeated, and the way she said it told me she'd already solved the problem I hadn't finished formulating. "Charity invites suspicion. But a lord who supplies his own healers with better medicine? That's simply good management. The smallfolk who benefit will know where it came from. The lords who hear about it will see prudence, not generosity." She paused. "And prudence, unlike generosity, doesn't make enemies."

I hadn't thought of it that way. I'd been thinking about logistics: how many healers, how much spirit, what concentration. She was thinking about perception: how it looked, who noticed, what story it told. The gap between my knowledge and her experience was the width of a lifetime, and moments like this reminded me that The Book, for all its wonders, had nothing in it about how to be Olenna Tyrell.

"How old are you?" she asked. Not because she didn't know. Because she wanted to hear me say it.

"Nine, Grandmother. Almost."

"Nine." She smoothed her skirts. Walked to the door. Turned back.

"I'm going to fund a second still. Larger. And a space to house it, something more permanent than this shed, charming as it is." She paused. "The spirits will be produced under Tyrell seal. Distributed through channels I'll establish. The profits go into a separate ledger that I will manage personally, and your father will be told only what he needs to know, which is that his clever son's hobby is making money."

"I'll need to train people," I said. "The process isn't difficult, but the cuts matter. Keep the wrong fraction and you poison someone. Keep the right one and it's smooth as silk. I can't run every batch myself forever."

"Naturally." She'd already thought of this, I could tell. "Who?"

"Two or three people to start. Trustworthy, sharp enough to learn the timing and the temperatures, loyal enough to keep the method to themselves." I'd been thinking about this since the second distillation. The heads, the hearts, the tails — each one required judgement that came from practice, and practice meant training, and training meant trusting someone with the process I'd just agreed to keep secret. "Aldric already understands the apparatus."

"Household servants," Olenna said. "Young ones. People who've grown up in Highgarden service, who have no ties to guilds or outside lords. People whose livelihood depends entirely on us." She ticked requirements off on her fingers like she was reading from a list she'd already written. "Loyal. Discreet. Smart enough to learn, not ambitious enough to sell what they've learned. I have a few in mind."

Of course she did. Olenna kept a mental ledger of every servant, retainer, and dependent in Highgarden the way Merrell kept his accounts. She knew who was reliable, who talked, who owed debts, and who could be trusted with something valuable. I'd been thinking about the problem for days. She'd solved it in the time it took to sit down.

"Thank you, Grandmother."

"Don't thank me. Produce results." The door opened. "And Willas?"

"Yes?"

"Your father is going to tell everyone he meets that his son invented a miraculous new drink. Let him. The more he boasts about the product, the less anyone asks about the process."

She left. The door closed. The still gleamed in the morning light, warm and patient, waiting for the next batch.

Time to scale up.
