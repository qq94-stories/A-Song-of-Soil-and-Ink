[H2]Chapter 8 -- War Comes Home[/H2]

The first thing war took from Highgarden was the noise.

Not the sound of battle -- we were hundreds of kilometres from that, safe behind the Reach's rolling green distance and the comfortable fiction that wars happened to other people in other places. No, the noise that disappeared was the ordinary kind. The daily clamor of a great household running at full capacity: stable boys shouting, smiths hammering, the constant rumble of carts on cobblestones, dogs barking at nothing because dogs considered nothing a serious threat that required immediate vocal attention.

When the army marched, it took all of that with it. And what was left behind was a silence that pretended to be peace and wasn't.

My father rode to war on a bright morning in the second month of 282 AC. I was seven years old. I stood in the courtyard with my mother and my brother Garlan and watched the most spectacular display of organized waste I had ever witnessed.

It was, I had to admit, impressive.

Three hundred knights in full armor, their plate and mail catching the morning sun until the whole column flashed and glittered like some enormous, lethal parade float. Behind them, twice that number of mounted men-at-arms. Behind [I]them[/I], the infantry -- pikemen, crossbowmen, and the general mass of levied men who'd been pulled from fields and workshops and taverns to go stand outside a castle and wait for something to happen. Wagons beyond counting, loaded with food and fodder and the extraordinary quantity of [I]stuff[/I] that an army required to exist. Banners everywhere. The green hand of House Tyrell. The golden rose. Pennants and streamers and standards fluttering in a spring breeze that smelled like flowers and horse dung in roughly equal measure.

The Reach was going to war for the Targaryens, and I'd been piecing together why from fragments overheard at dinner tables and in hallways where adults forgot that children had ears.

The story, as far as I could assemble it: Prince Rhaegar had crowned a Northern girl at a tourney instead of his wife. The girl had disappeared — taken, or gone willingly, depending on who was telling it. Her brother and father had gone to King's Landing to demand answers, and King Aerys had burned them alive, because the king was mad, and mad kings did things like that. The dead men's families had called their banners. Robert Baratheon, who'd been betrothed to the girl, had raised a rebellion. The Starks, the Arryns, the Baratheons, the Tullys — half the realm, marching south.

And the Tyrells had answered the crown's call, because the Iron Throne had commanded and Mace Tyrell was not the sort of man who weighed the justice of a cause when the alternative was the spectacular opportunity to ride to war at the head of the largest army in Westeros.

My father sat his warhorse at the head of the column, and I will say this for Mace Tyrell: the man knew how to sit a horse. Whatever his other deficiencies -- and I catalogued them privately with the dispassionate thoroughness of a child who loved his father and saw him clearly, which was the cruelest combination of traits a son could possess -- Mace Tyrell on horseback, in armor, with his banners flying behind him, looked exactly like what he wanted to be. A great lord. A commander. A man of consequence, riding to do consequential things.

He'd come to say goodbye that morning, still half in his armor, squires trailing behind him like ducklings. He'd picked up Garlan, who was four and delighted by the attention, and swung him in a circle while Garlan shrieked with joy. He'd kissed my mother, who held his face in both hands and said something I couldn't hear that made his eyes go bright for a moment before the performance reasserted itself. Then he'd turned to me.

"Look after things while I'm away, Willas." A big hand on my shoulder, heavy with the gauntlet he hadn't put on yet. "You're the man of Highgarden until I return."

I wasn't. My mother was. The steward was. Half a dozen castellans and household officers were. I was seven, and the man of Highgarden until he returned was whoever had the authority and competence to keep the place running, which was everyone except me. But this was Mace's way -- the grand gesture, the dramatic delegation of authority that was entirely symbolic and entirely sincere.

"I will, Father."

He smiled. The big one. The one that could light a room or, at this particular moment, a courtyard full of armed men who would follow this large, cheerful, deeply mediocre commander to the gates of Storm's End and sit there for a year accomplishing absolutely nothing.

But I didn't know that yet. None of us did.

"We'll be home by the harvest!" he called as he mounted, and the column began to move, and three hundred knights and a thousand soldiers and ten thousand bushels of the Reach's finest grain rolled out through Highgarden's gates and down the Roseroad toward a war that would end a dynasty.

My mother watched until the last wagon disappeared. Then she turned, and her face was calm, and her eyes were steady, and she said, "Come, boys. There's work to do."

There was. More than any of us guessed.

The first month was almost normal.

The household adjusted to Mace's absence with the practiced efficiency of a mechanism that had been designed to function with or without its most decorative component. The steward continued stewarding. The cook continued cooking. Ser Vortimer, too old for campaign, continued putting me through my paces in the training yard with the same methodical patience he'd shown when Mace was there to watch from the balcony.

My mother ran things. She'd always run things, in that quiet, competent way of hers -- the domestic economy, the household accounts, the management of servants and supplies and the thousand daily decisions that kept a great castle functioning. With Mace gone, the pretense that these were joint decisions evaporated, and Alerie stepped into the visible role with a naturalness that told me she'd been doing the real work all along.

I watched her with new appreciation. Here was competence -- not the flashy kind that drew attention but the structural kind that held everything together. She met with the steward each morning. Reviewed accounts. Managed disputes between servants. Corresponded with the bannermen's wives, maintaining the social web that Mace couldn't be bothered with because it involved letters rather than lances.

The Hightower blood showed. Alerie's family had run Oldtown for thousands of years, and running things was in her bones the way martial pretension was in Mace's. Different inheritances. Different values.

The second month was when the squeeze began.

[HR]

It started with the grain.

Not a shortage -- not yet. A thinning. The wagons that normally arrived from the surrounding holdfasts with grain for Highgarden's stores came less frequently, and when they came, they carried less. The army had requisitioned heavily on its march south, because armies travel on their stomachs and Mace's stomach was the size of a small county. Each lord he'd passed had been expected to contribute supplies, and they had, because refusing the Lord of Highgarden on his way to war was the kind of decision that aged a man quickly.

The result was a slow, gentle, entirely predictable redistribution of the Reach's food supply from the people who grew it to the soldiers who were going to sit outside Storm's End and eat it while watching Stannis Baratheon starve behind his walls.

I understood this with a clarity that was, frankly, infuriating.

The Book had an extensive section on wartime economics. Supply chains, logistics, the way military mobilization distorted agricultural production by removing labor from the fields and redirecting output to non-productive consumption. Every bushel of grain that went to feed a soldier sitting idle outside a besieged castle was a bushel that didn't feed the farmer who'd grown it, or the farmer's family, or the communities that depended on the farmer's surplus being available in the markets.

It wasn't famine. The Reach was too wealthy, too fertile, too deeply stocked for a single campaign to empty the granaries. But it was a tightening. A constriction. The belt being drawn one notch closer to the bone, not enough to hurt but enough to feel.

The servants noticed first. They always did -- the people closest to the kitchens and the storerooms were the ones who could feel the difference between abundance and adequacy, and adequacy made them nervous in a way that abundance never did. Conversations I overheard in hallways, half-caught whispers between the cook and the cellarer, the steward's slightly more careful allocation of stores at supper.

Nobody said anything official. Nobody would. The Reach was at war, and the Reach's people would endure because that was what people did when their lords went to war: they endured, and they waited, and they tried not to think about the fact that the breadbasket of Westeros was sending its bread to feed an army that was accomplishing nothing.

The irony sat in my chest like a stone.

I found the kitchen steward on a grey afternoon in what I guessed was the fourth month of the war.

His name was Gormon, and he was a round man with a red face and hands that moved in quick, practiced gestures, like a musician playing an instrument only he could hear. He'd run Highgarden's kitchens for twelve years and knew every barrel, sack, and crock in the stores with the intimate familiarity of a man whose professional reputation depended on meals appearing on time and in sufficient quantity, regardless of what idiotic things the lord of the house was doing with the food supply.

I found him in the pantry, counting. Actually counting -- lips moving, fingers pointing, the silent arithmetic of a man who was doing the math and not loving the answer.

"Gormon."

He startled. Turned. Recalibrated downward to find me, the way adults always did, because I existed at roughly the altitude of a moderately ambitious shrub.

"My lord Willas." The [I]my lord[/I] was automatic, perfunctory. He was distracted. The counting was more important than the courtesies. "You shouldn't be in the stores, my lord."

"I wanted to ask you something."

He waited. Patience was not among Gormon's many virtues -- he had the perpetual air of a man who had seventeen things to do and was already behind on twelve of them -- but he was a servant and I was the heir and the social mathematics were simple.

"How long will the food last?"

His face changed. Not dramatically -- Gormon was too experienced for dramatic -- but there was a flicker of something behind his eyes. Surprise, maybe. Or discomfort. Or the particular unease of a man being asked a question he'd been asking himself.

"Long enough, my lord." The diplomatic answer. The one he'd give my mother, my grandmother, anyone who asked.

"I heard the steward say the grain deliveries are down by a third."

I hadn't actually heard this. I'd deduced it from the reduced frequency of wagons and the thinner portions at the servants' table, which I paid attention to because the servants' table was the canary in the coal mine of any household's food supply. But attributing deductions to overheard conversations was much less alarming than a seven-year-old conducting independent economic analysis.

Gormon's lips compressed. "Your lord father's army needs feeding, my lord. The Reach provides."

"But if it goes on for a long time..." I let the sentence trail. A child's worry. Natural enough.

"It won't." He said this with a conviction that I suspected he manufactured fresh each morning. "Lord Mace will bring the rebels to heel quickly enough."

He would not. Even without knowing the specific course of the war, I could have told Gormon that besieging a well-fortified castle was one of the slowest military operations available and that "quickly" was not a word that belonged in the same sentence as "siege." But I was seven, and seven-year-olds didn't critique military strategy.

"I read something," I said instead. The magic words. The safe words. [I]I read something in a book.[/I] "About keeping food longer. Salt-curing. For meat."

Gormon looked at me with the same expression Harmon had given me three years ago in the garden. The expression of an expert being told something by a child and trying to decide whether to be amused, annoyed, or politely interested.

"We salt our meats, my lord. Always have."

"The book said a different method. More salt, packed in layers, with -- with certain spices." I was simplifying brutally, compressing The Book's detailed chapter on food preservation into something a precocious seven-year-old might plausibly have extracted from a text. "It said the meat lasts twice as long."

He blinked. "Twice."

"And there was another thing. About vegetables. Putting them in brine -- salt water with vinegar. In sealed crocks. They last through winter."

Lacto-fermentation and vinegar pickling. The Book had an entire section on preservation techniques that predated refrigeration, methods that humanity had used for thousands of years to store food through lean seasons. Sauerkraut, kimchi, pickled vegetables, salt-cured meats, dried fruits, smoked fish -- the full arsenal of pre-industrial food preservation, most of which this world already practiced in crude, inefficient forms that wasted half the potential shelf life through poor technique.

I wasn't introducing anything new. I was refining what already existed. The distinction mattered.

Gormon rubbed his chin. I could see the calculation happening -- the same calculation that happened in Harmon's head when I'd suggested companion planting. The cost of trying versus the risk of refusing a lord's heir.

"What book?" he asked, which was the question I'd prepared for.

"One of Grandmother's. She had it sent from Oldtown. About food in the Free Cities." A lie with enough truth in it to hold weight. Olenna did send for books. The Free Cities did have sophisticated food preservation techniques. A child reading one of his grandmother's books and being excited about something he'd found was the most natural thing in the world.

"The Free Cities," Gormon repeated, as if tasting the words.

"Could we try? Just one barrel of vegetables, one curing of meat? If it doesn't work, we've only lost one barrel."

The bean experiment, scaled up. The same principle: make the cost of trying negligible, the potential benefit obvious, and let the evidence do the persuading.

Gormon studied me for a long moment. Then he shrugged, with the particular resignation of a man who had dealt with the Tyrell family long enough to know that resistance was more trouble than compliance.

"One barrel, my lord. And one curing rack. And if it goes wrong, I'll tell your lady mother it was your idea."

"Fair enough."

I practically ran from the pantry, because seven-year-olds run when they're excited, and I was excited, genuinely excited, the thrill of another experiment about to begin mixing with the grim satisfaction of doing something, however small, about a problem I could see and most people were pretending didn't exist.

[HR]

The barrel worked. The meat worked. They worked because chemistry doesn't care about wars or politics or the fact that the person suggesting the technique was 107 cm (3.5 ft) tall.

Gormon tested the brined vegetables after six weeks and found them crisp, tangy, and perfectly preserved. The salt-cured pork, packed in the layered method I'd described -- thicker salt application, with crushed juniper berries and a small amount of honey that acted as a secondary preservative through osmotic action, though I described it to Gormon as "it keeps the meat sweeter" -- lasted markedly longer than his usual preparations without developing the off-taste that signaled spoilage.

He didn't praise me. Gormon wasn't the praising type. But he made a second barrel. And then a third. And the cook started brining turnips and cabbages as a matter of routine, and the salt-curing method became "how we do it now" with the quiet ease of a change that nobody needed to announce because it obviously worked.

Small victory. Tiny, in the grand scheme of a war that was reshaping the continent. But it was mine, and it was real, and every barrel of preserved food was a barrel that wouldn't rot in the stores while the army ate its way through the Reach's surplus at Storm's End.

The war ground on. Summer turned to autumn turned to a winter that didn't come -- the seasons in this world followed their own mad logic, years-long summers and winters that lasted a generation, and we were deep in summer still, which was a mercy for the harvest if not for the men fighting in the heat.

News came in fragments. Ravens from the army, coded messages for my mother and the steward, rumors that filtered down from the bannermen's households to their servants to Highgarden's servants to the kitchens to my ears. I was seven and invisible, the small lord who read books and asked questions, and people spoke freely around me because children didn't understand war.

I understood war. I understood it better than most of the adults discussing it, because The Book had sections on military logistics, campaign economics, and the societal costs of prolonged conflict that were considerably more detailed than the average Westerosi lord's analysis of "we're winning" or "we're losing."

The Tyrell army was at Storm's End. Besieging. This much was known. What was also known, though spoken more quietly, was that the siege was accomplishing very little. Storm's End was famously impregnable -- its walls were supposedly warded against magic and definitely warded against conventional assault -- and Stannis Baratheon, the man holding it, was apparently the kind of person who would eat his own boots before surrendering.

Meanwhile, my father feasted.

This too filtered back. The Lord of Highgarden maintained his standard of living on campaign the way a fish maintained its relationship with water -- naturally, inevitably, and with no apparent awareness that alternatives existed. While Stannis rationed his garrison's food down to rats and shoe leather, Mace Tyrell hosted tourneys and banquets outside the walls, because besieging a starving castle was apparently no reason to skip dessert.

I learned this from a returning wounded soldier -- a man-at-arms who'd taken an arrow in the shoulder during a sortie and been sent home to heal. He told the story in the courtyard, to an audience of servants and stablehands, with the slightly dazed cheer of a man happy to be alive and keen to share the absurdity.

"Lord Mace says we'll starve them out by harvest!" the soldier announced. "Meanwhile, we eat better than we do at home!"

The servants laughed. It was a good story. The lord, the siege, the endless feasting. Classic Mace.

I didn't laugh. I thought about the grain stores I'd been watching dwindle, and the carefully preserved barrels in the pantry, and the farmers in the Reach's outer holdfasts who were tightening their belts so that an army could sit outside a castle and eat.

War is waste. The Book said this in its section on military economics, stated it as a principle so fundamental it barely needed supporting evidence. War is waste -- of resources, of labor, of human life, of the accumulated productive capacity that a society builds over generations of peace. Every sword is a ploughshare that wasn't made. Every soldier is a farmer who isn't farming. Every bushel of grain consumed at a siege is a bushel that didn't feed the people who grew the next harvest.

I knew this in theory. Now I was living it, watching the slow erosion of Highgarden's surplus in real time, measuring the gap between what the Reach produced and what the war consumed, and the gap was closing faster than anyone seemed to notice or care.

Not famine. Never famine -- the Reach was too rich for that. But the thinning continued. The belt tightened another notch. And the war went on.

Wounded men started appearing at Highgarden in the sixth month.

Not many. A trickle. Men injured in sorties and skirmishes, sent back from the front because the army's surgeons -- such as they were -- had done what they could and the rest was time and luck. They arrived in wagons, on horseback, sometimes on foot, bandaged and stitched and varying degrees of damaged.

My mother handled it with Hightower grace. She opened rooms in the lower keep, arranged for bedding and care, conscripted servants as nurses. The maester attended to the worst cases. The rest healed or didn't, according to the medieval calculus of wound treatment that was roughly equal parts prayer, poultice, and probability.

I was kept away. Deliberately, firmly, with the gentle insistence of adults who believed that a seven-year-old shouldn't see what arrows and swords did to human bodies. They were right, in a sense -- the seven-year-old parts of me had no need for those images. But the other parts, the parts that lived behind the carefully constructed facade of childhood, understood what was happening in those rooms with a clinical clarity that would have frightened the adults if they'd known about it.

Infection. That was the killer. Not the wounds themselves -- a human body could survive remarkable trauma if you kept it clean and fed and let the immune system do its work. But in a world without antiseptic, without germ theory, without the basic understanding that the thing killing wounded men wasn't bad air or divine punishment but microscopic organisms colonizing damaged tissue, the mortality rate from even minor wounds was appalling.

I couldn't fix this. Not yet. Not at seven, not without a way to explain how I knew what I knew, not without overturning a medical paradigm that had been in place for centuries. The germ theory of disease was the single most revolutionary idea in The Book's medical section, and it was also the one most likely to get me branded a lunatic, a heretic, or both.

But I could do one small thing.

"The bandages should be boiled," I told the laundress who handled the linens for the wounded men's rooms. Casually. Almost off-handedly, the way a child repeats something he's heard without fully understanding it. "In very hot water. Before they're used. My grandmother's maester told me it helps wounds heal."

Olenna's invisible maester. My favorite fictional authority figure, versatile enough to have opinions on food preservation, wound care, and agricultural technique.

The laundress gave me a look that combined indulgence with scepticism in roughly equal measure. But she boiled the bandages, because it cost nothing and the lord's heir had asked nicely, and whether it made any measurable difference in that small sample of wounded men I honestly don't know.

But it was something. A pebble tossed into a pond, barely visible. The ripples wouldn't matter for years. But the pebble was there.

Winter held off. The war didn't.

I turned eight somewhere in the middle of it, though the celebration was muted -- a small gathering, my mother and grandmother and Garlan and the household, cakes from a kitchen working with slightly less sugar than the year before. Garlan gave me a wooden horse he'd carved himself, with five legs and no discernible head. I loved it. I still have it.

Grandmother gave me a book. Naturally. This one was a Qohorik treatise on metalworking that must have cost a small fortune and that she presented with a look that said, plain as speech: [I]I know what you are. I don't know how. I don't care. Here.[/I]

I thanked her. Our eyes met over the table, and something passed between us that I couldn't name and didn't try to. Understanding, maybe. Or alliance. Or the quiet, fierce, unsentimental version of love that Olenna Tyrell specialized in, the kind that expressed itself not in embraces or endearments but in resources, protection, and the strategic application of wealth in service of someone else's potential.

She was shielding me. I'd understood this for some time -- the books appearing in the library, the parchment and quills that materialized without explanation, the subtle redirections of attention away from my stranger habits and toward more palatable explanations. Grandmother was building a wall between me and the world, and she was doing it so skilfully that the world didn't know the wall existed.

I couldn't tell her what I was. Not yet, maybe not ever. But I could show her that her investment wasn't wasted.

The war taught me things that peacetime couldn't. It taught me that knowledge without application was academic. That theory mattered less than a single barrel of preserved cabbage when the stores were running thin. That the people who suffered most from the decisions of lords were the people who'd had no say in making them -- the servants, the smallfolk, the farmers whose grain went to feed soldiers and whose labor went to a harvest that was thinner than it should have been because half the able-bodied men were standing outside Storm's End watching Mace Tyrell eat.

It taught me that wars are won by logistics and lost by everyone.

And it taught me patience. Not the peaceful kind -- the hard kind, the kind that sits in your chest like a clenched fist, the patience of knowing exactly what needs to change and being too small, too young, too powerless to change it. Not yet. The most hateful two words in any language.

Not yet.

But the barrels were full and the food would last and the wounded men's bandages were clean, and somewhere in the future -- a future that felt impossibly distant and impossibly close at the same time -- I was going to build something that made this kind of waste harder to inflict.

That was the promise I made to myself on my eighth birthday, sitting at a table with slightly less sugar than the year before, holding a five-legged wooden horse that my brother had carved with the clumsy love of a five-year-old.

The war would end. They always did. And when it did, I'd be ready.
