[H2]Chapter 15 — "Oldtown"[/H2]

[CENTER]286 AC — Oldtown[/CENTER]

The first thing I noticed about Oldtown was the smell.

Sewage, fish market, tanneries clinging to the eastern bank of the Honeywine. But underneath all that: ink. Old parchment. Stone baking in the sun. The accumulated residue of ten thousand years of people thinking hard about things and writing them down.

"Stop gaping," Mother said, adjusting my collar with the efficiency of a woman who'd spent nine years managing a child who dressed like he'd been caught in a windstorm. "You're a Tyrell. Tyrells don't gape."

"Grandmother gapes," I said. "At Father. Regularly."

"That's horror. Entirely different."

She was smiling, though. Alerie Hightower, Alerie Tyrell now, though the Hightower never quite left a person. She'd been looking forward to this trip for weeks. Coming home had changed something in her. She walked differently here. Straighter. She knew these streets the way her fingers knew needlework: without looking, and with a confidence she never quite managed in Highgarden's halls, where Grandmother's presence compressed everyone else into smaller versions of themselves.

The Hightower dominated everything. It rose from Battle Isle at the mouth of the Honeywine, over 244 m (800 ft) from base to beacon. I'd read the dimensions. I'd thought I understood what that meant. Standing beneath it, neck craned until the muscles burned, I discovered there was a gap between reading a number and standing in its shadow. The base was black stone, fused and seamless, no mortar, no chisel marks. Whatever had built it hadn't been human. Above that, pale stone tiers rose in precise diminishing proportions. Whoever had built the upper sections understood load distribution, tapering, wind resistance. They'd made something that should have toppled centuries ago.

"You're doing it again," Mother said.

"Doing what?"

"That look. The one where your mouth hangs open and your eyes go narrow. You get it when you're counting something nobody asked you to count."

I closed my mouth. "I was admiring the architecture."

"You were taking it apart in your head. I'm your mother, Willas. I know the difference." She took my hand, a gesture she reserved for public settings where a nine-year-old lordling needed to look like a nine-year-old and not whatever he actually was. "Come. Your uncle Baelor is expecting us."

We entered through the Thieves' Gate, wide and well-guarded, positioned to give arriving visitors a view of the Starry Sept's black marble domes. The Sept was genuinely beautiful. Each of its seven walls caught the sun at different hours, and the black marble absorbed the light and gave it back changed, richer somehow. I wanted to study the stone. I filed the thought and kept walking.

[HR]

Baelor Hightower earned his epithet within ten seconds of meeting us. He came into the receiving hall smiling, and it was the kind of smile that made you smile back before you'd decided to. Warm and easy and completely genuine, or at least so well-practiced that it didn't matter which.

"Alerie!" He lifted Mother off her feet. She laughed and swatted his shoulder and looked younger than I'd ever seen her. "And this must be Willas. Gods, he's got your face. Come here, let me look at you."

He crouched to my level. I submitted to being looked at. Up close, he was handsome like good marble: proportioned, smooth, very aware of the impression it made. Brown hair, bright eyes, a jaw that belonged on a tournament shield. "Your mother tells me you read too much."

"Reading doesn't have a maximum."

"It does. I've watched maesters demonstrate it." He straightened and offered Mother his arm. "Father sends his regards from upstairs. He won't be joining us."

"Still?" Something shifted in Mother's face. Old concern, older exasperation.

"Still. Malora's with him. They're reading." Baelor said "reading" the way most people said "drinking": affection and worry layered together so tightly you couldn't separate them.

Lord Leyton Hightower had not descended from the upper levels of his own tower in years. Nobody said "the upper levels" with a straight face if they knew what was up there: ancient texts, occult volumes, works the Citadel's own restricted archives would have balked at. The household ran around his absence smoothly, by long practice. Baelor handled Oldtown's governance, its port administration, its diplomacy, its thousand daily decisions. Voice of Oldtown, in practice if not yet in title.

The Hightower's interior matched its exterior: old, impressive, and layered. The lower halls were decorated in the martial style of three centuries ago, banners from wars nobody remembered, armor that hadn't been worn since the Conquest. But the upper levels, the ones I was allowed to see, were different. Books everywhere. Tapestries depicting scholars, not warriors. A library that wasn't the Citadel's but still larger than Highgarden's entire collection. The Hightowers hadn't built the tallest tower in Westeros because they wanted to look down on people. They'd built it because they wanted to be closer to the stars.

Dinner was a production. Uncles, cousins, retainers. I committed every name to memory. Uncle Gunthor, who managed the port garrison and spoke in clipped military sentences that would have gotten along well with Ser Vortimer. A collection of cousins ranging from a girl of three who hid under the table to a young man of twenty who was studying at the Citadel and kept glancing at me like I was a specimen he hadn't classified yet.

Baelor sat me beside him. This was deliberate. He spent the first course asking me about Highgarden in the easy, interested way of a man who already knew the answers and wanted to see how I'd frame them. By the second course, I realized he was assessing me the same way I assessed a mechanism: gently, precisely, looking for where the parts fit and where they didn't.

"Your mother says you've been working with the steward on new planting methods," he said, carving a slice of duck with surgical precision. "How's that going?"

"Early results are promising. The rotation system is showing measurable improvement over the traditional three-field approach."

"Measurable how?"

"First cycle just finished. Wheat matched the control fields, which sounds like nothing, but the rotation also produced a full turnip crop on land that would have been fallow. And the soil's already showing improvement for the next round." I paused. "I'm tracking everything. Written records, controlled comparisons."

He chewed his duck thoughtfully. "And the maesters? What do they make of it?"

"Maester Lomys has been observing. He's keeping his own records alongside mine."

"Smart. A lord's word is political. A maester's word is academic. You'll want both when you start showing results to people outside Highgarden." He refilled my cup with water and his own with wine. "Whose idea was it to involve him?"

"Mine." Technically true. Grandmother had made sure Lomys was available, but the invitation had been my words. "He was skeptical at first. Less so now."

"Things that seem obvious to you at nine don't seem obvious to most people at forty. Remember that. It's going to cause you problems." He said it lightly. A warning dressed as conversation.

Between the third and fourth courses, a door at the far end of the hall opened. A woman walked in, collected a plate from the side table without speaking to anyone, and turned to leave.

Malora Hightower. The Mad Maid.

She was near forty, tall and thin, with dark hair that had been pinned up in a hurry and was slowly abandoning the effort. Her dress was expensive and slightly crooked, as if she'd put it on while thinking about something else. She moved through the hall like a person passing through a room she'd forgotten was occupied.

Then her eyes found me, and she stopped.

"You're the Tyrell boy," she said. Her voice was light and a little distant, as if part of her attention was still upstairs with whatever she'd been reading. "Alerie's eldest."

"Yes, my lady."

She tilted her head. Studied me. There was no social calculation in it, no warmth or hostility, just a frank and slightly unsettling curiosity, the way a person might examine an unusual moth that had landed on their sleeve.

"You have very still hands for a child," she said.

I looked down at my hands. They were resting on the table, motionless. Most children fidgeted. I'd trained the fidgeting out of myself years ago because steady hands were useful for drawing and measuring, but I hadn't thought anyone would notice.

"Do you know why the Hightower is so tall?" she asked.

The question had no connection to what came before. The adults nearby were politely pretending this conversation wasn't happening.

"Engineering," I said. "The base is fused stone. The upper tiers taper to reduce—"

"No." She said it gently, like correcting a child who'd given a reasonable answer to the wrong question. "It's tall because the people who built the base wanted to see something that was very far away. The stone remembers what it was built for, even if we've forgotten." She picked up her plate. "You should visit the top before you leave. The view changes depending on who's looking."

She walked out. The door closed behind her.

Baelor caught my expression. "Don't try to make sense of Malora," he said, not unkindly. "She's been like that since she was your age. Half of what she says sounds like nonsense and the other half keeps you up at night."

"Which half was that?"

"I've never been able to tell." He reached for his wine.

Later, while the younger children chased each other through the corridors and the adults settled into deep chairs and deeper wine, I found Mother.

"What does Aunt Malora study?" I asked.

"Old things. Books most people have forgotten exist. Your grandfather indulges her." Mother paused, choosing her words carefully. "She's not mad, whatever the servants call her. She's focused. On subjects most people find uncomfortable."

"What subjects?"

"The kind the Citadel keeps in its restricted archives. And that's all I'll say about it tonight." She gave me a look that was part amusement, part warning. "And if she invites you to visit the top of the tower, ask me first."

The next morning, a novice arrived to escort me to the Citadel. Mother had arranged it through Baelor, who'd arranged it through someone who'd arranged it through someone. The chain of favors was invisible and perfectly efficient. A tour, Mother called it. Educational. Her son was so interested in learning.

The novice's name was Perestan. Sixteen, ink-stained fingers, the slightly dazed look of someone who'd been reading all night. He was working toward his copper link: history.

"The Citadel is the largest repository of knowledge in the known world," he began, with the practiced cadence of a speech he'd given too many times. "Founded during the—"

"Reign of the Hightowers, before the Doom of Valyria," I said. "The order predates the fall of the Freehold by centuries. The original buildings were a gift from the Hightower family."

Perestan blinked. "You're nine."

"I read."

He showed me the grounds with the wariness of a man guiding a cat through a fish market. The Seneschal's Court, administrative hub. The Ravenry: hundreds of ravens, each trained to fly to a specific castle, the communication backbone of an entire continent. I could have watched them for an hour: their calls to each other, the intricate system of identification tags and training schedules that made the whole network function. A living, breathing postal service with feathers.

Then the scribe's workshops. Men hunched over desks, copying books by hand. One page at a time. Months of labor per volume.

I watched a scribe reproduce a text on botany, his hand moving with the patient steadiness of long practice, and thought about moveable type. About printing presses. The infrastructure wasn't ready: I needed better paper, better ink, precision metalwork for the type. But the need was written in every slow stroke of that pen. Thousands of texts in single copies, deteriorating in vaults, one fire away from being lost forever.

"Can I see the libraries?" I asked, already knowing the answer.

"Restricted to maesters and acolytes." Perestan sounded genuinely sorry. "Even I can only access the lower collections. The restricted archives need Archmaester approval."

"What's in the restricted archives?"

He glanced around, then lowered his voice. "Higher mysteries. Advanced metallurgy. Histories that contradict the official accounts. Texts on the Long Night. Dragonlore." A nervous pause. "Things the Archmaesters don't discuss openly."

[HR]

I found what I'd come looking for in a courtyard near the Quill and Tankard. An older man in maester's robes was holding forth to a small crowd of novices, a wine cup in one hand, the other jabbing at the air.

"Can't explain plague ships," he was saying. "A ship arrives in port. Crew's healthy. They unload cargo. Within a week the dockworkers are dying. Same air, different outcomes. If bad air caused disease, the crew would be dead first. They breathe it longer. But they're fine."

Archmaester Ryam. Perhaps fifty, copper link for history and silver for medicine on his chain. I recognized the argument before I recognized the man.

"It's the cargo," I said.

Every head turned. Nine-year-old boy in lordling's clothes, interrupting an Archmaester's lecture.

Ryam didn't look offended. He looked interested. "Explain."

"Something in the cargo, or on it. The disease is carried by something physical. On surfaces, in water, on hands. The crew loads and seals. The dockworkers open, unpack, sort. Closer contact, longer contact, hands touching their faces and food."

Silence.

"The boy has read Archmaester Quillion," Ryam said slowly.

I had never heard of Archmaester Quillion. I made a mental note to learn everything about him. "I've read widely."

"Who are you?"

"Willas Tyrell. My mother is Alerie Hightower."

I watched him recalculate. A curious lordling was a novelty. A Tyrell-Hightower lordling who could discuss disease theory at nine was something else entirely.

"Quillion wrote about contact as a disease vector two hundred years ago," Ryam said. "He was laughed at. His work sits in the restricted archives. The miasma theory is more convenient. Simpler. Easier to explain to lords who don't want to pay for clean water."

"That's political, not scientific."

His smile was crooked and genuine. "Welcome to the Citadel, young lord."

We talked for an hour. He ordered wine for himself and watered cider for me, waved away Perestan's nervous hovering, and gave me the most stimulating conversation I'd had since arriving in this world. He'd been arguing for empirical methods, test, observe, record, repeat, for twenty years. The Citadel had responded by granting him tenure and pretending he didn't exist.

"They can't dismiss me. Archmaester is for life. But they can refuse to teach my methods and wait for me to die." He drank. "Ordinary maesters don't have that protection. A fellow named Qyburn found that out. Brilliant with a knife. Wanted to understand how the body actually worked, not how the textbooks said it worked. They stripped his chain and threw him out."

"For being right?"

"For cutting people open to find out." Ryam's voice was flat. "There's a line between empiricism and butchery, and Qyburn couldn't see it. Or didn't care to. Last I heard he'd fallen in with some sellsword company in the Riverlands." He set down his cup. "The Citadel uses him as a warning. Every novice who asks too many questions gets told the story of the maester who went too far. It's effective. It's also convenient, because it lets them pretend that anyone who challenges the old methods is one step away from vivisection."

"What if someone proved your methods worked? In practice. Results that couldn't be argued away."

"Such as?"

"Handwashing. Boiling drinking water. Cleaning wounds with diluted spirits instead of poultices. Track the outcomes carefully over months."

He stared at me for a long time. "You've tested this."

"Minor cuts and scrapes. Household staff who volunteered. Wounds treated with boiled water and diluted spirits heal cleaner than those treated with poultices. Consistently, over a year of tracking."

He leaned back and studied me, looking for where the weight rested, where the stress points were.

"Write to me," he said. "Your results. I can't get you library access, but I can correspond, and I can tell you what to read. I'll send you a copy of Quillion's work. I made several when I realized the original was rotting and nobody cared."

"Restricted archives. Where knowledge goes to die."

"You understand perfectly." He raised his cup. "To empiricism, young lord."

Mother was waiting in the Hightower's courtyard. She fell into step beside me, her stride unhurried, her expression pleasant and completely unreadable.

"You made an impression," she said.

"How do you already—"

"Baelor heard. An Archmaester spent an hour drinking with a nine-year-old Tyrell who discussed disease transmission theory. You don't think that reached my brother before you finished your cider?"

I hadn't considered that. The realization landed like a cold stone in my stomach.

"Willas." She stopped walking. Her voice was still warm, but there was something underneath it that reminded me of Grandmother when she meant something completely. "You are clever. Everyone who spends five minutes with you knows it. That is useful, but it is also visible. Visible things get watched."

"I was just talking—"

"You were demonstrating knowledge a nine-year-old shouldn't have, in a courtyard full of novices who will gossip about it tomorrow, in a city where the Citadel's entire purpose is to be the place that knows things." She let that settle. "The Hightowers and the Citadel have lived beside each other for thousands of years. We built their home. We protect their walls. And they watch us for it, always, because the family that gave them their power could also take it away, and maesters never forget that."

"You're saying I was careless."

"I'm saying that a lord's son who knows too much makes people ask where the knowledge comes from. And the answer to that question, for you, is not one you want anyone examining." She resumed walking. "Correspond with Ryam. But let Baelor know about the correspondence. If the Citadel sends inquiries, the Hightowers need to have answers ready."

This was the education I'd been missing. The Book had physics, chemistry, metallurgy, agriculture. It didn't have this: how suspicion traveled, how institutions protected their territory, how a careless afternoon could generate consequences that rippled for years.

"Thank you," I said.

She squeezed my hand. "Your grandmother handles Highgarden's politics. Your father handles the ones he understands. But Oldtown requires a lighter touch than either of them has. It needs someone who can speak the city's language."

"And you think that's me."

"I think it's us. You have the mind for it. I have the family. Between the two of us, we might manage without getting you denounced as a heretic before your twelfth nameday." She said it with a straight face, but her eyes were laughing.

On the third morning, she took me to the harbor. "Every heir needs to understand trade," she said, leading me through the wharf district. She moved through the crowds as comfortably as through the Hightower's own halls. "Your father understands tournaments. Your grandmother understands people. You understand mechanisms. None of it matters if you don't understand money."

The harbor was enormous. Hundreds of ships packed the wharves. Reach cogs, Dornish galleys, fat-bellied traders from Myr and Tyrosh and Volantis. The air smelled of tar, salt fish, and the sharp sweetness of Tyroshi dye.

"That ship," she said, pointing to a wide-beamed merchant vessel flying the orange pennant of a Myrish trading house. "What do you notice?"

I looked at the hull design, the waterline draft, the leverage ratio on the cargo crane—

"Don't look at the wood. Look at the business."

I looked again. "It's sitting high. Holds aren't full. But the crew hasn't disembarked, so they're not done here. They're buying, not selling."

"And?"

"Myrish lens glass comes in. Reach goods go out. Wine, probably. Maybe wool."

"The Hightowers take a tariff on everything that moves through this port. Three coppers on the silver for foreign hulls, two for Westerosi. Your grandfather earns more from this harbor in a month than most minor lords see in a year." She looked at me sidelong. "When you think about Oldtown and what it could become, remember that. The city already works. It already produces wealth. Any changes you imagine making need to work with that, not against it."

She hadn't said "I know you're planning something." She'd said something more precise: "I know how you think, and here's the piece you haven't accounted for." She'd been doing this for years, I realized. Placing facts in front of me quietly, trusting me to pick them up.

She walked me through the trade quarter. Which houses had permanent factors in Oldtown. Which goods moved fastest. Where the bottlenecks choked the port infrastructure. Practical knowledge, learned young, refreshed by years of paying attention. The same thing I had with crop rotation data, except hers was worth actual money.

I'd underestimated my mother. The realization was uncomfortable and completely deserved.

On the last evening, I stood on the balcony of the guest quarters and watched the city at sunset. Amber and gold, all of it, the rooftops and the water and the Hightower's beacon lighting above us, steady and ancient.

Mother stood beside me. We were quiet for a while.

"I miss it," she said. "Every time I come back, I miss it more when I leave."

"Why did you leave?"

"Because the match was good for both houses and that's how it works." She said it without bitterness. "I love Highgarden. I love your father. But this is where I learned to think. I'd like you to have that too."

"Can we come back?"

"I spoke to Baelor. Twice a year, at least." She gave me the look she wore when she'd been three steps ahead and was waiting for me to arrive. "Your grandmother manages the Reach's politics. Your father manages his own reputation. But this city, the trade, the scholars, the connections, it needs a Tyrell who understands it. Or rather, a Tyrell and a Hightower, working together."

Below us, the city settled into evening. Lanterns coming on in windows. Ships rocking at anchor. Somewhere in the Citadel's courtyards, Archmaester Ryam was probably arguing with someone about empirical method. Somewhere above us in this tower, Aunt Malora was reading books that most people pretended didn't exist.

I thought about what I'd gained in three days. An academic ally who'd send me restricted texts. A family network that could smooth my path through Oldtown's institutions. A mother who understood things I'd been too focused to notice. And a list of what I didn't know that was, for the first time, longer than the list of what I did.

That last part felt like progress.

"Thank you for bringing me," I said.

Mother put her arm around my shoulders. It was the kind of gesture she usually reserved for when I was tired or sick, and I was neither, but I didn't pull away. For a moment I let myself be nine years old, standing with my mother on a balcony, watching a beautiful city go to sleep.

The plans could wait until morning.
