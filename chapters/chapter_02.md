[H2]Chapter 2 — Small Hands[/H2]

The indignity of being carried to the privy by a servant was, I decided, going to be the thing that broke me.

I could handle the stone walls. I could handle the candles and the bone-deep wrongness of inhabiting a body whose proportions belonged to a creature approximately the size of a border collie. I could even handle the smell, and there was [I]so much smell[/I], layers of it, an olfactory archaeology of rushes and woodsmoke and bodies and food and animals and the faint, ever-present tang of human waste that said more about medieval sanitation than any chapter of The Book.

But the privy. Specifically, being hoisted onto it by a woman named Dalla who had the arms of a longshoreman and the casual efficiency of someone who had been managing small children's bodily functions for decades.

"There you are, little lord."

[I]There I was.[/I] I gripped the edges of the seat, my fingers too short to reach all the way around, and contemplated the series of choices, cosmic or otherwise, that had led to this moment. Whatever afterlife I'd been expecting, this wasn't it. Then again, I couldn't remember what I'd been expecting, so perhaps this was exactly right. A pre-industrial toilet and a servant who called me [I]little lord[/I]. The universe has a sense of humor. Noted.

It was my third day of proper consciousness. The fever had left me weak as water, and The Book's entry on post-febrile recovery in young children was not encouraging. [I]Expect fatigue, appetite loss, and muscle weakness lasting one to three weeks. Ensure adequate hydration and caloric intake. Watch for secondary infection.[/I] Helpful, that. Less helpful when you were the patient and your primary medical concern was whether you could hold a cup without spilling it.

I could not, as it turned out, hold a cup without spilling it. My hands were the problem. They were small, yes, everything was small, and I was getting used to that, or at least I was getting used to the constant background buzz of wrongness that came with being 90 cm (3 ft) tall. But worse than the size was the [I]imprecision[/I]. The fine motor control I instinctively expected wasn't there. I'd reach for something and overshoot, or undershoot, or close my fingers too early, or not enough. The disconnect between intention and execution was maddening.

On the morning of that third day, I asked Alerie for a quill.

She was delighted. Her Willas, the original, had been starting to show interest in letters before the fever. The maester had been working with him. So a quill and ink and a scrap of parchment appeared, and Alerie settled me at a table that came up to my chin and watched with bright, expectant eyes.

I picked up the quill. It was enormous in my hand. I positioned it the way I knew it should be held, three-point grip, nib angled at forty-five degrees, and touched it to the parchment.

What I intended to write was a simple letter A.

What appeared on the parchment looked like a spider had fallen into an inkwell and then suffered a seizure.

Alerie laughed. Kindly, but she laughed. "You'll learn, sweeting. It takes practice."

She had no idea. I spent the rest of that morning practicing lines and circles rather than letters, because I didn't dare write anything legible yet. The most basic exercises in fine motor control, disguised as a child's aimless scribbling. By the end of it my hand was aching and I'd produced nothing that would alarm anyone, which was exactly the point.

The thing about being a grown mind in a child's body is that everyone expects you to be bad at things. The expectations are so low that you could trip over them. No one watches a four-year-old scribble and thinks [I]he's systematically training his hand-eye coordination using progressive difficulty exercises[/I]. They think [I]oh, how sweet, he's drawing[/I]. I intended to exploit this for all it was worth.

[HR]

Mace Tyrell, Lord of Highgarden, Warden of the South, Defender of the Marches, and High Marshal of the Reach, was a very large man.

That was the first thing. He filled doorways. He filled rooms. He filled the world with his voice and his presence and his relentless, booming good cheer. When he came to see me on the fourth day, he entered the sickroom like a siege engine entering a garden party, with tremendous energy and absolutely no sense of proportion.

"The boy! There's my boy!" He crossed the room in three strides, scooped me off the bed, and held me at arm's length like a man inspecting a prize melon. "Color's coming back. Good. Good! Maester said it was a near thing, but I told him, I said, [I]Lomys, that boy's a Tyrell[/I]. We don't die in bed. Isn't that right?"

He shook me gently, the way you'd shake a bottle to see if there was anything left in it. I blinked at him.

"He's still weak, my lord." Alerie, from somewhere behind the human wall that was my father. Her voice had the patient, practiced tone of someone who had been managing Mace Tyrell for years. "The maester says he needs rest."

"Rest! Rest, she says." Mace set me down on the bed with surprising gentleness, then dropped into the chair that Alerie had been occupying. The chair groaned. "When I was his age, I was already riding. Well, being held on a horse, but the principle's the same. A Tyrell needs fresh air, not a dark room. We'll have him out in the yard in no time. Get a wooden sword in his hand. Start him young."

He beamed at me. It was the beam of a man who had already composed the ballad of his son's future and was merely waiting for reality to catch up.

I looked at Mace Tyrell, my father, and tried to reconcile the man in front of me with what I knew. Which was mostly what the original Willas had known, a child's fragmentary impressions: loud, warm, always moving, always talking, smelled of leather and wine. The broader assessment took only a moment. Mace was not a stupid man, exactly. He was something more complicated, a man of moderate intelligence and tremendous self-regard who had inherited one of the most powerful positions in the realm and mistaken the accident of birth for personal merit.

He loved his children. That much was obvious from the way he'd held me, the way his eyes had gone bright and wet for just a moment before the bluster took over. He loved us the way he loved everything — loudly, publicly, without much reflection. I could work with that.

"Papa." The word came out small and hoarse, and I saw it hit him like an arrow. His whole face changed, the performance falling away for just a second, and what was underneath was raw, unguarded relief.

"That's my boy." Quieter now. He reached out and ruffled my hair with a hand the size of a dinner plate. "That's my boy."

Alerie appeared at his elbow, and I watched them exchange a look over my head, the complicated wordless communication of parents who have just come through something terrible together. Alerie's hand found Mace's shoulder. He covered it with his own.

They were good people. They weren't brilliant or scheming, weren't playing the game of thrones or whatever the local equivalent was. Just... people. Trying to raise their children and keep their house in order and not die of anything medieval. I could do a lot worse for parents.

[HR]

Baby Garlan was a problem I hadn't anticipated.

He was roughly two years old, round and sturdy and operating at the developmental level where everything was either food, a toy, or something to hit with a toy. His primary mode of communication was pointing and making sounds of varying urgency, and his primary hobby was following me around with the dogged determination of a very small, very drunk private investigator.

The first time they brought him to see me, on the fifth day when I could sit up without the room spinning, he stood in the doorway for a long moment, studying me with an expression of profound suspicion. Then he waddled across the room, grabbed my hand, and placed it firmly on his head.

I looked at Alerie.

"He missed you," she said, as if this explained the head thing. Maybe it did. Maybe before the fever, the original Willas had been in the habit of patting his brother's head. The body remembered things the mind didn't. I'd noticed this already, the way my hand knew to grip a spoon even when my brain was still arguing with my fingers about angles.

I patted Garlan's head. He made a sound of satisfaction and sat down next to me on the bed, leaning his warm, solid weight against my side.

Something unexpected happened then. Something that The Book had nothing to say about, because The Book dealt in science and engineering, not in the complicated chemistry of a borrowed heart. I looked down at this small person, this child who was my brother by no choice of either of ours, and I felt the first genuine emotion that wasn't grief or confusion or grim pragmatic resolve.

Protectiveness. It was fierce and immediate and completely irrational. He was a toddler. I was a four-year-old with the structural integrity of wet paper. I couldn't protect him from a stiff breeze. But the feeling was there, rooted deep, and I knew with a certainty that had nothing to do with The Book and everything to do with whatever I was becoming that I would keep this child safe.

"Wih-wah," Garlan said, which was apparently his version of my name. He patted my knee with a fat hand. "Wih-wah up."

"I can't pick you up," I told him. "I am unfortunately the size of a fire hydrant and have the upper body strength of a consumptive kitten."

He stared at me blankly. Right. Audience. I needed to remember my audience.

"Too tired," I amended. "Later."

This was acceptable. He leaned against me more heavily and began chewing on the edge of my blanket. I let him. We sat together like that while the afternoon light came through the window, which had no glass, just a wooden shutter thrown open to let in the air, and I listened to the sounds of Highgarden and tried to build a map of my world from what I could hear.

Birds. Wind. The distant clang of a hammer on metal, a smithy somewhere. Voices, too far away to parse. The creak of a cart. Dogs barking. And underneath it all, a quality of silence that I was beginning to understand would take a long time to get used to. The silence of a world without engines.

[HR]

The maester, Lomys, came to examine me every morning.

He was a careful man. Thorough in the way of someone trained to a system, every step performed in order, every observation noted. He checked my eyes, my throat, my pulse. He had me track his finger, squeeze his hand, touch my nose. He asked me questions, simple things, a child's questions. [I]What is your name? How old are you? What is your mother's name?[/I]

I answered correctly, because the answers lived in the body's memory and surfaced without effort. Willas. Four. Alerie. The easy ones.

Then he asked me what color the banner was. There was a Tyrell banner hanging on the wall opposite the bed, green and gold. I said "green and gold," and he nodded, and then he paused.

"The boy is... sharper than before the fever," he told Alerie on the sixth day. I was supposed to be sleeping. I was not sleeping. The walls were thick but the door was cracked open and sound carried in stone corridors. "More focused. More present."

"Is that not a good thing?" Alerie's voice, carefully neutral.

"It can be. Fever sometimes affects the brain. Usually for the worse. Occasionally..." He trailed off. I could practically hear him selecting his words. "Some children, after a severe illness, show a change in temperament. More serious. More attentive. As if the fever burned away some childish fog. It's documented, though rare."

I exhaled quietly. This was the story, then. The explanation that would cover me, at least for a while. The fever changed him. Made him sharper, more focused, more [I]present[/I]. A medical curiosity, not a possession, not a changeling, nothing that would get me thrown into a sept for exorcism. A child who woke up from a near-death experience slightly different. I could work with that too.

The key was calibration. Too sudden a change and people would really notice, the kind of noticing that led to questions I couldn't answer. Too slow and I'd waste years pretending to be something I wasn't, years I might not have if the world was as dangerous as my instincts suggested.

So I aimed for [I]precocious[/I]. Just precocious. A bright child made brighter by his brush with death. Interested in books, in how things worked, in asking questions. Children asked questions constantly anyway; mine would just be slightly better aimed.

I began with small things. Asking Lomys what was in the poultice he put on my chest. Asking Alerie why the water from the well tasted different from the water from the rain barrel. Asking Dalla why the bread was dark in the morning and white at supper. Kid questions, but with just enough edge that the adults would file them under [I]clever[/I] rather than [I]impossible[/I].

It was, I reflected, the most exhausting performance of my life. Both lives. However you counted.

My hands were getting better, though. Slowly. I practiced every moment I was alone, gripping things, releasing things, training the neural pathways that a four-year-old's brain hadn't finished building. The Book had a section on childhood motor development that I found both useful and deeply depressing. [I]Fine motor control adequate for basic writing typically develops between ages five and seven.[/I] I had at least a year before I could hold a quill properly, and that was with aggressive practice.

A year. I looked at my hands, small and pudgy and useless and [I]mine[/I], and I thought about everything I knew and everything I couldn't do and the vast, impassable distance between the two.

Then I picked up a wooden block from the toy chest by my bed, and I practiced gripping it. Again. And again. And again.

You build a cathedral one stone at a time. The Book said so.

It neglected to mention that the stonemason would be four years old and working with fingers like sausages.
