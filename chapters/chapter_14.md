[H2]Chapter 14 — Interlude: Olenna — "The Rose's Roots"[/H2]

[CENTER][B]-- Olenna --[/B][/CENTER]

Olenna Tyrell sat in her solar with a glass of something extraordinary and reflected on the fact that her grandson was
going to be the death of her. Not literally. Literally, she intended to outlive everyone who annoyed her, which at
current rates meant she'd be the last woman standing in the Seven Kingdoms. But Willas, her impossible, brilliant,
exhausting firstborn grandchild, was testing the limits of even her considerable stamina.

She sipped the spirit. Triple-distilled, infused with lavender from Highgarden's own gardens. The boy had given it to
her three days ago with that careful expression he wore when he was trying not to seem proud. He'd failed, of course. He
was nine, and nine-year-olds were terrible at hiding pride, even nine-year-olds who were better at hiding everything
else than anyone she'd ever met.

The lavender spirit was smooth. Warm. Dangerously easy to drink for something that could strip varnish.

She set the glass on the table next to her private ledger and let herself think backwards, because understanding where
you were required knowing how you'd gotten there, and the journey from [I]my grandson is unusually bright[/I] to [I]my
grandson is rebuilding the Reach's economy from scratch[/I] had been a strange one.

[HR]

It had started with charcoal, of all things. Charcoal.

When the servants had reported that young lord Willas was building a brick dome behind his workshop, Olenna had sent a
girl named Mina to watch. Not spy, exactly. Watch. There was a difference, in principle, if not in practice.

Mina had reported back dutifully. The young lord was burning wood inside a sealed brick structure. There was smoke,
sometimes more, sometimes less. He stood next to it for hours, adjusting metal plates. The gardener, Harmon, helped him
load and unload it. The results, as far as Mina could tell, were lumps of black stuff that looked exactly like the
charcoal everyone else used, except that the young lord treated them like they were made of gold.

Olenna had sent for Joram next. The mason who'd built the dome. She'd caught him in the kitchens, eating a meat pie, and
sat him down with the quiet authority that made even a man with a mouthful of pie swallow quickly and pay attention.

"Tell me about the kiln," she said.

Joram had told her. A simple structure, he said. Brick dome, air holes, a chimney. The young lord had provided sketches,
good ones for a boy his age, and the design was sound. Nothing Joram hadn't built before, in principle. "What's
different is the control," Joram had said, wiping crumbs from his chin. "The plates on the air holes. He opens and
closes them all through the burn. Watches the smoke like a hawk watches a rabbit."

"And the charcoal it produces?"

"I'm a mason, my lady. I know bricks. But Harmon says it burns hotter. And the smith, Tobho, sent word back through
Harmon that it was the best fuel he'd worked with in twenty years." Joram had paused. "The boy thinks nobody knows about
this except him and Harmon and the two lads who helped load it."

Olenna had allowed herself a small, private smile. The boy thought he was being discreet. He was eight. His idea of
discreet was not telling his father, which was admittedly the most important form of discretion at Highgarden, since
Mace would have turned it into a feast and a proclamation within the hour. But servants talked. They always talked. The
trick was not to stop them talking, which was impossible, but to make sure what they said was boring.

She'd handled it. A word to the kitchen steward: the young lord's hobby was to be indulged, not discussed. A quiet
instruction to Harmon, delivered through a trusted maid: keep the charcoal work going, mention it to no one outside the
workshop, and if anyone asked, it was just the boy playing with fire the way boys did. Harmon, bless his taciturn soul,
had understood immediately. He was a man who valued silence the way other men valued wine, and his cooperation hadn't
even required a bribe, only the acknowledgment that what they were doing mattered.

The charcoal was good. Better than good, from what Tobho said through back channels. But good charcoal alone didn't
build anything. Good charcoal was a foundation stone. Useful only if something was built on top of it.

Olenna had waited to see what her grandson would build. She hadn't waited long.

[HR]

The smithy came next, and this was where Olenna's interest sharpened from idle curiosity to active calculation.

Willas had approached Tobho through Harmon, which showed a degree of social intelligence she hadn't expected from a boy
his age. He hadn't gone directly, heir to craftsman, throwing his weight around. He'd used a trusted intermediary, a man
the smith already respected. By the time Willas walked into the forge, Tobho was already primed to listen, because
Harmon had vouched for the boy, and Harmon's word was good.

Then the bellows. Then the steel. Olenna had tracked it all through her network of eyes and ears, the quiet web of
household informants that she maintained with the same casual attention other women devoted to embroidery. She knew
about the double-acting bellows before Tobho finished building them. She knew about the first carburization run before
the crucible cooled.

And she knew, with a certainty that settled in her bones like winter cold, that this was no longer a child's hobby.

The improved steel changed everything. Olenna had it tested quietly, three smiths in three different towns, each told
the sample came from a visiting Essosi merchant. She'd varied the cover story because consistent lies were detectable
lies. All three had said the same thing in different words: this steel was exceptional.

One had wept. Men were always weeping over things. It was exhausting.

But weeping meant value. Weeping meant a smith who would sell his mother's teeth for a steady supply. And that meant
leverage, and leverage was the only currency Olenna truly respected.

She couldn't let the steel sit in Tobho's forge. A secret that stayed in one room was a curiosity. A secret distributed
through a network was power.

And that was when she'd started building.

Gareth Fossoway arrived on a Tuesday. Second son of a second son. Hungry without being important, her favorite
combination. She'd laid out the plan for the Tyrell Rose Smithy over the course of an hour, watching his face cycle
through disbelief, calculation, and greed in roughly equal measure.

"You want to open smithies," he'd said, not quite believing it.

"I want to build a network, Gareth. The smithies are just the shopfronts." She'd leaned forward. "Any smith who
works under the Tyrell rose gets access to our steel. Better than anything they've ever used. In exchange, they pay a
licensing fee, maintain our quality standards, and sell at prices I set. They keep their pride and their independence.
They just happen to depend entirely on us for the one thing that makes them better than every other smith in the Reach."

Gareth had understood. Hungry men always did. They saw the meal before they saw the strings.

The charcoal was the hook. Tyrell kilns would produce the fuel. Tyrell steel would be forged with it. Tyrell smithies
would buy it. Every link in the chain led back to Highgarden. A smith who left the network lost access to the best
charcoal and the best steel simultaneously. Nobody would leave.

"You'll be the first factor," she'd told Gareth. "One part in twenty on licensing fees. Report to me directly."

He'd accepted before she finished the sentence.

[HR]

Then the crop rotation. Olenna had known about it before Willas walked into the steward's study, because Mina watched and
Olenna had been paying attention to the south fields since Harmon's bean experiment four years prior. She'd seen the
companion planting results. She'd noted the increased yields in the kitchen garden. She'd done the arithmetic.

When whispers reached her that Willas was planning to ask Merrell for test plots, she'd spoken to the steward first.

She hadn't ordered him to agree. That would have been crude, and crude was for people who lacked better tools. She'd
planted a seed, nothing more. A casual remark over the household accounts: "The south fields have been producing consistently,
haven't they? Good soil down there. It would be a shame to let consistency breed complacency."

Merrell, who worshipped consistency the way the Faith worshipped the Seven, had bristled slightly. "The south fields are
among our most productive, my lady."

"Of course they are. I merely wonder if productive is the same as optimal." She'd let the word hang. [I]Optimal.[/I] A
word that would burrow into a steward's brain like a worm into an apple, because a steward who wasn't optimal was a
steward who could be replaced.

When Willas came to Merrell two hours later with his proposal for twenty test acres, the steward had been pre-softened.
Merrell wouldn't roll over, not yet. The boy would still have to make his case, and Olenna wanted him to work for it,
because persuading a cautious man was a skill he needed to develop. But the ground was prepared. Merrell would have agreed
eventually regardless, because the proposal was sound and the risk was negligible. Olenna had simply ensured that [I]
eventually[/I] didn't stretch into [I]never[/I].

She'd watched the test plots grow with the patient attention of a woman who understood that agriculture was just
politics conducted through soil. If the rotation worked, and she was increasingly certain it would, the implications
dwarfed the steel and the charcoal combined. Steel made better swords. Food made kingdoms.

Maester Lomys's visits to the plots had been her doing as well. She hadn't asked him to go. She'd mentioned, in
passing, that "the boy's farming experiment seems to be going rather well. I do hope someone with proper training is
keeping an eye on it."

Lomys had marched out to the south fields the next morning, bristling with skepticism and clutching a notebook. Exactly as intended.

The first cycle was still in the ground when the boy surprised her again.

The plots were growing. The wheat was up, the turnips were spreading, the clover was doing whatever clover did in the
soil that Willas claimed was the whole point. Olenna had checked with Donal, the farmhand keeping records, and the early
signs were promising, but agriculture was slow work and results wouldn't come for months. She'd settled into the rhythm
of waiting, building the smithy network in the meantime, laying groundwork for the tool shops that would sell implements
to whatever farmers eventually adopted the rotation.

She'd been thinking about supply chains for plowshares when Mina brought word that the young lord was building something
new in his workshop. Something involving copper and a great deal of wine.

Olenna had sighed. The boy could not sit still. The plots weren't even harvested and he was already on to the next
thing. She'd sent Mina back to watch, expecting another incremental improvement, a better pot or a new tool or some
other useful but manageable invention she could fold into the existing plans.

She had not expected Mina to come back saying the young lord was "cooking wine and collecting the steam."

[HR]

Olenna remembered the dinner. She'd orchestrated it once she understood what the boy had made, the suggestion to Mace,
the coordination with Alerie, the whole performance designed to give him a stage and his father a reason to be proud.
She'd expected something interesting. A novelty drink, perhaps. A stronger wine.

She had not expected her first sip to rearrange her understanding of what alcohol could be.

The spirit had hit her palate like a revelation. She'd kept her face still, because Olenna Tyrell's face moved only when
she wanted it to, and what she'd wanted in that moment was to appear mildly interested while her mind raced. She didn't
know what she was tasting, not exactly. Something stronger than wine. Something [I]different[/I] from wine. The how and
the why would have to wait.

She'd gone to his workshop the next morning, and that was when the boy had laid it out. A product that didn't spoil.
That shipped without loss. That concentrated more value into less volume than anything she'd encountered in thirty years of managing Tyrell commerce. He'd come prepared with arguments about where to sell it and how to keep others from making their own,
perfectly sound in theory, and she'd made the case faster than he could present it, watching him close his mouth with
the expression of a clever person who'd just realized someone else had reached the finish line first.

Her grandson had invented the Reach's next great export, and he was nine.

Good. He needed to learn that knowing a thing and doing a thing were separated by a lifetime of experience.

The medical angle had genuinely surprised her. That was rare. Olenna did not enjoy surprises as a general principle, but
this one she'd savor. A wound treatment made from the same apparatus as a luxury drink. The boy had handed her a gift he
didn't fully appreciate, because he was thinking about medicine and she was thinking about something more elegant.

The apothecaries.

She'd already been planning them, in a vague, someday fashion. Willas's willow bark preparation worked. The
boiled-bandage practice he'd introduced during the war worked. Small things, but real, and each one a recipe that could
be taught and replicated and sold under the Tyrell rose. A network of healers' shops, carrying remedies that actually
did something, spreading the Tyrell name into every town and village through the one thing smallfolk valued above all
else: their health.

But the apothecaries had lacked a centerpiece. Willow bark was useful. Boiled bandages saved lives. Neither was exciting
enough to draw the nobility in alongside the commons. Lords didn't visit apothecaries. Lords had maesters.

Spirits changed that.

A "restorative tonic," sold through the apothecary network, made from the same distillate that the spirit houses served
in crystal cups. Diluted, infused with herbs, presented in fine bottles with the Tyrell rose on the seal. The nobles
would buy it because it was Tyrell, because it was exclusive, because it made them feel vigorous and modern. The
smallfolk would buy the wound wash because it actually worked. Same product, different concentrations, different prices,
different stories. One funded the other.

She'd need someone to run it. The name came to her before she'd finished the thought: Dalla Crane, a widow from Red
Lake, cousin to the minor Crane lords. The woman had been running a small herb trade for six years with sharp eyes,
honest scales, and the kind of quiet competence that didn't advertise itself. Olenna had filed her away years ago, the
way she filed away every capable person she encountered, in the mental ledger that was her most valuable possession.

Dalla would receive a letter by week's end.

The distillery staffing had been simpler. She'd already identified three household servants suitable before Willas had
raised the question. Quiet, young, bound to Highgarden service. She'd have them vetted and assigned within the week.

But the spirits still meant new work on top of everything else. A second still to commission. A production space to
build. Spirit houses for the drinking trade, separate from the apothecaries, because a lord who drank spirits at a
spirit house didn't want to feel like he was visiting a healer's shop. Quality control on every batch, because one
poisoned lord could destroy the brand faster than a decade of good product could build it. Pricing: high enough to
signal luxury, low enough that lords bought regularly rather than saving it for name days.

And above all: silence. The household staff who'd been at the dinner knew what they'd tasted. Servants talked. The
trick, as always, was to make the talk work for you. Mace would boast. Let him. His boasting was the best possible
cover, because a fat lord bragging about his son's clever drink was the most boring kind of news, and boring news didn't
attract the kind of attention that asked [I]how[/I].

[HR]

Olenna refilled her glass. The lavender spirit caught the evening light, clear and faintly golden.

The private ledger was open on the table. She added a new section beneath the smithy accounts and the apothecary
projections. [I]Spirits.[/I] Initial investment, estimated production capacity, projected pricing, distribution channels
to establish.

Below that, in smaller script: [I]Medical spirits. Production at cost. Distribution to Tyrell-affiliated healers only.
Framing: good management, not charity.[/I]

She closed the ledger. Locked it away.

Eight smithies to start. More to follow, as fast as Gareth could vet new candidates and she could secure locations.
Apothecaries under Dalla Crane, with the tonic as their flagship. Spirit houses she'd need to staff from scratch, which
meant finding someone with a merchant's head and a servant's discretion, and that search hadn't even begun yet. And down
on the south fields, twenty acres of test plots still growing, still untested, still months from a harvest that might
change everything or nothing.

If the rotation worked, and the early signs said it would, the implications ran deeper than anything else the boy had
done. Steel made better swords. Spirits made better revenue. But food made kingdoms. Every farmer who adopted the new
rotation would need Tyrell seed stock, Tyrell tools, Tyrell guidance. The shops she was already building would sell the
implements. The surplus grain would ship through channels she was establishing. The entire chain, from soil to market,
running through Highgarden.

But that was a future she couldn't count yet. For now, there was enough to manage with what already existed.

The boy made the seeds. She planted the garden.

Outside, the sun was setting over the Mander, painting Highgarden in shades of gold and rose. Garlan was shouting in the
training yard, sword practice from the rhythm. Mace was laughing at something, his booming laugh carrying across
courtyards like a siege engine.

And somewhere in his workshop, her eldest grandson was bent over a workbench, designing something that would change the Reach again. He didn't know about the smithy network. He didn't know about the factors and the guild structure and the letters of introduction she'd had Lomys draft with Mace's seal. He didn't know about the quiet conversations with Merrell, or the girl who watched his workshop, or the three smiths who'd tested his steel.

He thought he was working alone. He thought the garden grew itself.

Let him think that. For now.

Olenna finished the spirit, and it burned all the way down, warm and clean and full of promise.

She had work to do.
