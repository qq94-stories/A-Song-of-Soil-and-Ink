[H2]Chapter 12 — The Test Plots[/H2]

The steward of Highgarden was a man named Merrell, and he had the kind of mind that kept ledgers balanced and innovations at bay.

This was not entirely a criticism. Merrell was good at his job — excellent, even, and his job was to manage the vast agricultural estate of the Reach's most powerful house with the consistency, predictability, and freedom from catastrophe that a conservative approach to land management could provide. He'd been steward for eleven years. In that time, the harvests had never failed, the stores had never emptied, and the books had never been in the red. No sane person would jeopardize that on the strength of an eight-year-old's suggestion.

I understood this. I respected it, even. And I could have simply walked into his study and told him to allocate the acres. The heir's word was enough. Merrell would have complied, because Merrell served at the pleasure of House Tyrell, and when the heir gave an instruction, the steward followed it.

But compliance wasn't what I needed. A steward who'd been ordered to run an experiment he didn't believe in would assign the worst soil, the laziest farmhands, the seed that was going to rot anyway. He'd comply to the letter and undermine it in spirit, and eighteen months later I'd have useless data and a steward who could say [I]I told you so, my lord[/I]without ever having technically disobeyed.

I needed Merrell's competence working [I]for[/I] me. That meant persuasion, not command. And Merrell wasn't stupid. He was [I]careful[/I], and careful people needed a different approach than curious people like Harmon or Tobho. They didn't need to be excited. They needed to be reassured.

I found him in his study on a morning in mid-284 AC, a room as organized and precise as the man himself, with ledgers on shelves and quills in rows and a desk that held exactly two documents at a time because Merrell believed, correctly, that a cluttered desk was a cluttered mind.

"Steward Merrell." I stood in the doorway with the deliberate posture of a boy making a formal request: back straight, hands visible, chin up. Body language mattered. With Tobho, I'd been casual, excited, a child playing in a forge. With Merrell, I needed to be something closer to the heir he expected. Serious, measured, respectful of process.

He looked up from his ledger. Adjusted his spectacles, actual glass spectacles, a Myrish import that must have cost a modest fortune. He touched the frame before setting them down, the way you touch something you can't replace. "My lord Willas. What can I do for you?"

"I'd like to discuss the field rotation."

The spectacles came off. This was significant; Merrell removing his spectacles was the equivalent of another man pushing back from his desk or leaning forward in his chair. It meant [I]you have my full attention, and I'm not sure I want to give it to you[/I].

"The field rotation," he repeated. "What about it?"

"We follow the three-field system." Statement, not question. I already knew the answer. "Wheat, barley, fallow. Rotate through three fields, leave one empty each year to rest the soil."

"As we have for generations, yes." There was a gentle emphasis on [I]generations[/I] that carried a wealth of implied argument: this system has worked for longer than you've been alive, longer than your father has been alive, and the accumulated wisdom of generations is not to be lightly challenged by a child who reads books.

"What if we could eliminate the fallow year?"

Silence. Merrell stared at me. He didn't say no. He wouldn't — I was the heir. But I could see him reaching for the most diplomatic way to tell me I was wrong.

"The fallow year exists for a reason, my lord." His voice was careful, respectful, but firm. The voice of a steward who owed the heir honesty even when the heir didn't want it. "The soil needs rest. A field that is planted continuously will exhaust itself within a few seasons. The yields drop. The crops sicken. The field becomes useless."

"Yes. If you plant the same crop every year. But what if you rotated four crops instead of three, and one of them actually [I]restored[/I] the soil instead of depleting it?"

He put his spectacles back on. Took them off again. The man was wrestling with himself.

"I'm listening," he said. Not [I]I agree[/I]. But considerably better than [I]go away[/I].

The Norfolk four-course rotation. The agricultural innovation that had transformed English farming in the eighteenth century of whatever world The Book came from, turning a nation of periodic famines into one of the most productive agricultural economies on the planet. Not because it was complicated (it wasn't) but because it was [I]systematic[/I], and that distinction hadn't fully landed in this world yet.

The principle was nitrogen.

Plants needed it. Soil contained it, but planting crops, especially grain crops, depleted it steadily, draining the nitrogen from the soil the way a well drained groundwater. Leave the field fallow, and the soil slowly recovered through natural processes: decomposing plant matter, atmospheric deposition, the slow work of soil bacteria fixing nitrogen from the air. One year of fallow restored enough nitrogen for two years of planting. Hence the three-field system: plant, plant, rest. Plant, plant, rest.

But there was a shortcut. Legumes — clover, beans, peas, vetch — fixed their own nitrogen through the symbiotic bacteria in their root nodules. Plant a legume crop, and instead of depleting the soil, you [I]enriched[/I] it. The legume pulled nitrogen from the air and deposited it in the soil, ready for the next crop to use.

Combine this with turnips, which broke up compacted soil with their deep root systems and provided animal feed that could be stored for the lean times, and in this world, the lean times could last [I]years[/I], because Westerosi winters weren't the polite four-month affairs The Book's agricultural chapters assumed. When winter came to the Reach, it might stay for three years. Or five. Or nine. Stockpiling feed wasn't a convenience here. It was survival. And that made the four-field system not just better than the three-field. It was the difference between a kingdom that endured winter and one that starved through it.

Wheat. Turnips. Barley. Clover. Repeat.

Each crop in the sequence served the ones that followed. Wheat used the nitrogen the clover had fixed. Turnips broke up the soil compacted by the wheat harvest. Barley grew fast in the loosened, partially-restored soil. Clover fixed nitrogen and added organic matter, setting up the next wheat crop. A cycle that fed itself, literally, cycle after cycle without rest.

I explained this to Merrell without using the word [I]nitrogen[/I], because the word didn't exist in Westerosi chemistry. Instead, I talked about [I]soil strength[/I], the idea that different crops took different things from the soil and gave different things back, and that by choosing the right sequence, you could keep the soil strong without ever leaving it empty.

"The clover gives back what the wheat takes out," I said, simplifying brutally. "And the turnips open the soil up so the barley can grow deeper roots. Each crop prepares the ground for the next."

Merrell listened. He was a good listener, better than most, because his job required him to separate useful information from noise, and he was evaluating my proposal with the same analytical rigor he applied to grain prices and labor costs.

"And you know this from..."

"The Qohorik book. And one from Volantis. And..." I paused, calculating. "Harmon's been growing beans next to wheat in the kitchen garden for three years. The wheat next to the beans grows taller. He'll confirm it."

The companion planting. The tiny experiment from three years ago, the beans and the wheat, the test plot and the control plot that Harmon had expanded three times because the results were undeniable. Three years of evidence, growing quietly in the kitchen garden, waiting to be useful.

This was why patience mattered. This was why I'd started small and waited. The evidence was [I]there[/I], accumulated over seasons, and now it could be deployed.

Merrell's expression shifted. Something cautious and calculating crept in, as close to enthusiasm as Merrell ever came. "Harmon's garden plots are small. A few beds. Field-scale is different."

"Which is why I'm not asking for field-scale. Not yet." The critical concession. The sentence I'd rehearsed in my head twenty times before walking into this room. "I'm asking for test plots. A few acres. Four fields, one for each crop in the rotation. Side by side with our normal three-field system, so we can compare."

"A few acres." He weighed it. "How few?"

"Five. Five acres per plot, twenty acres total. Out of Highgarden's... what, twelve thousand acres of arable land?"

"Fourteen thousand." The correction was automatic, the reflex of a man who knew his numbers.

"Twenty acres out of fourteen thousand. Less than two tenths of one percent. If it fails completely, we've lost twenty acres of production for one season. The household won't even notice."

This was the bean experiment again. Make the cost negligible. Make the risk invisible. Make the comparison undeniable. I could see Merrell's resistance shifting from [I]this is a bad idea[/I] to [I]this can't hurt us[/I], which was where I needed him.

Merrell picked up a quill. Set it down. Picked it up again. He'd moved past weighing the idea's merit. Now he was thinking about how to justify it if it failed. A steward's concern. Political, not agricultural.

"Your father—"

"Would need to know, yes." I said it before he could finish, not because Father would object but because Merrell was doing his job. A steward who changed field allocations without informing the lord was a steward who'd forgotten his place. "I'll tell him. He won't mind."

We both knew Father wouldn't mind because Father wouldn't notice. Mace's engagement with the agricultural details of his estate was limited to knowing that the Reach was the most fertile region in Westeros and feeling good about it.

"The maester—"

"Will want to know too. I'll speak to him." I'd rather have that conversation myself than let Lomys hear it secondhand from servants and arrive at my door already offended.

Merrell regarded me for a long moment. The spectacles went back on, which meant the deliberation was over and the decision was made.

"Twenty acres," he said. "Four plots of five. I'll designate the south fields by the river. Good soil, good drainage, representative of our better land." He fixed me with a look that was part warning, part challenge. "I want records. Every plot, every season. What goes in, what comes out. Weight of seed. Weight of harvest. I want [I]numbers[/I], my lord."

I nearly laughed. He wanted numbers. He was asking me for the one thing I wanted to give him more than anything else in this world.

"You'll have them," I said.

I didn't get to Maester Lomys first. He got to me.

He appeared in my workshop the day after Merrell began preparing the test plots, his chain clinking and his expression already set. He wouldn't presume to be angry with the heir, but he was wounded in that particular way of a man whose expertise had been bypassed.

"My lord Willas." He stood in the doorway, not quite entering. "I'm told the steward is preparing experimental plots on the south fields. On your instruction."

"Test plots," I corrected gently. "Twenty acres. A comparison trial."

"The three-field system has served the Reach for centuries."

"It has." Agreement was the first step. Never argue with the premise; argue with the conclusion. "And if the test plots show that it's still the best approach, we'll have proven it. Which is valuable too."

He blinked. He'd come prepared for a child's stubbornness, not for one who agreed with the existing system and merely wanted to test an alternative alongside it.

"The soil needs rest," he said, finding his footing on familiar ground. "Every maester learns this. Plants take strength from the earth. Without a fallow period—"

"The clover gives it back."

"Clover." He managed to make the word sound faintly beneath him, as if actual plants were a concession to the undignified reality of soil. "Clover is a weed, my lord."

"Clover is a legume. Legumes improve soil. Harmon's bean plots have been demonstrating this for three years." I kept my voice respectful. Deferential, even.

"Three years in a kitchen garden," he said. "Gardens are not fields."

"Which is exactly why we need test plots. To find out if it scales."

He opened his mouth. Closed it. Opened it again.

"I will be observing these plots," he said. It wasn't a request. He was staking his claim — if agriculture was going to change on his watch, the Citadel's representative would be on record as having overseen it. The implicit corollary was clear: [I]and if it fails, I will have documented exactly why[/I].

But under the institutional posturing I caught something I hadn't expected: a sliver of genuine curiosity. Lomys had studied agriculture. He'd read the texts. If the test plots produced interesting results, the scholar in him, buried under the conservative but alive, would want to understand why.

"I'd welcome that, Maester. Your observations would be valuable."

He gave me a sharp look, checking for mockery. Found none, because there was none. I meant it. A skeptical maester who documented the results of the test plots with his own eyes was worth more than a hundred enthusiastic gardeners, because his word carried institutional weight.

"Hmm," he said, and left.

We were in the middle of a long summer, had been for years, and the maesters said it would last years more. That was the gift this world's strange seasons gave an agricultural reformer: uninterrupted growing cycles, one after another, for as long as summer held. No frozen ground, no shortened days. Just warmth and rain and the Reach's impossibly fertile soil, with no hard frost to reset the clock.

In the world The Book came from, the four-field rotation was locked to the calendar. One crop per field per year, because winter shut everything down for months. Wheat went in during autumn, grew through spring, was harvested in summer. Then turnips. Then barley. Then clover. Four years for one full rotation, and you couldn't speed it up because frost and frozen ground set the pace whether you liked it or not.

Here, the rules were different. A Westerosi summer could last five years, ten, sometimes longer, and during that time the Reach stayed warm and wet enough to grow almost continuously. Wheat still needed its four to five months to mature. Turnips, two to three. Barley, three to four. But without a winter gap between them, you could plant the next crop within weeks of harvesting the last. The bottleneck was the clover. It needed time in the ground to fix nitrogen properly, at least six months, longer if you wanted the soil truly enriched. Rush the clover and you defeated the whole purpose of the rotation.

So each full cycle ran roughly eighteen months instead of four years. Wheat to turnips to barley to clover and back to wheat, with barely a pause between. In a decade-long summer, that meant six or seven complete rotations where The Book's world would have managed two or three. The soil got richer faster. The yields climbed faster. The stockpiles grew faster.

And they needed to. Because when winter came, and it [I]would[/I] come, it wouldn't be the polite four-month affair The Book's agricultural chapters assumed. A Westerosi winter could last three years. Five. Nine. The North would freeze solid, the Riverlands would go dormant, and even the Reach would feel it. Probably not frozen — the Reach sat far enough south that true killing frost was rare even in the longest winters. But cold enough to slow growth to a crawl, stretch each cycle from eighteen months to three years or more, kill off anything tender, and turn the breadbasket of Westeros into something merely adequate. The Reach in winter could still grow, just not well, and not fast, and not enough to feed the kingdoms that came knocking when their own fields gave them nothing.

That was the real calculation. Feeding ourselves through winter, yes, but more than that: being the region everyone else depended on when their harvests failed. The North would need grain. The Riverlands would need grain. King's Landing would [I]definitely[/I] need grain.

Every cycle counted.

The first cycle was planting.

Merrell allocated the twenty acres as promised: four plots of five, side by side on the south fields, where the soil was deep and rich and the Mander's tributary provided reliable water. Beside them, a control: five more acres planted in the traditional three-field rotation, so the comparison would be direct, grown in the same soil under the same conditions.

Plot one: wheat, sown at the start of the cycle, the golden backbone of the Reach's agricultural economy.

Plot two: turnips, sown after a light ploughing. The turnip seeds went in thick. The Book recommended dense planting for root crops, which forced the plants to compete for light and drove the roots deeper, producing the soil-breaking effect that was half the point.

Plot three: barley, a quick-growing grain that would be harvested within a few months of sowing.

Plot four: clover. Red clover, specifically, which Harmon sourced from a seed merchant in Oldtown at my request and Grandmother's quiet funding. This was the keystone crop, the one that made the whole system work, and I paid more attention to its planting than to any of the others.

I hired a boy. Well, [I]hired[/I]. I asked Merrell to assign a young farmhand to the test plots full-time, someone who could tend, weed, observe, and most importantly [I]record[/I]. The boy's name was Donal, he was fourteen, he could read and write at a basic level (rare among smallfolk, valuable for my purposes), and he approached the assignment with the stiff-backed attentiveness of a young man who'd been told by the steward himself that the heir wanted this done properly.

"You write down everything," I told him, handing over a ledger that I'd ruled by hand with columns for date, plot number, observation, and measurement. "When the wheat sprouts. How tall it grows each week. How many weeds you pull. When the turnips break the surface. How deep the clover roots go. Pull up a sample plant every fortnight and measure. Everything."

Donal looked at the ledger. Looked at me. Back at the ledger.

"Everything, my lord?"

"Everything."

He nodded. He didn't understand why, and he didn't need to. He understood [I]the heir told me to[/I], which was sufficient motivation, and over the following months he would discover that systematic record-keeping, while tedious, was also strangely satisfying, the accumulation of data as its own reward, the growing ledger as evidence that you were paying attention to a world that mostly went unobserved.

The first growing cycle was uneventful. This was good. Uneventful, in agriculture, meant [I]nothing went catastrophically wrong[/I], the minimum bar for any experiment to mean anything.

The wheat grew. The turnips grew. The barley grew. The clover grew. They grew at different rates and in different ways, but they all grew, because the south fields were good soil and the weather was warm and wet and the gods, or whatever passed for gods in this world, were cooperating with my experimental design.

I visited the plots every week. Sometimes more. I measured the wheat height with a marked stick. I pulled turnip samples and weighed them on the kitchen scales, to Gormon's mild annoyance. I watched the clover spread across its plot with the quiet, relentless tenacity of a plant that had been doing its job for millions of years and didn't need a child's encouragement but got it anyway.

Donal kept his records. They were rough at first; [I]wheat is growing fine[/I] was not the level of detail I was after, but he improved with guidance, and by the second month his entries had evolved into genuinely useful observations: [I]wheat Plot 1 — 14 inches average height, no yellowing, three weeds pulled. Barley Plot 3 — 9 inches, some crowding at the east edge, thinned.[/I]

Maester Lomys visited twice. Both times, he walked the plots with his hands behind his back and his chain clinking, the posture of a man conducting an inspection. Both times, he said nothing and left. I took this as a positive sign. If the plots had been failing, he'd have said something. His silence was the sound of a man who had nothing to criticize and wasn't happy about it.

The harvest came at cycle's end. Wheat first, then the turnips, then barley. The clover stayed; it would be ploughed under before the next planting, its biomass and nitrogen-rich root nodules incorporated into the soil for the next cycle.

Merrell weighed the harvests personally.

The wheat was first, and the numbers were... fine. Fourteen bushels per acre on the test plot. Fourteen on the control. Identical. The soil hadn't had time to change yet; the clover's work was still ahead, still underground, still building toward something the first harvest couldn't show. I'd expected this. The four-field system wasn't magic. It was chemistry, and chemistry needed time.

Maester Lomys, I learned later, smiled when he heard the wheat numbers. Fourteen and fourteen. The boy's experiment had produced exactly what the traditional system produced, and wasn't that interesting.

But the wheat wasn't the point. Not yet.

The turnips were the point. 544 kg (1,200 lbs) of root vegetables per acre, harvested from land that under the traditional system would have been sitting empty. Fallow. Producing nothing. The turnips were found yield, food from acreage that the three-field system simply wrote off as the cost of keeping the soil alive. And they were storable — animal feed that could be packed away for the lean times, and in this world the lean times could last [I]years[/I].

And the clover. The clover couldn't be measured in bushels because its value wasn't in the harvest; it was in the soil. Harmon, who'd been quietly monitoring the test plots with the proprietary interest of a man who'd been growing beans next to wheat for three years, reported that the clover plot's soil was visibly different: darker, richer, looser in texture. The kind of soil that experienced gardeners called [I]good soil[/I] without being able to explain why it was good, because the explanation required a word, [I]nitrogen[/I], that wouldn't be coined for centuries.

"Next wheat on that plot will be better," Harmon said, standing at the edge of the clover field with his arms crossed and his face wearing the closest thing to a grin I'd ever seen from him. "The roots are deep. They're doing their work."

He was right.

[HR]

The second cycle proved it. Eighteen months after planting, the wheat went back into soil that had grown turnips, barley, and clover in sequence. Soil that had been broken up, aerated, and fed nitrogen by the clover's root nodules. Soil that was, by every measure Harmon and I could devise, fundamentally better than what we'd started with.

Wheat yield, test plot, second cycle: 18 bushels per acre.

Wheat yield, control: 14. Same as always. Same as it had always been.

Twenty-nine percent improvement. From the same field, the same seed, the same weather. The only difference was what had grown there before.

The barley showed a similar jump. Sixteen bushels versus twelve on the control.

Lomys stopped smiling.

The third cycle made it impossible to deny. Wheat yields on the four-field plots hit 20 bushels per acre, compared to a consistent 14 on the three-field controls. A 43 percent improvement, driven by the cumulative enrichment of the clover and the soil-structure benefits of the turnip roots.

The total food production from the four-field plots — counting the grain, the turnips, [I]and[/I] the clover's value as animal feed — was roughly 70 percent higher than the same acreage under the traditional system. Seventy percent. From the same land. The same labor. The same sun and rain.

And no fallow year. Every acre productive, every cycle. The three-field system left a third of its land empty at any given time. The four-field system left none. This alone was worth a 33 percent increase in effective acreage — before you even counted the yield improvements.

I compiled the data. Four and a half years of records, three full cycles, Donal's careful observations supplemented by my analysis, organized into a report that I presented to Merrell on a cool morning with the ceremonial gravity of a general presenting battle plans.

He read it. Slowly. With his spectacles on, which meant he was working. The quill in his hand twitched occasionally, the unconscious habit of a man who wanted to annotate but was restraining himself until he'd read the whole document.

When he finished, he set the report down. Removed his spectacles. Looked at me.

"These numbers," he said. "They're verified?"

"By Donal's daily records. By Harmon's observations. By your own harvest weights." I paused. "And by Maester Lomys's visits, if he kept notes of his own."

"He did." Merrell's voice was neutral, which from Merrell was practically enthusiasm. "He showed them to me last week. His numbers match yours."

The maester's numbers matched mine. The institutional skeptic, the man who'd called clover a weed, had independently verified the results. I couldn't have designed a better outcome if I'd scripted it.

"If we expanded this to the full south fields," Merrell said carefully, "we'd be looking at..."

"A 40 to 50 percent increase in grain production across those fields. With the turnips as supplemental feed and food. And the clover building soil quality for the following year." I kept my voice even. Measured. The voice of a boy presenting data, not a man who was barely containing the urge to jump on the desk and shout. "Gradually. Field by field. Converting as we gain experience with the rotation timing."

"Field by field." He liked that. Merrell liked gradual. Merrell liked manageable risk. "How many years to convert the south fields?"

"Three or four. A quarter of the acreage each year, so we're never fully committed to an unproven system on any given field."

He nodded slowly. The numbers had done what my arguments couldn't. Merrell didn't care about theory. He cared about yields.

"I'll draft the plan," he said. "Your father will need to approve the reallocation."

Father would approve whatever Merrell recommended, because Father approved everything Merrell recommended, because Father understood that the steward's job was to manage the things that Father preferred not to think about. Most things, frankly, that didn't involve feasting, hunting, or burnishing his reputation.

"Thank you, Steward."

He put his spectacles back on. Picked up his quill. I was dismissed by the simple expedient of a man returning to his work. From Merrell, that was practically a warm embrace.

I walked out of his study into the morning air, and the Reach stretched before me — green and gold and vast and mine in a way that had nothing to do with inheritance and everything to do with intention.

Twenty acres. That's all it had taken. Twenty acres and four years of patience and a boy who knew that the earth would tell you the truth if you were willing to count.

And the south fields were just the beginning.
