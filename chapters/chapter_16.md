[H2]Chapter 16 — Interlude: Malora — "The Mad Maid"[/H2]

[CENTER]286 AC — Oldtown[/CENTER]

[CENTER][B]-- Malora --[/B][/CENTER]

The upper chambers of the Hightower held the quiet of stone, the green smell of the herbs drying on the western beams, and the precise small sound of a vial being sealed. Malora's hands moved without hurry. The wax cooled fast in this air, and her fingers had long since learned the pace it asked of them. She pressed her seal, lifted, set the vial on the rack. Nineteen vials. The work would take her another half-hour if no one came up the stairs.

The chamber was hers because her father had given it to her at seventeen and never asked for it back. Three rooms, joined: a workroom with the long oak bench she was at now, a study with shelves on every wall, and a smaller room beyond the study where she slept on the rare nights she remembered to. The window above her bench faced south, and she had lined the southern wall with star charts in three different inks, one ink for each observation season. The chart for late summer had a fresh line drawn that morning, marking a conjunction she had been waiting six weeks to confirm. She had looked at the line twice already today, and she would look at it again before she slept.

Most of the household believed she lived among books that no woman of her station should own. Most of the household was correct. The shelves in the study split into tiers. The upper shelves held what a noblewoman of mild scholarly inclination might be allowed to read — Citadel-approved astronomy, Reach herbalism, a respectable book on the lineages of the Andal kings — and any visitor to her chambers would see that tier and conclude that the household exaggerated. The lower three shelves held the rest. Asshai herbals her cousin had brought back from Qarth. Three Valyrian glass-glyph fragments in a wooden tray. The unsigned folio on glamours that had cost her a full year's stipend at twenty-six. An old Lengii dream-text, brittle, that she had repaired herself with rice paste and patience. She had read it often enough to recite it in either of its two languages, and she had not yet shown it to anyone alive.

The "Mad Maid." She had built the name herself, slowly, a single decision at a time, beginning when she was twenty-two and had realised that the noblewoman who studies suppressed alchemy gets hounded and the noblewoman who studies "the meanings of certain birds" gets left alone. The household had completed the rest of the story for her. By thirty she had stopped having to perform the role; by thirty-five she had begun to lose track of where the performance ended and the rest of her began.

The wax cooled. She sealed the eighteenth vial, set it on the rack, reached for the nineteenth.

Footsteps. The retainer's tread, measured, three at a time on the stone stair. He took the stairs at a careful pace because he had been told, six years ago, that Lady Malora's chambers were not to be approached at speed. He was the third retainer to learn this. They tended to last about nine years before retiring to a quieter post.

She had two breaths. By the time the man's knuckles touched the door her hands had stopped working with their morning precision and her shoulders had taken on the slight forward droop she favoured when she wanted to be pitied a little; her eyes had gone half a degree out of focus on the candle in front of her. The vial in her hand was set down slightly off-true.

"My lady. Letters."

"Oh, yes." She turned, smiled, took the bundle. "Thank you, Hewen. The moon is four days from full. Did you know? It's why the geese have been louder."

"My lady."

She watched him decide what to think of this and conclude, as he always did, that the answer was nothing. She watched him notice her star charts and look away from them. He would tell the kitchen, later, that he had taken the letters up to the lady and the lady had said something about geese. The kitchen would nod. The story would end there.

"Good evening, Hewen."

"My lady."

He went. She listened to him on the stair until the sound of his step shortened and disappeared three floors below. Then she set the bundle of letters on the bench, and her shoulders straightened, and her hands were precise again.

Six letters in the bundle. Five were what they should be: a request from a cousin in the Arbor who wanted her opinion on the dating of an old shipping ledger, an apothecary in Oldtown who needed her to confirm the source of a herb he was being asked to authenticate, two correspondences in the unbroken sequence she kept with two scholars she had never met in person, and a short note from her sister Lynesse that she would read later because Lynesse's letters tended to require the right cup of wine to receive properly.

The sixth was from Perestan. She set the others aside and read the sixth where she stood.

Perestan's hand was the disciplined hand he used when he wrote to her. Smaller than the hand he used when he wrote to his maesters, and steadier; he had been writing to her for six years, and he had learned in the second year that she preferred a margin clean enough to pencil in. The letter ran to half a page. It told her three things, in the order she had taught him to put them: what had happened, who had been present, what had been said and by whom. The fourth thing, what he himself made of it, he would not include unless she asked, because she had asked him not to include it.

The thing that had happened was the Tyrell heir's appearance in Archmaester Ryam's courtyard. The thing that had been said was a sentence about disease being carried on the hands.

She read the letter once, set it down, and went to the file. The file was not where the household would expect to find it. It lived in the third folio from the right on the second-lowest shelf, between a treatise on the propagation of figs and a thin catalogue of suspect coinage. The fig treatise was a real book. The coinage catalogue had been hollowed out and was now a box. Malora had carved it herself, badly, at twenty-four, and had never replaced it because she had grown fond of the unevenness. She lifted the box, unhooked the folio behind it, and brought the folio to the bench.

The folio was three years old. It held seven entries.

The first entry was older than the folio. She had recopied it because she had forgotten to start the file when the original observation came in, and three months later she had decided the observation belonged in a file. [I]Three years ago: Alerie wrote to Lynesse that Willas was reading her old metallurgy treatise without help, and that he was six. Filed: unusual but not yet remarkable. Children of the Reach do begin reading early, and Alerie has always preferred to think well of her children.[/I]

She had written that line in the matter-of-fact register she used with herself. The matter-of-fact register was the one she trusted. It refused to flatter her.

The second entry was eighteen months old. [I]Hewen back from a Highgarden errand: a charcoal kiln that produced cleaner fuel than the household had used in years, attributed by the Highgarden retainers to "the young lord's notion." Filed: now twice attributed. The boy's name now attached to a method, not a habit.[/I]

The third entry was six moons later. Perestan, reporting an irritated maester who had received a letter from a Highgarden steward asking peculiar questions about field-rotation theory, the kind of questions a steward had no business asking. [I]Filed: third source. Tyrell heir. Recurring.[/I]

The fourth entry was five moons old. A Highgarden retainer had stayed three nights at the Hightower on his way back to the Reach, and over wine in the seneschal's hall — wine the seneschal had served at her quiet request — the man had said the young lord was much changed since the fever, and that the household had begun to put the change down to a vision the Seven had given him while he lay close to dying. [I]Filed: the cover story. Possible interpretations: (i) Lady Olenna manufactured it; (ii) the fever genuinely produced something in him; (iii) both, in sequence. The third would be the one I would back. Note: the cover story has not yet reached the wharves or the lectures. It is travelling among household retainers. Within a year it will be elsewhere.[/I]

The fifth entry was three months old. [I]Alerie's letter announcing the Oldtown visit. I will be present at the family meal. I will not attempt anything beyond the meal. Children form impressions, and my impression on him should be the one that appears to require no second look.[/I]

The sixth entry was the meal itself. She had not fitted it neatly into the file's matter-of-fact register, because the meal had not let her. She did not need to read what she had written.

The meal had been the night before he went to the Citadel. She had not eaten with the family. She rarely did. She came down between courses, the way the household expected her to, collected a plate from the side board, and went back upstairs to her bench. The seventh-floor long room had three good fireplaces and tapestries that did not yet smell of mice; she could pass through it in under a minute and be back at her star charts before the cousins from Whitegrove had finished asking after her health. The household read each appearance as proof that the Mad Maid still ate; the household required no more than that.

The night the Tyrell boy was at the table she had intended to do exactly that. Father was upstairs, where Father was. Alerie sat at the head with Baelor at her right hand and the boy at her left. Two of Malora's own siblings were there, the cousin from Whitegrove, Uncle Gunthor, an older Citadel-bound nephew who watched everyone as if he were grading them. A small enough gathering that everything could be heard, large enough that not everything had to be answered. She had stepped through the doorway, seen the table, and had been on her way to the side board when the boy came into her line of sight.

He was smaller than she had expected. She had read his age in Alerie's letters and known it as a number, and knowing it as a number had not prepared her for the height at which his head cleared the table, or for the way his collar had been turned slightly up where the household tailor had left a margin to grow into. He sat at his mother's left and ate without affectation, and his hands were resting on the table as still as the silver beside them. That was the data she had not expected to gather.

Children fidgeted at table. Children his age fidgeted because the body had not yet learned to be still on demand and the mind had not yet learned to want stillness. She had watched her cousins' children at this table for two decades. She had a working baseline. The boy's hands sat against that baseline the way a forged seal sits against an honest one: close enough that the household would notice nothing, off enough that a person looking for the seam could find it.

She had stopped at the side board. She had taken longer to fill her plate than she ever did. She had decided, in the time it took to choose between a cold cut of duck and a piece of cheese, to stay long enough to set one probe and then leave. No more. She had no intention of giving the household a second moment of Mad Maid story to chew on, and she had no intention of leaving the room without taking what she had come downstairs and not come downstairs to take.

She had crossed to the table on the side away from Alerie and stood by the boy's chair as if she had only just noticed it had a person in it.

"You have very still hands for a child," she said.

He looked at his hands. He did not move them. The pause was the right length for a child registering an adult comment; half a beat too long for a child not registering one.

"Do you know why the Hightower is so tall?"

She had built the sentence over four winters. The household heard a Mad Maid question and let the air around it close; the boy heard a question, and what he did with the question was the thing she had come down to see.

The Hightower's lower courses were the reason. The fused-stone foundations under the seventh floor, the courses laid before the visible tower had been laid, were one of the small handful of sites in Westeros where she had personally documented an anomaly that did not yield to any non-magical explanation she had been able to rule out. The anomaly was small. The lowest courses did not hold candle wax the way ordinary stone did. A drop set on the unworked surface of a foundation block did not bead and set; it spread a half-line further than surface tension allowed, and lifted clean. She had tested the effect against eleven control sites in Oldtown over four winters. She had ruled out temperature, ruled out a porous mineral, ruled out the breath of the testers. The folio with the test logs was on the shelf above her left hand at this moment, two books in from the fig treatise.

Magic in this age was thin. By her own count, felt sensitivity in a Westerosi child of noble line was somewhere on the order of one in a hundred thousand, and that figure was generous; the count had been falling since the Doom and had been falling faster since Aegon. The slow business of knowing whether a child carried any sensitivity at all took years: patterns of dream the mother half-noticed and the household preferred to forget, accidents that resolved a quarter of an inch better than they should have, the long accumulation of coincidences that finally outran chance. A meal could not give her any of that. What a meal could give her was a single forked question, asked of a child living a stair away from the most reliably anomalous stones she had ever measured.

A child without sensitivity would answer with what they already knew about towers: engineering, foundations, the weight of the upper tiers. That was where the word [I]tall[/I] led the mind that was not pulled elsewhere. A child with sensitivity would do something else. They would drift, in the answering, toward the stones rather than the height. They would ask what the foundation had been built around. They would give the wrong kind of pause.

The boy answered with engineering.

"The base is fused stone. The upper tiers taper to reduce—"

She let him finish. She was already filing the answer. He had reached for the mechanical explanation cleanly and without hesitation, and the explanation was not wrong; it was, in fact, the answer the master builders gave when asked. Whatever else the boy was, he was not a child who felt the lower stones.

She allowed herself one small movement of regret and stored it. The regret was old; she had felt it eleven times in fifteen years, each time a little less. She had no instrument that could test for sensitivity from a stranger's mouth and she had never had one. The result was, on the question of magic, the result she had expected.

But the answer's content had not been the only data point. The vocabulary had been the other. She had heard cousins his age explain the Hightower with the words [I]it's tall because it's the Hightower[/I] and a shrug. [I]Fused stone[/I] was not in the mouth of a nine-year-old. [I]Tapering courses to reduce load[/I] was not in the mouth of a nine-year-old. The vocabulary belonged to a steward thirty years into the post or a builder midway through a guild apprenticeship. It was not the vocabulary of his name day. The seam was exactly where his age was supposed to be.

She filed that too, and then she said her own line, a Mad Maid line that was also true.

"No. It's tall because the people who built the base wanted to see something that was very far away. The stone remembers what it was built for, even if we've forgotten."

She had said the line because the household needed her to say a line of that shape before she left the room. The story the household was going to tell the next morning required a Mad Maid sentence at the close, and she had given them one. The line was also the truest sentence she had spoken at the table; she had simply delivered it in the voice that ensured no one would treat it as true.

She had told him to visit the top of the tower before he left. She had picked up her plate. She had gone back upstairs.

She had returned to her chambers and written a single sentence on the page that became the sixth entry of the file, and the sentence had refused to lie flat the way the earlier entries had, so the entry now read like the handwriting of a person who had not finished thinking.

The candle on her workbench had burned down a finger's width, and she picked up Perestan's letter again and read it a second time, more slowly. The second reading was the one that mattered. [I]Tiny things, too small to see, on the hands.[/I] The phrase had not been "miasma." It had not been "humours." It had not been "evil airs." It was concrete. It was material. It belonged to a vocabulary she knew.

The vocabulary belonged to Quillion.

Her hand, still holding the pen she had picked up for no reason except that her fingers were used to a pen, stopped moving. The rest of her stayed still for the count of nineteen, by the candle, while she went over the sentence again to be certain she was not letting herself want what she had already decided to suspect.

Quillion had been an archmaester two centuries ago. He had argued — calmly, in carefully numbered theses, in what should have been a forgettable monograph — that disease travelled on surfaces and in water rather than through air. The Citadel had not so much refuted the argument as buried it. The volume [I]On Contagion[/I] had been removed from the open catalogues during the reign of Aegon the Unworthy and never reissued. She knew of three women and four men who had read the buried volume in the last fifty years. She was one of them. Two of the others were dead.

There were three ways the boy could have arrived at the phrase. The first was that the Highgarden library had Quillion. The second was that someone in his household had read Quillion and had spoken in his hearing. The third was that the boy had arrived at the framework on his own, which was the possibility her training told her to discount and her instincts told her to leave on the list.

She wrote the third possibility down anyway. It was the one she had been waiting fifteen years for. The note read: [I]the boy has either read what he should not be able to read, or he has noticed what almost no one notices. Both possibilities are interesting. One of them is the one I would prefer.[/I]

She closed the folio, set the box back over it, and sat with the Perestan letter in her left hand and the new note in her right. The letter said the boy had spoken Quillion's framework aloud in front of an archmaester. The note said she had watched a steward's vocabulary in a child's mouth, three months earlier, at her own family table. The two readings agreed in the way she least wanted them to agree. The boy had read what he should not be able to read, almost certainly; and his mind had been running ahead of his name day at a pace no nine-year-old should have managed. Both could be true. Both probably were.

The footsteps on the stair this time were Father's. Her father did not visit her chambers. He had visited her chambers four times since her thirtieth name day; she had counted. He was on the fifth visit now. She heard him because Father walked the stair at his own slow rhythm, and because the stair was older than he was and complained at the third step, the seventh step, and a step near the top that no one had ever managed to identify.

She did not put on the mask. He would not have asked her to.

"Daughter."

"Father."

He came in. He did not sit, which meant the visit would be short. He crossed to the south window and looked at her star chart for the time it took her to count to four, and then he turned and looked at her.

"Alerie wrote."

"I read it last week."

"The boy."

"Yes."

He had not asked the question he had come to ask. He was waiting for her to say something he could agree or disagree with. This was the form of conversation they had used since she was fourteen; he had taught it to her then by refusing every other form.

"The phrase is Quillion's," she said.

"You have read Quillion."

"I have."

He did not ask how. They had not had that conversation in twelve years; starting it now would have changed the rules of every conversation they had had since, and he had decided, twelve years ago, that he preferred the rules.

"What do you make of it."

"He has either read it or he has reproduced it." She let the sentence rest. "I would back the first. The second is on the list."

"The household."

"The Highgarden library. Or someone in the household with access to texts the Citadel does not list."

He considered this. He considered it longer than she had expected him to. The candle on her bench burned by another small fraction; she could see the wax bead at the rim move and gather and drop.

"You will write."

"Through Perestan."

"Not directly."

"Not directly. The boy is not ready to know we are watching, and I am not ready to be watched in return."

He nodded, once. He did not move toward the door. He stayed at the south window with his hand resting on the sill, and he looked at her star chart again, and then he asked the question he had not yet asked.

"Was he well at the meal."

She did not answer immediately. The question had a different shape from the others. It was not a question she would have known how to phrase, and Father had phrased it without effort, which meant he had been carrying it the whole conversation.

"He was well," she said. "Tired. A little thinner than Alerie cares to admit. He kissed your hand at the close. You remember?"

"I remember."

He did not look away from the star chart. The candle at her bench burned by another small fraction.

"His grandmother was small at his age," he said. "So was his father, before he ate himself wide. The boy will grow."

Willas. He had said the boy's name in a register she did not have for the boy. She had not heard him say it in the household for years. It fit oddly in his mouth and not, she thought, unhappily.

He took three steps toward the door, then stopped, with his hand at the latch, without turning around.

"If those words came from where I think they came from," he said, "he will be noticed by people who are not us. Find out where he got it before they do."

She heard the sentence in two registers. The first he had said in: the strategic register. The second he had not said in: the register of an old man who wanted his grandson safe before someone hostile reached for him.

"Father."

He went.

She listened to his step on the stair until it had passed the seventh. Then she pulled a fresh sheet of paper toward her and dipped her pen.

She would not write to the boy. She would write to Perestan, who would write to the boy, and the boy would never know he had been answering her. She would mix what she wanted to know with what would seem reasonable for a sympathetic novice to ask. She would lay her probes among Perestan's natural curiosities. Some would be ordinary. Some would not. The ordinary ones would be there to make the others invisible.

Father had carried two questions into her chambers tonight. The first was the one they had answered together: that the household had produced something the household now needed to understand. The second was the one he had asked at the window — whether the boy was well, a question only a grandfather had any reason to ask. She had watched her father hold the second question for the whole length of the first one and had felt, briefly, the shape of an interest she did not have. It would have been easy to have it. The boy was her sister's son. She had chosen not to.

She acknowledged this. Family did not require that one be uninstrumental with one's family; family ensured that one's instrumentality would not become betrayal. Hightowers did not turn on Hightowers, and within the constraint she would use him. Father would worry about whether the boy was well. She would worry about what the boy could be made to yield.

The word she allowed herself, because there was no one in the room to read her face and because the word was load-bearing for what she would do next, was [I]pawn[/I]. Useful pawn. She let the word sit and felt the shape of it. The shape was honest. She would not have used it to anyone else. Father would not have used it at all. She had not needed to use it to herself before now.

She thought, briefly, of the way the upper rooms of this house had begun to feel more closed in the last year, and of the way her father stood longer at his own south window than he had used to. She thought she might have a few years more, and she might have many, and she did not know which. Both were possible. She had filed that question, too, in a folio she did not look at often.

She dipped the pen.

[I]To my friend Perestan, with the post, on the matter of the visiting Tyrell heir.[/I]

[I]The phrase about the hands has reached me. I should like, if you can, the names of any novice in the dormitory who has heard him use the phrase a second time, and the precise wording on each occasion. There is also a small matter I am following up at home concerning the candles in the southern wall.[/I]

She paused, lifted the pen, considered the second sentence. She let it stand.

She wrote the date below it, the day of the moon, and her closing. The candle on her bench had burned by another small fraction. The night was already old.

The letter went into the leather pouch with its three brothers, ready for the morning rider, and Malora Hightower returned to her workbench and to the wax that had cooled while she had not been watching.
