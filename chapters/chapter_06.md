[H2]Chapter 6 — The Wooden Sword[/H2]

The wooden sword was heavier than it looked.

This shouldn't have surprised me — everything was heavier than it looked when you weighed roughly 18 kg (40 lbs) and your primary form of exercise was turning pages — but there's a difference between knowing a fact intellectually and experiencing it in your wrist and shoulder and the small of your back as a blunt practice weapon tries to drag your arm to the ground.

"Hold it higher, my lord."

Ser Vortimer was the master-at-arms at Highgarden, and he had the kind of patience that comes from training noble children for twenty years and knowing that most of them would grow up to be adequate swordsmen at best and that nothing he said or did would change that. He was a compact man, heavily muscled, with a salt-and-pepper beard trimmed close and eyes that missed nothing and approved of less.

I held the sword higher. My shoulder burned.

"Higher."

Higher. My arm trembled. The sword — ash wood, shaped to roughly approximate a longsword, scaled down for a child — felt like it was gaining weight with every passing second, as if it were secretly filling with lead. I could feel my grip weakening, the muscles in my forearm sending increasingly urgent signals that they had not been designed for this and would like to file a formal complaint.

"Now swing," said Ser Vortimer.

I swung. The motion was technically correct — Vortimer had demonstrated it eight times and I had an excellent memory — but the execution was terrible. The sword wobbled. My wrist collapsed halfway through the arc. The blade (such as it was) described a trajectory more reminiscent of a drunk butterfly than a killing stroke, and the practice post I was aiming at regarded me with the supreme indifference of a target that knew it was in no danger whatsoever.

I missed.

"Again," said Ser Vortimer, in the same tone he'd used for the first swing and the fifth and the twelfth. Patient. Professional. Completely unsurprised.

[HR]

This was my father's doing, of course.

Mace Tyrell, Lord of Highgarden, Warden of the South, Defender of the Marches, High Marshal of the Reach, and a man who believed that the measure of a lord was the length of his sword and the breadth of his victories — most of which, in Mace's case, were imagined or at least generously reinterpreted — had decided that his eldest son was old enough to begin martial training.

"Five is the perfect age!" he'd announced at supper, punctuating the declaration with a chicken leg for emphasis. Mace had a habit of using food as punctuation. It was, I had to admit, effective. "I held my first sword at five. My father held his first sword at five. It's tradition!"

It may or may not have been tradition. What it definitely was, was non-negotiable.

My mother had made a quiet suggestion about waiting until I was six — she'd noted my interest in reading, my time in the library, the way I seemed happiest with a book in my lap — but Mace had rolled over the objection with the cheerful, oblivious momentum of a boulder going downhill. Not maliciously. Mace was never malicious. He simply couldn't conceive of a world where his son wouldn't want to learn swordplay, because in Mace's mental universe, all boys wanted to learn swordplay, because swordplay was the finest activity available to mankind, because swords.

The logic was circular but unbreakable. Like Mace himself.

So: wooden swords. Three mornings a week, after breakfast and before my library time, in the training yard behind Highgarden's outer wall. Ser Vortimer, who'd trained three generations of Tyrell men and their household knights, presided over my education in the art of hitting things with sticks.

I did not enjoy it.

This was, I suspected, obvious to everyone involved, including Ser Vortimer, my mother, the household servants who occasionally watched from the walkway above the yard, and possibly even the practice posts. The only person to whom my lack of enthusiasm was not obvious was my father, who had the selective perception common to people who assume everyone shares their passions.

"He'll grow into it," Mace told my mother. I heard this through an open window, not deliberately — I'd been walking past, notebooks under my arm, heading for the library after a particularly grueling session that had left my shoulders feeling like they'd been used as anvils. "Every boy struggles at first. He just needs time."

"He's five, my lord," Alerie said, in that measured way she had — neither contradicting nor agreeing, just placing a fact on the table and letting it sit there.

"I was five once! And look at me now!"

I did not look at him, but I knew what I'd see: a large man with an even larger voice, already running to fat in his thirties, who talked about tourneys and martial glory with the enthusiasm of someone who'd never been in a real fight and assumed that was a minor detail. Mace Tyrell approached warfare the way he approached feasting — with tremendous confidence and very little technique.

Not that I could say this. Not that I would. Mace was my father, and whatever his delusions about martial prowess, he loved me. That was obvious in the way he watched my training sessions from the balcony, leaning forward with barely contained energy, living vicariously through every clumsy swing. He wanted me to be the knight he imagined himself to be. It wasn't a small thing, to want your son to inherit your best self, even when your best self was largely imaginary.

I loved him back. It would have been easier if I didn't.

[HR]

Ser Vortimer was a professional, and professionals notice things.

"You understand the movements," he told me one morning, a month into our sessions. We were taking a water break — his concession to my age, which meant I got one per session instead of the zero he'd have allowed an older student. "Your form is adequate for your age. Better than adequate, for the footwork."

This was true. The footwork came naturally, or at least more naturally than the sword work, because footwork was geometry and physics — angles, balance, weight distribution — and my engineer's mind processed spatial relationships faster than it processed the brutish mechanics of swinging a heavy stick at a target. I understood [I]why[/I] you stepped this way or that. I could visualize the lines of force, the vectors of attack and defense, the mathematical relationships between distance and reach and timing.

Understanding, however, is not the same as enthusiasm.

"But you train the way a scribe copies a text," Vortimer continued. "Correctly. Carefully." He paused, selecting his next word with the precision of a man who understood that children of great houses required diplomatic handling. "Dutifully."

[I]Dutifully.[/I] The word landed with the soft thud of an accurate assessment. I trained dutifully. I showed up, went through the motions, maintained adequate form, and counted the minutes until I could leave.

"I'm trying," I said. Also true. I was trying — trying to meet the minimum acceptable standard of martial competence for the heir to the Reach, no more and no less. Trying to be good enough that my father wouldn't despair and bad enough that nobody would expect me to pursue knighthood with any real vigor.

A narrow target, but I was getting better at hitting narrow targets. Metaphorically, at least. Literally, the practice post remained stubbornly unimpressed by my efforts.

Vortimer studied me. He was not a man who wasted words on problems he couldn't solve, and I think he recognized — perhaps before I did — that the problem here was not one of skill but of will. My body was willing. My five-year-old muscles were developing the way five-year-old muscles develop when subjected to regular exercise, which is to say: slowly, but noticeably. I could hold the sword longer than I could a month ago. My swings were straighter. My grip was stronger.

But my heart was in the library, and we both knew it.

"Horsemanship tomorrow," he said, changing the subject with the practiced ease of a man who'd decided to try a different door. "You'll like the horses."

I would, as it turned out, like the horses. Not because riding triggered some latent martial spirit — it did not — but because horses were a technology, and technologies interested me.

[HR]

The horses of Highgarden were beautiful animals, bred for generations with the kind of careful selection that this world applied to livestock instinctively even without understanding the genetics behind it. Big-boned coursers, palfreys with smooth gaits, destriers bred for war with temperaments that ranged from "tolerant" to "actively homicidal."

They put me on a pony. A round, patient, elderly pony named — and I am not making this up — Pudding.

Pudding was the equine equivalent of a very comfortable chair. He had no opinions about anything, moved at a speed that could charitably be described as "deliberate," and responded to commands with the languid indifference of a creature that had been carrying small children for so long that he could do it in his sleep, and frequently did, based on the way his eyes half-closed during our circuits of the training yard.

I learned to ride Pudding the way you learn to operate a particularly unenthusiastic piece of furniture. Sit up straight. Grip with the knees. Don't pull the reins unless you mean it, and even if you do mean it, Pudding will think about it for a while before deciding whether to comply.

But while my body was learning the surprisingly complex skill of staying on top of a moving animal — core strength, balance, the subtle communication between rider and mount that was, at its heart, an engineering problem of two dynamic systems learning to synchronize — my mind was elsewhere.

My mind was watching Ser Vortimer's other students.

Highgarden maintained a cadre of squires and young household knights, boys and young men between eight and eighteen who trained daily in the yard. I watched them while Pudding ambled in slow circles, and what I saw was an education in medieval military science — its strengths and its considerable limitations.

The swordwork was good. Better than good — the Reach produced fine knights, and Vortimer was a skilled instructor. But the methodology was pure tradition. Techniques passed down master to student, refined over generations, never analyzed, never systematized. Nobody asked [I]why[/I] a particular guard position worked against a specific attack — they just knew it did, because their teacher's teacher had taught it that way.

Force. Leverage. Angles.

The words appeared in my mind with the crystal clarity of The Book's physics sections, and I started seeing the training yard differently. Every sword swing was a problem in rotational mechanics — moment of inertia, angular velocity, the transfer of kinetic energy from arm to blade to target. Every defensive position was a structural engineering challenge — how to arrange the body and weapon to redirect incoming force with minimum energy expenditure. Every step, every dodge, every clash of practice swords was physics, and nobody knew it was physics, and that was both fascinating and deeply frustrating.

Because if you understood the physics, you could optimize.

You could design better weapons.

The thought arrived quietly, the way the important thoughts always did, and lodged itself in the part of my brain that was rapidly becoming the Department of Things I Can't Do Yet But Am Going to Write Down.

Crossbows.

[HR]

That evening, in my room, notebook on my knees, candle flickering, I sketched.

The crossbow was not a new invention. It existed in Westeros — had existed for centuries, in various forms, mostly crude. A bow mounted on a stock, drawn by hand or with a simple lever, firing bolts with reasonable accuracy at short range. Useful but limited. Not a battlefield weapon, not here — not when armored knights on horseback dominated warfare and the social structure that supported them specifically devalued projectile weapons as unknightly, unworthy, the weapons of peasants and cowards.

Which was, from an engineering perspective, idiotic.

The Book's section on crossbow design was extensive. Spanning centuries of development, from the simple hand-drawn cross to the complex windlass-and-pulley mechanisms that could store enough energy to punch a bolt through plate armor at 183 m (200 yards). The evolution was a case study in mechanical engineering: cam mechanisms, ratchets, cranequins, goat's-foot levers, each innovation increasing the draw weight that a single person could manage, each pushing the effective power of the weapon further beyond what human muscle alone could provide.

Force multiplication. That was the key. A man couldn't draw a bow with 136 kg (300 lbs) of force, but a man with a mechanical advantage could cock a crossbow to 136 kg (300 lbs) of draw weight and release it with a finger. The machine did what the body couldn't, and the result was a projectile weapon that didn't care how strong or skilled or noble you were — it just cared about the mathematics of stored elastic potential energy and ballistic trajectory.

I sketched a basic crossbow first. The kind that already existed here, more or less. Wooden stock, steel bow, simple stirrup for the foot so you could draw the string with both hands while standing on it. Nothing revolutionary. Reference point.

Then I started improving it.

A lever mechanism — the goat's-foot lever, a simple L-shaped device that hooked onto the string and multiplied the user's pulling force by a factor of three or four. I drew it from The Book's diagrams, adjusting the proportions, simplifying the design for the metalworking capabilities I thought this world's smiths could manage.

A basic trigger mechanism — the revolving nut, a cylinder with a notch that held the drawn string until a long trigger lever rotated it to release. Simple. Elegant. The kind of engineering that looked obvious after someone showed you and was nearly impossible to invent from scratch.

A stirrup redesign that allowed the crossbow to be drawn while the user was standing, kneeling, or braced against a wall. Versatility. Flexibility.

I sketched for an hour, my handwriting forgotten, the drawings increasingly detailed and increasingly dangerous. If anyone found these pages, they wouldn't see a child's scribbles — they'd see technical diagrams for a weapon that could put a bolt through chainmail at ranges that made conventional archery irrelevant.

So I tore the pages out and burned them.

Not because I was going to forget — I would never forget, The Book was permanent — but because the notebook had to stay clean. The notebook was my public secret, the one that people knew about and that contained nothing more threatening than bean measurements and childish sketches. The real plans lived in my head, behind walls that no one could breach because no one knew they existed.

I watched the crossbow designs curl and blacken in the candle flame, and I thought about Ser Vortimer and his training yard, and about force multiplication, and about the fact that somewhere in the future — years, decades — these ideas would stop being sketches and start being objects.

But not yet. Not for a long time.

The beans first. The soil. The crops. The boring foundations that nobody wrote songs about. The swords and crossbows could wait.

[HR]

My father watched a training session on a bright morning in what I estimated was late spring.

I didn't know he was watching at first. Vortimer didn't tell me — the old knight was too professional for that, knew that an observed student performs differently than an unobserved one, and wanted Mace to see the unvarnished truth. Which was: his son was competent. Not gifted. Not passionate. Competent.

I was working through the basic sword forms — eight positions, each flowing into the next, a choreography of attack and defense that Vortimer had drilled into me until I could perform them in sequence without thinking. Which was exactly the problem. I performed them without thinking. The motions were correct and utterly hollow, the way a student recites a poem they've memorized but don't understand.

Strike. Parry. Step. Strike. Rotate. Parry. Step back. Guard.

Correct. Careful. Dutiful.

I felt his eyes on me before I heard him. Something shifted in the quality of the attention directed at the yard — the servants straightened, Vortimer's other students glanced upward — and I knew.

I didn't change anything. Didn't try to swing harder or move faster or fake an enthusiasm I didn't feel. That would have been a different kind of lie, and also, I was five and a half years old and my capacity for athletic performance enhancement through sheer willpower was limited by the fact that my muscles had the density of wet bread.

When the session ended, I wiped my face with a cloth and looked up at the balcony. Mace stood there, both hands on the railing, his broad face arranged in an expression I recognized because I'd seen it on fathers' faces before, in some half-remembered context that The Book didn't cover but that my soul — if I had one — apparently retained.

Disappointment. Not the sharp kind. Not anger. The soft kind that comes from watching your child do something adequately and knowing, in the deep place where you keep the things you don't say out loud, that adequate is all it's ever going to be.

He smiled when he saw me looking. Big, broad, Mace's smile, the one that could fill a room. "Good work, son! Coming along!"

"Thank you, Father!"

He left the balcony. I stood in the yard and felt something complicated settle in my chest.

[HR]

The thing about Mace is that he's not a bad father. He's a bad [I]fit[/I].

The distinction matters. A bad father would be cruel, indifferent, actively harmful. Mace is none of these things. Mace loves me with the uncomplicated, slightly baffled warmth of a man who produced a child and cannot quite figure out how the child he produced is the child he ended up with. He brings me sweets from the kitchen. He ruffles my hair. He tells me stories about his tourneys, always, polished and embroidered until the story bore roughly the same relationship to reality that a portrait bears to its subject, which is to say: recognizable but better-looking.

He wants me to be a knight. A warrior. A lord who leads from the front with a sword in his hand and a warhorse between his knees, because that is what lords do, that is what Tyrells do, that is the natural order of things as Mace Tyrell understands the natural order of things.

I am never going to be that.

Not because I can't — with enough training, my body will become serviceable, adequate, capable of holding a sword and sitting a horse and going through the motions of martial competence. Not because I refuse — I train three days a week and will continue to train because this world demands a minimum standard of martial capability from its nobles and meeting that standard is survival, not choice.

But because my heart — my strange, displaced, adult heart trapped in a child's chest — belongs to the notebook and the garden and the library, to the plans that live in my head, to the slow patient work of building something that will outlast any single swing of any single sword.

Mace wants a knight. He has an engineer.

I can't tell him this. Not in words he'd understand, and certainly not at five and a half years old. So I train, and I'm adequate, and he watches from the balcony, and we both carry the weight of the gap between what he wants and what I am, and neither of us mentions it because love makes you protect the people you love from the truth even when the truth is standing in the training yard holding a wooden sword like it might bite.

This will be permanent. I know this the way I know the nitrogen cycle or the principles of mechanical advantage — not as hope or fear but as fact, observed and catalogued and accepted. Mace will always want something I can't give him. I will always give him something he doesn't know how to want. And between those two truths, a family will exist, imperfect and loving and fundamentally misaligned, the way most families are.

I'll manage it. I manage everything now — the library, the garden, the maester, the notebook, the careful architecture of a child who is less and more than he appears. Managing my father's expectations is just another variable in a very complex equation.

Not solving it. Managing it. Some equations don't have solutions. They have equilibria — states of tension that hold steady so long as nobody pushes too hard. I wouldn't push. Mace wouldn't push. We'd meet in the middle, at the point where love and disappointment balance out to something livable, and we'd stay there, and it would be enough.

It would have to be.

[HR]

That night, I trained in a different way.

Crossbow designs in my head. Lever mechanisms, trigger assemblies, draw-weight calculations. The Book's pages turning in the dark behind my closed eyes, each diagram sharp and perfect, each specification exact.

I couldn't build them. Couldn't even draw them safely — the burned pages in my candle were proof of that precaution. But I could think about them, refine them, hold the designs in my mind and rotate them the way an engineer rotates a model, looking for weaknesses, improvements, simplifications.

Someday, years from now, I'd have the authority and the resources to build things. Real things. Not bean experiments and notebook sketches but actual machines, actual tools, actual changes to the way this world worked. The crossbow was one piece. The agricultural reforms were another. The printing press that I couldn't let myself think about yet because it was so impossibly far away was another.

For now, I had a wooden sword I didn't love, a garden that nobody noticed, a notebook full of secrets, and a father who wished I were someone else.

For now, that was what I had.

The list of things I was going to change was very long. The list of things I could change right now was very short. And the distance between the two lists was measured not in pages or miles but in years — years of growing, learning, building trust, building credibility, building the careful architecture of a person that people would listen to when the time came to say things worth listening to.

I rolled over in bed, pulled the blanket to my chin, and closed my eyes.

Tomorrow there would be more sword training, and I would be adequate. More library time, and I would be careful. More garden visits, and I would be patient. More notebook entries, and I would be invisible.

And the beans would keep growing, quiet and green and entirely unremarkable, doing their invisible work in the soil where nobody thought to look.

Good enough. For now, good enough.
