[H2]Chapter 11 — The Blacksmith[/H2]

The castle smith was named Tobho.

Not Tobho Mott of King's Landing, the famous one, the man who could rework Valyrian steel and charged prices that made Lannister bankers flinch. This was a different Tobho entirely, a Reach man, born in a village near Bitterbridge, who'd apprenticed at Highgarden's forge at twelve and never left. He was in his mid-forties now, thick across the shoulders with the lopsided musculature that came from decades of hammering; his right arm visibly larger than his left, like a man who'd been assembled from two different people and no one had noticed.

He was also, as I discovered within five minutes of meeting him, the most stubbornly skeptical person I'd ever encountered. And I'd met my grandmother.

"You're the boy with the charcoal," he said, looking down at me from a height that was frankly unnecessary. Tobho was not a tall man, but I was eight and still hadn't hit the growth spurt that The Book's chapters on human development assured me was coming eventually, so [I]everyone[/I] was looking down at me, and Tobho managed to do it with a specificity of judgment that made the height difference feel personal.

"I'm the boy with the charcoal," I confirmed.

"Good charcoal." He said this the way a man might say "nice weather," an acknowledgment of fact, stripped of enthusiasm, implying that while the fact was noted it didn't necessarily lead anywhere interesting. "Your man Harmon said you built a kiln."

"A brick kiln. With air controls." I'd prepared for this meeting the way a general prepared for battle: with reconnaissance, strategy, and the grim awareness that the first plan never survived contact with the enemy. Tobho wasn't the enemy, exactly, just a master craftsman being approached by a child, and the gap between what I knew and what I could credibly claim to know was a canyon that I had to cross without falling in. "The charcoal burns hotter because the conversion is more complete. Less raw wood left in the finished product, fewer impurities."

He stared at me. The stare of a man recalibrating his assumptions about what eight-year-olds said.

"Impurities," he repeated.

"The parts that aren't carbon." I caught myself. Stepped back. [I]Child. You are a child.[/I] "The parts that aren't... the burny bit. The wood stuff that didn't finish turning into charcoal."

Better. Clumsier. More age-appropriate. Tobho's expression shifted from [I]alarmed[/I] to [I]amused[/I], which was a significant improvement.

"The burny bit," he said. Was that the ghost of a smile? Hard to tell under the soot.

"Could I see your forge?"

He looked at me for a long moment. Behind him, the forge breathed, the rhythmic wheeze of bellows pushed by an apprentice, the dull red glow of coals, the smell of hot metal and quench oil that was, I was forced to admit, one of the most appealing smells I'd encountered in this or any other life. There was something primal about a smithy. Something that spoke to whatever part of the human brain had decided, a hundred thousand years ago, that hitting rocks until they became useful was a good way to spend an afternoon.

"Come on, then," Tobho said, and turned back into the forge, resigned in the way men are resigned when they've learned long ago that refusing a lord's heir is more trouble than accommodating one.

I followed him into the heat.

The forge was everything I'd expected and less.

That sounds cruel. It wasn't meant to be. Tobho's forge was a competent, functional workspace, well-organized, well-maintained, equipped with the tools and infrastructure necessary to serve a great castle's metalworking needs. He had an anvil of good quality, a coal forge with a side-blast tuyere, a quench trough, a grinding stone, racks of hammers and tongs in graduated sizes, and the accumulated paraphernalia of a career spent turning raw metal into useful objects.

What he didn't have was temperature, or at least enough of it. The forge ran on traditional charcoal, the inconsistent, partially-converted stuff that the castle's regular supplier provided, and the bellows were the standard type: two leather bags on a wooden frame, pumped by hand or by apprentice, delivering air to the fire in intermittent pulses that raised the temperature in bursts but couldn't sustain the steady, high-oxygen environment that serious metallurgy required.

The result was iron and low-carbon steel. Serviceable. Functional. The kind of metalwork that equipped Highgarden's guards and kitchen staff and horse shoes and door hinges and the hundred other mundane applications that kept a castle running. Tobho was good, better than good; he was skilled and experienced and had the craftsman's intuition that comes from decades of watching metal move under the hammer. But he was working with limitations that he couldn't see because he'd never worked without them.

The bellows were the bottleneck. More air meant more oxygen meant higher temperatures meant better steel. The equation was that simple.

I didn't say any of this. I watched. I asked questions. I sat on an upturned bucket because there were no chairs in a smithy and I was too short for the standing-height surfaces, and I observed Tobho working a piece of iron bar into a door bracket, studying his technique the way a student studies a master in class.

His hammer work was precise. Economical. He didn't waste energy on showy strikes; each blow landed where it needed to land, with the force it needed, at the angle that moved the metal in the desired direction. The apprentice, a boy of maybe fourteen with the permanently startled expression of someone who'd recently discovered that metalworking involved getting very hot, pumped the bellows on command and fetched tools with the scrambling urgency of the young and terrified.

"You heat it until it's yellow?" I asked, watching Tobho pull the iron from the forge. The metal glowed a dull orange-red, hot, but not the bright yellow-white that indicated temperatures above 1,000°C (1,830°F).

"Yellow's for welding." He set the iron on the anvil and began to shape it. "This just needs forming. Orange is enough."

"Can you [I]get[/I] it to yellow? With those bellows?"

The hammer paused. A fraction of a second. Then it resumed, and the pause might have been nothing — a natural break in the rhythm, a momentary consideration of angle or force. But I thought it was something else. I thought it was the pause of a man who'd been asked a question he'd asked himself.

"Sometimes," he said. "On a good day, with fresh charcoal and a strong arm on the bellows. Never for long."

Never for long. And that was the problem. Welding temperature, the point where iron became plastic enough to forge-weld two pieces together, the technique that turned wrought iron into pattern-welded steel, required sustained high heat. Sustained, not the brief spike that faded as the bellows rhythm faltered and the apprentice's arms tired.

"What if the bellows were better?"

Another pause. Longer this time. Tobho set down his hammer, which was the smith's equivalent of a knight sheathing his sword; a gesture that meant [I]you have my attention[/I].

"Better how?"

I brought him the drawings the next day.

Not my drawings. I'd redrawn them overnight, translating The Book's precise technical diagrams into something that looked like a clever child's attempt to design a bigger bellows. Slightly messy. Slightly naive. With labels that spelled things almost correctly and dimensions that were given in terms like [I]this long[/I] with an arrow, rather than precise measurements.

The underlying design, however, was sound. Double-acting bellows, a hinged pair of chambers that delivered air on both the up-stroke and the down-stroke, eliminating the dead space in the pumping cycle where traditional single-chamber bellows delivered no air at all. The result: continuous airflow instead of pulsed, roughly twice the volume per minute, at pressures that were higher and more consistent.

It wasn't a new invention. Double-acting bellows had existed in various forms across human history; the Chinese had used them for centuries, and European blast furnaces would eventually adopt water-powered versions that produced enough airflow to smelt iron on an industrial scale. What I was proposing was simply a better version of what Tobho already had, built with materials he already possessed, requiring no technology that didn't already exist.

The trick, as always, was the presentation. "I was reading the Qohorik book," I said, handing him the drawings. "And it had this picture of their bellows. They're different from yours. They push air... both ways?"

Tobho took the parchment. Held it up to the light of the forge. His eyes moved over the drawing with the slow, careful attention of a man who reads diagrams the way scholars read text: fluently, instinctively, translating lines and labels into three-dimensional understanding.

He was quiet for a long time, and then: "Both chambers push air," he said finally. It wasn't a question. It was a statement, working it through. "When this one's blowing, that one's filling."

"The book said Qohorik smiths can keep their forges hotter. For longer. Because the air never stops." I bounced on my heels slightly. Child's enthusiasm. Genuine enthusiasm wearing a child's mask. "Could we build one? Could you build one?"

Tobho looked at me over the top of the parchment. The skepticism was still there — deeply embedded, a feature of his personality as permanent as the soot in his pores. But alongside it now was a new signal. Curiosity. The specific, dangerous curiosity of a craftsman who's been shown a tool he doesn't have and can already imagine what he'd do with it.

"This valve," he said, tapping the drawing. "Leather flap, hinged at the top. Opens in, doesn't open out."

"Like a door that only swings one way."

"I know what a one-way valve is, lad." The first time he'd called me [I]lad[/I] instead of [I]my lord[/I]. A demotion in protocol that was actually a promotion in something more important: he was talking to me as a fellow interested party, not as a lord's son.

"Air comes in through the intake," he continued, tracing the path on the drawing with a soot-blackened finger. "Chamber fills. Push the handle, air compresses, can't go back out the intake because the valve closes, so it goes forward through the outlet pipe. To the fire." He tapped the outlet. "And while this side is pushing, the other side is pulling in. So the fire never starves."

"That's what the book said."

"Book's not wrong." He set the parchment down on the anvil, anchoring it with a hammer. "Whoever drew this knew what they were about."

The Qohorik author. The fictional Qohorik author, whose book on metalworking had described bellows that happened to match the ones The Book detailed in its section on pre-industrial metallurgy. Convenient, that.

"Can you build it?"

Tobho scratched the back of his neck. The gesture of a man doing math. "The chambers, wood and leather, same as what I've got. The valves, those I'd need to think on. Leather wears. Gets stiff. If the flap doesn't seat properly, you lose your seal and you're back to pulsing."

"What if the valve seat was fitted?" I was pushing, and I knew it, and the eight-year-old mask was slipping. But Tobho was engaged now, leaning forward, thinking like a builder, and the conversation had its own momentum. "Smoothed and shaped so the leather sits flush against it?"

"Aye, that'd help. And if I hinged the flap on a bit of light chain instead of stitching it, it'd last longer. Chain doesn't crack like thread." He was talking to himself now, the way craftsmen did when they were designing in real time, speaking the blueprint into existence. "Outlet pipe, copper, I'd think. Copper for the tuyere too, instead of the clay one I've got. Clay cracks in the heat. Copper won't."

"How long to build it?"

He looked at me. The skepticism had faded to a background hum, still present but drowned out by the louder signal of professional interest. "A week, if I work on it between other jobs. Two, if your lord father needs anything urgent."

Father wouldn't need anything urgent. Father's engagement with the forge extended to occasionally requesting decorative ironwork for the gardens and expecting his armor to be maintained in a state of gleaming readiness for tournaments he was increasingly too heavy to compete in comfortably.

"I'll bring you charcoal," I said. "The good stuff. From the kiln."

And there it was. The trade. The partnership, forming in the space between a boy's offer and a man's acceptance. I had knowledge and fuel. He had skill and decades of hands-on understanding that no book could replace. Neither of us could do what I was proposing alone.

Tobho picked up the parchment. Folded it carefully, more carefully than he'd handle most things, with a precision that told me he valued what was on it, and tucked it into the pocket of his leather apron.

"Bring your charcoal, lad," he said. "We'll see what happens."

What followed was three weeks of iteration, argument, and steadily improving results. The first bellows Tobho built worked. Functionally, mechanically, it worked: the double-acting design delivered continuous air the way the diagram promised, and the forge temperature climbed noticeably within the first hour of use. The apprentice, whose name was Pate and whose arms had previously set the cadence of the entire operation, found that the new rhythm, a steady, even pumping rather than the frantic gasping of the old single-chamber design, was actually easier. Less force per stroke. More output per minute. The engineering term was [I]mechanical advantage[/I], and while I didn't use that term because eight-year-olds didn't, the result was obvious enough to need no naming.

But [I]worked[/I] wasn't [I]worked well[/I]. The leather valves leaked. Nothing dramatic, no catastrophic failure or blow-out, just a steady seep of air backwards through imperfect seals that reduced the pressure and created an annoying whistle that the apprentice found hilarious and Tobho found personally insulting.

"Leather's too stiff," Tobho announced on the second day, dissecting the bellows like a surgeon at work. "Won't conform to the seat. There's a gap here. Feel this."

He handed me the valve flap. I felt the edge. He was right. The leather, treated to resist heat and moisture, had lost the pliability needed to seal against the wooden valve seat. A material science problem masquerading as a design problem.

"Thinner leather for the valve," I suggested. "Softer. And coat the seat with tallow before you fit the flap. It'll fill the small gaps."

Tobho gave me a look. The look of a man being told something by a child that he should have thought of himself. There was annoyance in it, but under the annoyance was something more interesting: respect. Earned respect, too — respect for the suggestion itself, evaluated on its merits by a man who evaluated everything on its merits because that was how you survived in a trade where bad work got people hurt.

"Tallow on the seat." He turned it over. "That'll work until the tallow melts from the heat."

"How hot does the bellows body get?"

"Not very. The hot end's at the forge, not the pump."

"Then the tallow should hold."

He grunted. The Tobho version of [I]you're right and I'll never say so out loud[/I].

The rebuild took a day. Thinner, softer leather for the valve flaps. Tallow on the seats. Chain hinges instead of stitched ones, as Tobho had suggested in our first conversation, because the man remembered every detail of every design discussion and implemented improvements without being reminded.

Version two worked well. Far from perfectly: there was still a slight wheeze in the left chamber that drove Tobho to quiet fury. But well enough that the forge temperature climbed to a point where Tobho could do something he said he hadn't done consistently in years.

He reached welding heat.

I watched him forge-weld two pieces of iron bar into a single piece, the metal at yellow-white heat, the hammer blows sending showers of sparks that the apprentice dodged with practiced agility. The weld was clean. Solid. When Tobho examined it after quenching, turning the piece over in his tongs, his face had the expression of a man rediscovering something he'd lost.

"Good heat," he said. Two words carrying the weight of twenty years of frustration at working with insufficient tools.

"Good bellows," I said.

He looked at me. Then, and this was remarkable enough to warrant documenting — he smiled. A small smile, reluctant, extracted from his face against its will by the sheer pressure of professional satisfaction. But a smile.

"Good bellows," he agreed.

The steel came next. Because steel was the point. Steel was always the point.

Iron was soft. Useful, versatile, the backbone of every medieval economy, but [I]soft[/I]. An iron blade could be sharpened, but it dulled quickly. An iron tool could be shaped, but it bent under stress. Iron was the baseline, the material that worked well enough until you needed something that worked well, at which point you needed steel.

Steel was iron plus carbon. That was it. That was the entire secret, the alchemical mystery that the maesters and the smiths and the philosophers talked around without ever quite pinning down. Take iron. Add carbon — a small amount, between 0.2 and 2.1 percent by weight, though I was not about to start quoting percentages to Tobho. Heat them together until the carbon atoms migrated into the iron's crystalline structure, filling the gaps between iron atoms and locking the lattice into a harder, stronger, more rigid configuration.

The carbon came from the charcoal. My charcoal, specifically, the high-quality, consistently-converted product of the kiln, which was higher in pure carbon content than the traditional stuff because more of the wood had completed the pyrolysis process. Better charcoal meant more available carbon. More available carbon meant more efficient carburization. More efficient carburization meant better steel.

The bellows provided the heat. The charcoal provided the fuel and the carbon. The smith provided the skill.

"I want to try something," I told Tobho on a morning two weeks after the bellows were installed. "A different way of working the iron."

He crossed his arms. The posture of a man who had learned to be wary of sentences that began with [I]I want to try something[/I] coming from a boy who'd already rebuilt his bellows and improved his fuel supply.

"Different how?"

"Longer in the fire. With the charcoal packed around it. We don't just heat it to shape it; we [I]soak[/I] it. Let the iron sit in the charcoal at high heat for... a while."

Carburization. Case hardening, in its simplest form: pack iron in charcoal, heat it, and hold it at temperature long enough for the carbon to diffuse into the surface layers. The longer you held it, the deeper the carbon penetrated, the harder the steel became. The Book's section on historical metallurgy devoted several pages to the technique, which had been used in one form or another by every pre-industrial civilization that worked iron.

Tobho knew the principle. Of course he did; he'd been making steel for thirty years, and the basic method of heating iron in charcoal wasn't a secret so much as a practice. What he didn't have was the understanding of [I]why[/I] it worked, which meant he couldn't optimize the process. He worked by feel: heat it until it looked right, quench it until it sounded right, temper it until it felt right. Good results, sometimes. Inconsistent results, always.

"Soaking," he said, tasting the word. "For how long?"

"Hours." I'd calculated it from The Book's data on carbon diffusion rates at various temperatures, adjusting for the uncertainties of a pre-industrial forge. At the temperatures Tobho could now sustain with the improved bellows and charcoal, somewhere in the range of 900-1,000°C (1,650-1,830°F), I estimated, though I had no thermometer and was working from the color of the heated metal, carbon would diffuse into wrought iron at a rate of roughly a millimetre (1/25 inch) per hour. Four hours would produce a hardened case approximately 2 mm (1/12 inch) deep, which was enough for a knife blade. Eight hours would go deeper, producing a piece that was steel through and through rather than just on the surface.

"Hours," Tobho repeated. "In the fire. Packed in charcoal."

"The Qohorik book says—"

"You and that bloody book." But he was interested. I could see it — the same curiosity that had lit up when he'd seen the bellows design, the craftsman's hunger for a better way. "Hours, you say. The fuel cost alone..."

"I'll provide the charcoal. From the kiln. No cost to you."

He uncrossed his arms. Progress.

We set up the first carburization run that afternoon. A small bar of wrought iron, nothing special, stock material from Tobho's supply, packed in a clay crucible filled with crushed charcoal from my kiln. The crucible went into the forge, the bellows pumped to a steady rhythm by Pate, and we waited.

Four hours. Tobho worked on other things; a castle needed horseshoes and hinges regardless of what experimental metallurgy was happening in the corner, but I saw him glancing at the crucible every few minutes, checking the color of the glow, assessing the temperature by sight the way he assessed everything.

At the end of four hours, he pulled the crucible from the forge with long tongs, cracked it open, and extracted the iron bar. It glowed yellow-white. He set it on the anvil and struck it — once, experimentally, the careful tap of a man testing material he doesn't know.

The bar didn't deform the way wrought iron deformed. It moved less. Resisted more. The hammer bounce was different: sharper, more elastic, the feel of metal that had hardened beyond its original state.

Tobho hit it again. Harder. Then he quenched it, plunging it into the water trough, where it screamed and steamed and emerged dark and transformed.

He tested the edge with a file. The file skated. On wrought iron, a file bit — on steel, it slid, because the hardened surface was harder than the file's teeth.

Tobho set down the file. Picked up the bar. Turned it over in his hands with the slow, reverent attention of a man holding something important.

"This is good steel," he said quietly. "This is very good steel."

It wasn't great steel, of course. It was no Valyrian work, no Qohorik master-blade, none of the legendary pinnacles of what the craft could reach. But [I]very good[/I] steel, produced consistently, from ordinary iron and better-than-ordinary charcoal and a process that could be repeated and refined and taught.

"It's the carbon," I said, and then cursed myself silently because [I]carbon[/I] was not a word that existed in the Westerosi vocabulary and Tobho was looking at me with an expression that demanded explanation.

"The... charcoal essence. The burning part of the charcoal. It goes into the iron when you soak it. Makes it harder." Crude. Reductive. Scientifically painful. But close enough to the truth to be useful and vague enough to be plausible.

"Charcoal into iron," Tobho said. He turned the bar over again. "My master used to say the fire put its strength into the metal. Thought it was poetry." He looked at me. "Maybe it was closer to the truth than I thought."

"The fire's just the delivery method. The charcoal is the ingredient."

He nodded. Slowly, the way a man nods when he's rebuilding his understanding of something fundamental. "That's why your kiln charcoal works better. More of the... ingredient."

"More of the ingredient. Less of everything else."

A silence. The forge breathed. The apprentice pumped. Somewhere outside, a bird was singing with the indifferent cheerfulness of a creature that had no idea it was witnessing the foundation of an industrial revolution.

"What else," Tobho said, "is in that book of yours?"

I smiled. The real smile, the one I usually kept hidden, the one that had nothing to do with being eight years old.

"Quite a lot, actually."

Over the following weeks, a pattern established itself. I'd visit the forge every morning. Tobho and I would discuss, argue, experiment, and occasionally produce something. The discussions were the best part: the collision of theoretical knowledge and practical experience, each filling the gaps in the other, creating understanding that neither could have reached alone.

Tobho taught me things The Book couldn't. The way a piece of metal sounded when it was at the right temperature, a specific ring, a resonance that changed as the internal structure transformed. The visual cues: the color gradations from dark red to cherry to orange to yellow to white, each corresponding to a temperature range that I knew numerically and he knew instinctively. The feel of the hammer, how the resistance of the metal under the blow told you about its carbon content, its grain structure, its readiness to be quenched.

I taught Tobho things his master couldn't. [I]Why[/I] quenching in water produced harder steel than quenching in oil (the rapid cooling locked the carbon atoms in a stressed crystalline structure called martensite, while slower cooling allowed them to settle into the softer pearlite formation). He didn't need the names. He needed the principle: fast quench, hard and brittle. Slow quench, tough and flexible. And the revelation that you could do both: quench fast for hardness, then [I]temper[/I] by reheating to a lower temperature to relieve some of the stress, producing steel that was hard enough to hold an edge but tough enough not to shatter.

He already did this, roughly. Most experienced smiths did, through trial and error accumulated over lifetimes. What he didn't have was the understanding that let him do it [I]consistently[/I]. The tempering colors: straw yellow at 220°C (428°F) for razors and files, brown at 260°C (500°F) for knives and scissors, purple at 280°C (536°F) for axes and chisels, blue at 300°C (572°F) for springs and saws. Each color indicating a specific temperature, a specific degree of stress relief, a specific balance of hardness and toughness.

"Colors," Tobho said, when I explained this. "You're telling me I should judge the temper by the color."

"The color changes because of how thick the oxide layer is on the surface. Thicker oxide, different color, higher temperature." I was pushing the boundary again, saying things that an eight-year-old shouldn't know, but Tobho had stopped treating me like an eight-year-old somewhere around the third day and had started treating me like a very short colleague with an unusually good library.

"Straw for cutting edges. Purple for chopping edges. Blue for things that bend." He recited it back the way he'd recite a recipe — a man fitting new knowledge into existing experience. "I've seen the colors. Always thought they were... incidental. Didn't know they [I]meant[/I] something."

"Everything means something. If you know how to read it."

He gave me a look. The skeptical look of the first day was gone. This was something deeper. Something that might, under the soot and the stubbornness and the wariness Tobho had built up over a life of being told what to do by people who understood less than he did, have been gratitude.

"You're a strange lad," he said.

"So I've been told."

"That's not an insult."

"I know."

The first truly good knife came off the anvil six weeks after we'd started. Medium-carbon steel, carburized for six hours, forged at welding heat, quenched in water, tempered to straw yellow. Tobho ground the edge himself, with the careful attention of a man who knew he was working material that deserved his best effort.

He tested it on a piece of leather, and the edge bit in clean, slicing with an ease that made his standard knives look like they were made of cheese. He shaved a curl of wood off a block of oak. He ran his thumb along the flat, feeling for the balance, the weight, the indefinable [I]rightness[/I] that a smith could sense and a metallurgist could only measure.

"It'll hold that edge for months," he said. "Maybe longer." He looked at me. "If I can make these consistently..."

"You can. Same process every time. Same charcoal, same soak time, same quench, same temper. The consistency is the point."

He nodded. Set the knife on the anvil. Picked it up again, as if reluctant to let it go.

"I've been making steel for thirty years," he said. "Good days and bad days. Some batches brilliant, some middling, never knew quite why. And you're telling me it's... recipe. Ingredients and method, same as baking bread."

"Better analogy than you think. The baker who measures his flour makes better bread than the baker who guesses."

A long pause. The forge popped and settled.

"I'm going to need more of that charcoal," Tobho said.

I grinned. "I'm going to need more of those knives."

Because knives were just the beginning. Knives were proof of concept. What came after knives was tools: better chisels, better files, better drill bits, better saw blades. The tools that built other tools. The instruments that cut wood and shaped stone and carved the type for a printing press that existed, for now, only in my notebook and my head and the impossibly distant top of The Tree.

But the trunk was growing. I could feel it. Root to trunk, charcoal to steel, theory to practice, a boy and a smith in a forge building the future one hammer blow at a time.

Tobho picked up his hammer. Pate pumped the bellows. And I sat on my upturned bucket, notebook on my knee, and began to sketch the next improvement.

A water-powered trip hammer. But that was a project for another day.
