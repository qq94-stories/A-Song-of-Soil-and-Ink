[H2]Chapter 10 — The Workshop[/H2]

The abandoned outbuilding near the southern garden had exactly three things going for it: a stone floor, a working chimney, and a door that locked.

It also had a leaking roof, a colony of mice that had clearly been in residence longer than any Tyrell, a smell that suggested something had died in the walls at some point in the recent geological past, and enough cobwebs to stock a Halloween store, if Halloween stores existed in Westeros, which they did not. But I was eight years old and operating from a position of limited real estate options, so three out of seven wasn't bad.

My mother gave permission the way she gave permission for most of my eccentricities, with a warm smile and a slight furrow between her brows that said [I]I don't entirely understand you, but you're mine and I love you and this seems harmless enough[/I].

"A study?" She turned the word over as if testing its weight. "Willas, you have a perfectly lovely room with a perfectly lovely desk."

"I need somewhere to work on projects." The word [I]projects[/I] did a lot of heavy lifting in that sentence. "Experiments. Things that might... make a mess."

The mess angle was calculated. Every mother in the world understood the logic of [I]better there than in the house[/I]. Alerie Tyrell was no exception. The furrow between her brows relaxed.

"That old shed by Harmon's garden?" She considered. "It hasn't been used in years. Your father used to keep hunting dogs there, before he decided the kennels were more suitable."

"It has a good chimney." This was the critical selling point and I dropped it casually, as if the chimney were a minor detail rather than the single most important feature of the building, the feature that would make everything else possible.

"I'll have the servants clean it out. And fix that roof; it leaked terribly when we had the autumn storms." She reached out and tucked a strand of hair behind my ear. She did that a lot lately, as if checking I was still the child she remembered. "Every lord's son needs his hobbies."

Every lord's son needs his hobbies. My father would use almost exactly the same words when he heard about it, and he would mean them in almost exactly the same way: as a benign dismissal, a filing of information under [I]harmless[/I] and [I]normal[/I] and [I]not worth further thought[/I]. The heir to Highgarden wanted to putter around in an outbuilding. Fine. Better than the alternative, which in Mace's mind was probably [I]not enough interest in jousting[/I].

Grandmother, when she heard, said nothing at all. She just looked at me across the breakfast table with those sharp dark eyes and gave the smallest nod, the kind that was invisible unless you were watching for it.

I was watching for it.

The cleaning took three days. Four servants, an arsenal of brooms and buckets, and a pitched battle with the mice that ended in negotiated withdrawal; they relocated to the stables, where the cats could deal with them through established diplomatic channels.

The roof was patched. The chimney was swept and inspected and declared sound by the castle's maintenance man, a taciturn fellow named Willam who communicated primarily through grunts and whose professional opinion was expressed as a single nod, which in Willam's vocabulary was practically a sonnet of approval.

And then it was mine. I stood in the doorway on the morning the last servant left, looking at a room roughly six metres by five (twenty feet by fifteen), with stone walls, a stone floor, a fireplace with a functional flue, two windows that admitted reasonable light when the shutters were open, and nothing else. Empty and clean and waiting.

The feeling that moved through me was not something I could have explained to anyone in this world, because it was the feeling of an engineer looking at a blank workshop for the first time. I had no specific workshop to be nostalgic for, no memory of a particular space from whatever life had preceded this one. The Book's knowledge wasn't memory in that sense. It was information without autobiography, skills without stories, expertise without experience.

But the [I]feeling[/I] was there. The bone-deep satisfaction of a space that was yours, where you could think and build and fail and try again without an audience. A laboratory. A forge of ideas, if you'll forgive the melodrama, which you probably shouldn't because I was eight years old standing in an empty shed having a moment and that's exactly as ridiculous as it sounds.

I got to work. Furniture first. A work table: broad, heavy, sturdy enough to take abuse. A smaller writing desk for notes. Shelving along one wall. A stool. These arrived over the following week, requisitioned from Highgarden's stores by a mother who was happy to indulge a son's hobby and a steward who had bigger things to worry about than a child's furniture requests.

The table was the key piece. Oak, scarred and pitted from its previous life in a storage room, with legs thick enough to support a grown man's weight. I had it placed beneath the larger window, where the light was best, and ran my hand across its surface with proprietary pleasure.

The books stayed in my room. Grandmother's gifts, the Qohorik treatise on metalworking, the Myrish text on lenses and glass. None of them belonged in a building that had leaked until last week, shared a wall with a chimney, and was about to neighbor a charcoal kiln. Books were expensive. Books were irreplaceable. And books turning up water-damaged or smoke-stained in an outbuilding would make people ask questions. What I brought to the workshop instead were notes: extracts copied onto loose parchment, diagrams redrawn from the originals, measurements and formulas transcribed in my own hand. If a page got singed or soaked, I could copy it again. If someone found a stack of a child's scribbled notes, they'd see a boy's messy homework, not a lord's son hoarding rare foreign texts in a shed.

My notebook went in a locked drawer. The key went on a cord around my neck, exactly where a child would keep a key to his secret drawer, making it both obvious and invisible.

Parchment. Quills. Ink. A set of basic tools (a small hammer, pliers, a hand drill, files of various roughness) acquired piecemeal over months through a combination of birthday requests, grandmother's quiet procurement, and the simple expedient of asking the castle smith if he had any old tools he didn't need anymore. He did. Smiths always did. Broken and worn-out tools accumulated in every workshop in every world, and craftsmen were always willing to give their old stock to an interested child.

By the end of the second week, the outbuilding had transformed from an empty shed into something that looked, to an uneducated eye, like a particularly well-organized child's playroom. Cluttered but purposeful. Tools and loose parchment covered in sketches and the general air of a space where someone was [I]doing something[/I], even if what that something was remained pleasantly vague.

To an educated eye, and there were very few of those at Highgarden, it looked like the beginning of something else entirely.

Harmon visited on the third day and stood in the doorway with the expression of a man who has been surprised and is not sure he likes it. He looked at the tools. The parchment sketches pinned to the wall. The table with its careful organization. Then he looked at me.

"My lord," he said, in a tone that hovered between question and statement.

"Harmon." I gestured to the room with what I hoped was casual pride. "What do you think?"

"I think," he said slowly, "that you're not the usual sort of child."

"Is that a problem?"

He scratched his jaw, the same gesture he'd made three years ago when I'd suggested planting beans next to wheat. The same calculation behind his eyes: cost of engaging versus cost of walking away.

"No, my lord," he decided. "Reckon it's the opposite."

Three weeks into my tenure as Highgarden's youngest shed owner, the first real project was charcoal.

This sounds unglamorous. It was unglamorous. Charcoal was not the stuff of songs or tapestries. No one had ever composed a ballad about carbon reduction or written an epic poem about pyrolysis. But charcoal was the foundation of everything I wanted to build, the way the alphabet was the foundation of everything anyone wanted to write, and you didn't skip the alphabet because it wasn't exciting enough.

Here was the problem: Highgarden, like every castle in Westeros, used charcoal. Lots of it. The smiths burned it. The kitchens burned it. The braziers that heated the halls in the rare cold months burned it. Charcoal was to a medieval economy what electricity was to... well, whatever economy had come before this life. Essential, ubiquitous, and entirely unremarkable.

And it was made badly. The charcoal burners who supplied Highgarden knew their trade the way Harmon knew his garden, through years of practice and accumulated instinct. They built their piles, covered them with earth and turf, lit them, and waited, and what came out the other end was charcoal that burned hotter and cleaner than raw wood and served its purpose well enough that no one questioned the process.

But [I]well enough[/I] wasn't the same as [I]good[/I]. The Book's sections on materials science and fuel chemistry were very clear on this point, and once you understood the actual chemistry of what was happening when wood became charcoal, "well enough" started to look a lot like "massively inefficient."

Charcoal production was pyrolysis. That was the technical term, and it meant: heating wood in the absence of oxygen until the volatile compounds (water, methane, various organic gases) boiled off, leaving behind a structure that was mostly pure carbon. The key was the [I]absence of oxygen[/I] part. Wood burned in open air was just fire: messy, relatively cool, inefficient. Wood heated without oxygen didn't burn. It [I]transformed[/I]. The water evaporated. The gases escaped. And what remained was charcoal: light, porous, and capable of burning at temperatures between 900 and 1,100°C (1,650 to 2,010°F).

For comparison, and comparison was the whole point: wood burned in a fireplace reached maybe 300-400°C (570-750°F). Good enough to heat a room, cook a meal, keep the cold at bay. Nowhere near enough to smelt metal properly, forge high-quality steel, or drive any of the industrial processes that separated an agricultural economy from one that could actually build things.

The difference between 400 degrees and 1,100 degrees was the difference between a world that worked iron into serviceable but mediocre tools and a world that could produce steel. Real steel. The kind that held an edge, took a temper, and didn't shatter when it hit something harder than itself.

Charcoal was the bottleneck, and the traditional method, the earth-mound kiln that the charcoal burners used, worked, but it was inconsistent. Temperature varied wildly across the pile. Some wood overburned to ash. Other pieces barely converted at all, remaining raw wood in a charcoal shell. The yield was poor: you put in a certain amount of wood and got back maybe 20 to 25 percent of that weight as usable charcoal, when a well-controlled process could achieve 30 to 35 percent.

The fix was not complicated. It was, in fact, embarrassingly simple, as were most of the improvements I planned. The earth-mound kiln's problems were about control, or the lack of it. Temperature couldn't be regulated. Airflow couldn't be managed. The burner lit the pile and hoped for the best, adjusting by opening or closing air holes in the earth covering based on the color and density of the smoke, a method that was remarkably skillful but ultimately guesswork.

What I wanted was a permanent kiln. Brick-built, with controlled air inlets at the base and a proper chimney at the top. A structure that could be loaded, sealed, lit, and managed through the entire burn cycle with precision instead of prayer.

The Book had diagrams for this. It always had diagrams. But an eight-year-old boy could not build a brick kiln by himself, no matter how detailed his reference material. I didn't resent the limitation. It was a fact, like gravity or Mace's appetite, and resenting facts was a waste of energy I could better spend working around them.

Harmon was the obvious choice. He knew nothing about kilns, being a gardener through and through, but he knew everyone who did. Three decades of managing the grounds meant he'd worked alongside every craftsman at Highgarden at one point or another. He knew who owed him favors, who did clean work, who could keep their mouth shut, and who would take a strange request from a lord's eight-year-old son without making it the talk of the servants' hall by supper. I didn't just need skills. I needed a door into the castle's working world that didn't involve me, the heir, walking up to a craftsman cold and trying to explain why I wanted a brick dome built behind the gardens.

I consulted him one afternoon, bringing him to the workshop and laying out my sketches on the work table with elaborate casualness.

"I want to build a better charcoal kiln," I said.

He looked at the sketches. Looked at me. Back at the sketches. His eyebrows performed a complicated maneuver that expressed several emotions simultaneously, not all of them positive.

"A kiln." He said it the way people said it when they were buying time to figure out whether to take you seriously. "You want to build a kiln."

"The charcoal we buy is inconsistent. Some batches are good, some are half-raw wood. A proper kiln would produce better charcoal every time." I tapped the sketch with a confidence I mostly felt. "Hotter burning, cleaner fuel, and more of it per load."

"And you figured this out... from a book."

"From several books." The Qohorik treatise, bless its foreign heart, actually did discuss metalworking fuels in enough detail to justify my interest. I'd copied the relevant diagrams onto my workshop notes, a prop in a performance that was becoming second nature. "The Free Cities use brick kilns. They control the air flow. The charcoal burns at higher temperatures, which makes their steel better."

This was true. Qohor's smiths were famous across Essos, and while their reputation rested partly on rumors of blood magic and partly on the mystique of reworking Valyrian steel, the mundane foundation was straightforward: they had better fuel, which meant better metal, which meant better everything downstream. Cause and effect, dressed up in mystery because mystery was more interesting than thermodynamics.

Harmon studied the sketch again. He wasn't a builder or a smith, but he was a practical man who'd spent decades managing physical projects in the garden, and he could read a simple design drawing well enough to grasp what I was proposing.

"You'd need a mason," he said. "Someone who can lay brick. And the bricks themselves."

"And a spot to build it." I'd already chosen the spot: a patch of open ground behind the workshop, well away from any building that could catch fire, with good drainage and access to the woodpile. "Somewhere Father won't notice and worry about."

Harmon snorted. It was the closest he ever came to commenting on Mace, and I treasured it. "Your lord father doesn't walk the grounds much, my lord."

No. He did not. Mace's engagement with Highgarden's infrastructure was limited to the parts visible from horseback at a distance, which meant the gardens looked beautiful and the walls were well-maintained and everything else existed in a pleasant blur of assumed functionality.

"Grandmother might help," I said, and Harmon's expression shifted. He knew exactly what [I]Grandmother might help[/I] meant in Highgarden's actual power structure. Olenna's involvement changed the calculation from [I]a child's project[/I] to [I]a project with backing[/I], and Harmon understood the difference instantly.

"I'll speak to Joram," he said. "Castle mason."

Harmon was as good as his word. Hours later, Joram the mason arrived. He was a thin, precise man who measured things twice and spoke once. He listened to my explanation without interrupting, examined my sketches with professional focus, and said, "This'll work."

Three words. A Willam-level communicator. I was developing a type.

The build took two weeks. Joram laid the brick carefully, deliberately, clearly believing that anything worth building was worth building slowly. I agreed with this in principle even as my eight-year-old impatience made me want to hand him bricks faster. The kiln took shape: a dome roughly 1.2 m (four feet) in diameter and 0.9 m (three feet) high at the peak, with a loading door at the front sealed by a fitted brick cover, four air inlets at the base that could be opened or closed with sliding plates, and a chimney pipe at the top fashioned from a section of old copper flue that I'd salvaged — with permission — from a dismantled kitchen vent.

The air inlets were the crucial innovation. They were, literally, holes with covers. Simple. But they allowed control. Open them wide and the burn ran hot and fast. Close them down and the temperature dropped, the pyrolysis slowed, the conversion deepened. With practice, you could manage the entire burn cycle from start to finish, producing charcoal of consistent quality in consistent quantity.

Theory, anyway. The first test would determine whether theory and practice were on speaking terms.

We loaded the kiln on a cool morning in what I reckoned was early 284 AC. Oak and beech in rough-split billets, stacked vertically around a central channel of kindling, the way the traditional charcoal burners stacked their earth-mound piles. Harmon helped. Joram watched with professional detachment; he'd delivered his commission and considered the subsequent experimentation someone else's problem. Two servants, young men from the grounds crew who'd been recruited by Harmon with the promise that this would be interesting, an optimistic promise, carried the wood and packed it according to my instructions.

I lit the kindling through the loading door. The fire caught, licked upward through the central channel, and within minutes the chimney was producing a thin column of white smoke.

White smoke. Good. That was water vapor, the first stage of pyrolysis, the wood drying out as the temperature climbed past 100°C (212°F). When the smoke turned yellow, that would be the organic volatiles (methane, tar vapor, acetic acid) burning off as the temperature pushed through 200 and toward the critical zone around 300-400°C (570-750°F) where the actual conversion happened.

I closed the loading door. Sealed it with mud and straw; Joram had shown me how, packing the mixture into the gaps with finicky precision. Joram did not approve of air leaks. Then I adjusted the base inlets to half-open and settled in to wait.

"How long?" Harmon asked.

"The books say eight to twelve hours for a kiln this size." In practice, I had no idea. The Book's information on charcoal production was detailed but general — it couldn't account for this specific wood, this specific kiln, these specific conditions. This was experiment, not recipe. We were going to learn by doing.

Harmon nodded. He understood [I]learn by doing[/I] in his bones, because gardening was nothing but learn by doing, season after season, and the man who thought he knew everything about soil was the man who was about to lose a crop to something he hadn't seen before.

The two servants looked at each other with the expressions of men who'd been promised interesting and were watching smoke come out of a brick dome.

"It gets more exciting later," I told them.

It did not get more exciting later. Charcoal production was, by nature, a slow and boring process, which was actually a point in its favor because boring processes were reliable processes, and reliability was what I was after. We watched the smoke change color: white to yellow, yellow to blue-grey as the temperature climbed and the heaviest volatiles burned off, and I adjusted the air inlets every hour or so, narrowing them as the burn progressed, reducing airflow to prevent the charcoal itself from catching fire and burning to ash.

This was the art. The part that the traditional charcoal burners understood instinctively and that I was learning from scratch with only theory to guide me. Too much air and the charcoal burned. Too little and the pyrolysis stalled, leaving unconverted wood in the center of the kiln. The balance point was narrow, and finding it required attention, patience, and a willingness to stand next to a hot brick dome for the better part of a day adjusting copper plates by centimetre increments.

The servants lost interest around hour four and found other things to do. Harmon stayed until midday, then left with the promise to return for the result. I stayed.

Because I wanted to. Because standing next to that kiln, watching the smoke, managing the burn, I was doing something [I]real[/I] for the first time. Something beyond planting beans and hoping, beyond preserving food and waiting. I was building a tool that would build other tools. The first link in a chain that ran from this brick dome all the way to the printing press at the top of The Tree.

The smoke turned thin and pale blue in the early evening. I closed all the inlets, sealed the chimney with a fitted cap, and left the kiln to cool overnight.

I barely slept. Which was absurd; I was eight, the kiln wasn't going anywhere, and the charcoal would still be there in the morning whether I tossed and turned or slept like the dead. But the anticipation was physical, a buzzing in my chest, and the rational part of my mind that understood patience in theory was losing a pitched battle with the part that wanted to run down to the workshop in my nightclothes and crack open the kiln by candlelight.

I didn't. Because sneaking out of the castle at night to examine charcoal was the kind of behavior that got noticed and remembered and filed away under [I]strange things the heir does[/I], and my filing cabinet of strange behavior was full enough already.

Morning came. I dressed. I ate breakfast slowly, deliberately, absolutely not vibrating with anticipation. I walked, did not run, to the workshop. Opened the kiln door.

Black. Uniformly black, from the outer billets to the innermost pieces near the central channel. I picked up a piece and turned it over. It was light, much lighter than the raw wood had been, because most of the volatile mass was gone, expelled as gas during the burn. The surface had the characteristic silvery sheen of well-converted charcoal, with the fine cracks and porous texture that indicated thorough pyrolysis.

I broke it. The fracture was clean, the interior the same uniform black as the surface. No brown core, which would have indicated unconverted wood. No grey-white ash, which would have indicated overburning. Clean and even all the way through.

The real test was simpler and more definitive. I took a piece to the workshop fireplace, arranged it in a small pile with kindling, and lit it.

It caught slowly; good charcoal was harder to ignite than raw wood, because the easy-burning volatiles were gone. But once it caught, it burned with a steady, intense, nearly smokeless heat that was visibly, palpably different from the charcoal we normally used. Hotter and cleaner by a margin that made the air above the fireplace shimmer and warp.

I held my hand near the flame at the distance where normal charcoal was warm. This charcoal was painful.

Harmon arrived while I was grinning at a fireplace like a lunatic. Dignified? No. Accurate? Absolutely.

"Well?" he asked.

"Look." I gestured at the fire. "Touch the air above it. Carefully."

He held his hand out, felt the heat, and pulled back. He was a gardener, not a smith, but he'd lit enough fires in his life to know the difference between [I]hot[/I] and [I]hotter than it should be[/I].

"That's from your kiln?"

"That's from our kiln." The possessive was deliberate. Harmon had helped build it, helped load it, and the fastest way to make someone invested in a project was to make it theirs.

"Huh." He looked at the fire. Looked at the charcoal bin by the fireplace, which held the ordinary stuff from the castle's regular supplier. Looked back at the fire.

"Huh," he said again, which was practically an ovation by Harmon standards.

I built a second batch. Then a third. The fourth batch I modified: more air initially, less at the end, and the quality improved again, the charcoal even more uniform, the yield slightly higher. The fifth batch I loaded with pine instead of oak, noting the differences: pine charcoal was lighter, burned faster, reached temperature more quickly. Oak charcoal was denser, burned slower, held its heat longer. Different fuels for different purposes.

I was keeping records. Detailed records. Each batch documented in my notebook with the weight of wood loaded, the burn time, the adjustments made to the air inlets, and the weight of charcoal produced. The ratio of charcoal out versus wood in climbed steadily from an initial 24 percent (already better than the traditional method's 20-odd percent) to a consistent 31 percent by the fifth batch.

Thirty-one percent. That meant for every 45 kg (100 lbs) of wood I put in, I got 14 kg (31 lbs) of charcoal out instead of twenty. A fifty percent improvement in yield from the same raw material.

The servants who'd been pressed into service for the first burn had talked, because servants always talked, and by the third week a few curious members of Highgarden's staff had wandered by to see what the lord's heir was doing with a brick dome and a lot of firewood. Most looked, shrugged, and left. One, a young man from the smith's workshop who'd come to investigate the unusual smoke, stayed to watch the entire fourth burn, asking questions that were intelligent enough to tell me he understood the significance of what he was seeing.

His name was Edwyn. He'd remember the kiln. I'd remember him.

The charcoal itself was the proof that made the case, the way the beans had been the proof three years earlier. You could argue with theory. You could dismiss a child's ideas. You couldn't argue with a fire that burned hotter and cleaner than anything the castle smith had ever used, produced from the same wood in the same quantity of time with less waste and more yield.

I sent a sample to the castle smith, anonymously at first, through Harmon, because anonymous was safer than attributed and I wanted the result to speak for itself. The smith burned it. And then he sent a message back to Harmon asking where in seven hells that charcoal had come from, because it was the best fuel he'd worked with in twenty years.

I smiled when Harmon relayed this. The grin of a boy pleased with a science project, which was true enough, even if it was also the grin of a man who had just taken the first step on a very long road.

My workshop. My kiln. My charcoal. The roots of The Tree, pushing deeper.

And the trunk could grow now.
