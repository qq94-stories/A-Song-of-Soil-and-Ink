## Chapter 5 — Beans and Dirt

The head gardener of Highgarden was a man named Harmon, and he had the kind of hands that tell you everything you need to know about a person's life.

Thick fingers, scarred and calloused, the nails permanently dark with soil no amount of scrubbing would shift. Knuckles swollen from decades of work in cold morning air. Palms like leather. These were hands that had planted, pruned, weeded, harvested, and buried more things than I could count, and they belonged to a man who knew the earth the way a sailor knows the sea — intimately, respectfully, and with the bone-deep awareness that it could kill you if you stopped paying attention.

I liked Harmon immediately. He was the first person I'd met at Highgarden who knew something I actually needed to learn.

Not the *what* — The Book had the what, had more whats than I'd ever need — but the *here*. Which soils drained fast and which held water. When the last frost came and how you could feel it in the morning air before it arrived. Which of Highgarden's gardens had been planted and replanted so many times that even the earthworms were fat. Local knowledge, practical and specific, the kind of thing that exists in people's hands and never makes it into books, any book, including The Book.

I found him in the kitchen garden on a morning so bright it felt like an assault. Spring in the Reach was a spectacle — everything green, everything growing, everything so aggressively alive that you half-expected the roses to start composing sonnets. Harmon was on his knees in the dirt, transplanting herb seedlings with the focused delicacy of a surgeon, which is exactly what he was, in a sense. Plant surgery. The original kind.

"Good morning," I said.

He looked up. Down. Up again, recalibrating for my height, which was not impressive. "Morning, my lord."

The *my lord* had a specific texture to it — the careful deference of a servant who'd worked at Highgarden long enough to know that lords' children wandered where they pleased and the safest course was mild acknowledgment followed by hoping they went away. I was not going to go away. I was going to be his worst nightmare: a child with questions.

"What are you planting?"

"Basil, my lord. And thyme." He held up a seedling, its roots dangling in a clump of dark soil. "For the kitchens."

"Why there?" I pointed to the spot he'd chosen. "And not there?" I pointed to a bare patch a few feet away.

He looked at me with an expression I would come to know very well over the following months. It was the expression of a man trying to figure out whether a question was genuine curiosity or some kind of test, and if it was a test, who was grading it.

"Sun," he said finally. "These need morning sun and afternoon shade. That patch gets too much of both."

A correct answer, delivered simply. I filed it away next to The Book's extensive section on photosynthesis and photoperiodism and decided that I liked this man very much.

***

I came back the next day. And the day after. And the day after that.

Harmon tolerated me the way a draft horse tolerates a particularly friendly sparrow. I sat in the dirt — actually sat in it, legs crossed, breeches ruined, my mother's laundresses adding another grievance to their list — and watched him work. I asked questions. Lots of questions. Mostly the right kind of questions, the ones that let him show me what he knew rather than reveal what I knew.

"Why do the peas grow on that trellis and not in the open ground?"

"Why do you pull those weeds but leave those ones?"

"What happens to the clippings? Where do they go?"

Reasonable questions. Child's questions. The kind of questions that a boy who'd been spending time in the library might ask after reading about agriculture in terms too general to be useful and wanting to see how it actually worked.

Harmon answered them all, slowly warming to the novelty of a lord's son who actually cared about the difference between compost and mulch. Most noble children who visited the gardens wanted to pick flowers or chase butterflies or, in one memorable incident he told me about, eat an entire bush of currants and then vomit spectacularly on a prize-winning rose.

I was, by comparison, a model visitor. I sat still. I listened. I didn't vomit on anything.

The trust built slowly. By the second week, Harmon had stopped adding "my lord" to every sentence and started treating me less like a curiosity and more like a very small apprentice. By the third week, he was explaining things without being asked — pointing out how the drainage worked, which beds got turned first in spring, why the orchard trees were spaced the way they were.

By the fourth week, I made my move.

"Harmon," I said, watching him plant a row of wheat seedlings in one of the small trial beds near the kitchen garden. Not the main fields — those were managed by the estate steward and his army of tenant farmers. These were Harmon's domain, experimental beds where he tested new varieties and techniques before recommending them for larger-scale use. The fact that Highgarden had experimental beds at all told me something important about the Tyrells' approach to agriculture: they cared, or at least they'd historically employed people who cared.

"My lord?"

"I read something in a book." The magic words. Not *I know something*. Not *you should do this*. A child read something in a book. Charming. Harmless. "It said that beans can help other plants grow. That if you put beans next to wheat, the wheat does better."

He stopped planting. Looked at me.

The thing about companion planting is that it's one of those ideas that sounds too simple to be true, which is exactly why it works. Legumes — beans, peas, lentils — fix atmospheric nitrogen through symbiotic bacteria in their root nodules. Genus *Rhizobium*, The Book told me helpfully, as if I were going to walk up to a medieval gardener and say *actually, Harmon, it's about the Rhizobium bacteria converting diatomic nitrogen into ammonium ions through the enzyme nitrogenase, and the plant provides carbon compounds in exchange*. I was not going to say that. I was going to say "beans help wheat" and make wide, innocent eyes.

"Beans next to wheat," Harmon repeated.

"It was in one of the maester's books." It was not. "Something about the soil getting better where beans grow." Something about everything, really — nitrogen fixation, improved soil structure from the root systems, the bean plants providing ground cover that reduced water evaporation, and a dozen other synergistic effects that I couldn't mention because they relied on concepts this world hadn't invented yet.

Harmon scratched his jaw. The calluses on his fingers rasped against grey stubble. "My da used to say something like that," he said slowly. "That peas were good neighbors. Never thought much on it."

My heart rate increased. His father had noticed it. Generations of farmers had probably noticed it — the folk wisdom was there, scattered and unsystematic, mixed in with dead cats and moon planting and singing to wheat. The observation existed. What didn't exist was the understanding, the method, the discipline to isolate the observation and test it properly.

"Could we try?" I asked. Wide eyes. Slight bounce on my heels, because five-year-olds bounce when they're excited, and I was excited, genuinely excited, which made the performance easier. "Just one bed? Beans and wheat together, and wheat by itself, and we could see which one grows better?"

A controlled experiment. Test plot and control plot. The absolute foundation of the scientific method, proposed by a child who just wanted to play in the dirt.

Harmon looked at me for a long moment. Then he shrugged.

"Can't see the harm in it, my lord. We've got the seed and the space."

It cost nothing to try. That was the beauty of it. I was asking for something so small, so insignificant, that refusing would be stranger than agreeing. A lord's son wants to plant some beans? Let him plant beans. He'll lose interest in a week and move on to wooden swords or tormenting the stable cats or whatever it is that noble boys do.

I did not lose interest.

***

We planted two beds the following morning. Each about four paces long, two wide — tiny patches in Highgarden's vast garden complex, barely visible between the ornamental borders and the kitchen herb rows. One bed: wheat seedlings alone, spaced the way Harmon always spaced them. The other: wheat seedlings interplanted with broad beans, alternating rows, the beans tucked in between the wheat at intervals I'd calculated from The Book's companion planting tables and then rounded to numbers that looked like a child's random guesses.

"I think one bean for every three wheats," I said, pretending to count on my fingers. "Is that right?"

"Good as any," Harmon said, which was his way of saying he had no idea and didn't much care but was willing to humor the small lord who was getting dirt on his expensive tunic.

I planted my beans myself. My hands were small and my technique was terrible, but Harmon let me do it, correcting my depth and spacing with patient taps of his trowel. The soil was cool and damp under my fingers, rich and dark, and something in me settled at the contact. Dirt. Real dirt. The beginning of everything.

Then came the part that would have been easy in any rational system and was agonizingly difficult here: documentation.

"I want to draw pictures of them," I told Harmon. "Every week. To see how they grow."

He gave me the look again — the one that was recalibrating, trying to fit this child into categories he understood. But drawing pictures was a normal child activity. Harmless. Endearing, even.

"As you like, my lord."

I started a new section in my notebook. Date — or as close to a date as I could manage, since the Westerosi calendar was a thing I was still getting my head around. Weather. Soil condition: wet, dry, somewhere between. A rough sketch of each bed, showing seedling positions. Height measurements, taken with a piece of string I'd knotted at intervals because rulers didn't exist in any standardized form and I wasn't about to invent the metric system at age five.

I measured the wheat. I measured the beans. I noted which seedlings had sprouted and which hadn't. I recorded rainfall when I could tell it had rained and sunlight in vague, qualitative terms — bright day, cloudy day, that sort of thing. Not precise. Not rigorous by any standard The Book would recognize. But *consistent*, which was more than anyone in this world was doing with their agricultural observations.

The scientific method, stripped to its bare minimum: observe, record, compare. Not elegant. Not sophisticated. Just *systematic*, which was the one thing this world's knowledge desperately lacked.

Harmon watched me measure and sketch and scribble, and said nothing, which was the most gracious possible response. A lesser man might have laughed. A more suspicious one might have reported the behavior to the maester or my parents. Harmon simply went about his work and let me go about mine, and if he thought the heir to Highgarden was a strange little boy, he kept that opinion where it belonged — between himself and his spade.

***

The weeks passed.

I learned patience. Real patience, not the theoretical kind I'd been practicing in the library. Plants don't care about your timeline. They don't care that you know the exact mechanism by which nitrogen-fixing bacteria convert atmospheric N2 into bioavailable ammonium. They grow at their own pace, in their own time, according to their own ancient chemistry, and all you can do is provide the conditions and wait.

I watered both beds equally. Harmon helped, because watering was a two-hand job and my two hands could barely manage a child-sized pail. The soil was good — Highgarden soil was almost criminally good, centuries of cultivation in one of the most fertile river valleys on the continent — and both beds sprouted quickly, green shoots breaking the surface within days.

By the second week, I could see a difference. Slight. Subtle. The kind of thing you'd miss if you weren't looking for it, and nobody was looking for it except me.

The wheat in the companion bed was a shade darker green.

Not dramatically — not the kind of difference that would make someone stop and stare. Just a slight deepening of color, a hint of the more vigorous chlorophyll production that comes with better nitrogen availability. The wheat in the control bed was fine. Normal. Healthy by any standard Harmon would recognize. The wheat in the companion bed was just... a little more.

I didn't say anything. I sketched. I measured — the companion wheat was a finger's width taller on average, which could have been natural variation or could have been the beginning of what The Book promised. I noted it in my notebook and kept my mouth shut.

By the fourth week, the difference was clearer.

The companion bed's wheat was visibly taller. More vigorous. The stalks were thicker, the leaves broader, the overall impression one of plants that were getting something their neighbors weren't. The beans themselves were thriving, their root systems quietly hosting the microscopic symbiosis that was feeding nitrogen to the soil, which was feeding the wheat, which was growing in the way that wheat grows when it actually has enough of what it needs.

Harmon noticed. He didn't say anything directly — he was a man who spoke through actions, not words — but I caught him standing between the two beds one morning, looking back and forth, frowning the frown of a man whose assumptions were being quietly rearranged.

"They look well," he said when he saw me watching him.

"The ones with the beans look better," I said, because even a child could see it by now.

He grunted. Noncommittal. But he kept looking.

***

By the sixth week, there was no ambiguity.

The companion bed was outperforming the control by every measure I could track. Taller stalks. More tillers per plant. Deeper green color. The beans had also begun flowering, small white blossoms that attracted pollinators, which was another benefit I hadn't mentioned because explaining pollination ecology to a medieval gardener through the mouth of a five-year-old was a bridge too far even for me.

I measured. I sketched. I recorded. My notebook pages for the experiment were filling up with data that would have been barely adequate for a middle school science fair and was, by the standards of Westerosi agricultural research, the most rigorous field trial anyone had ever conducted.

And nobody cared.

That was the thing. Nobody noticed because nobody was looking. The kitchen garden existed in Highgarden's consciousness as a background function — food appeared on tables, gardens existed, someone maintained them. The connection between the two was understood in the vague, abstract way that people understand where their food comes from, which is to say: barely.

Mace didn't visit the gardens. Alerie visited the ornamental ones — the roses, the flowering borders, the sculpture gardens with their fountains and hedgerows — but not the kitchen garden, which was a servant's domain and therefore beneath notice. The maester had his own concerns. The steward tracked harvests at the estate level, not the individual-bed level. The cooks wanted produce delivered to the kitchen and didn't care how it got there.

My experiment could have produced wheat made of solid gold and nobody would have noticed until it showed up in the soup.

I noticed, though. And Harmon noticed, in his quiet way.

"Might do it again next year," he said one afternoon, almost casually, as we stood together looking at the two beds. The companion bed was now so obviously superior that denying the difference would have required active self-deception. "Bigger, maybe. In the outer beds."

My heart hammered. *Bigger.* He wanted to scale it up. Not because a five-year-old told him to, but because his own eyes told him it worked. The evidence did the persuading. I'd just created the conditions for the evidence to exist.

"That would be interesting," I said, which was the understatement of several centuries.

"Your da's beans," Harmon added, and I realized with a jolt that he was talking about his own father, the one who'd said peas were good neighbors. He was connecting this to his own history, his own experience, his own inherited wisdom. He was making it *his*, which was exactly what I needed. An innovation that comes from the lord's strange child is a curiosity. An innovation that comes from the head gardener's own family tradition is sustainable.

"Your father sounds like he was smart," I said.

"He was a gardener," Harmon said, as if that answered the question.

It did.

***

That evening, I sat in my room with my notebook and a candle and the quiet satisfaction of a man who'd just watched the first domino fall.

It was tiny. One small garden bed. A few rows of beans. A fractional improvement in wheat yield that wouldn't feed a single extra person, wouldn't change the Reach's agricultural output by any measurable margin, wouldn't even register as a rounding error in the grand calculus of Highgarden's harvest.

But it worked.

The science was real. The knowledge in The Book translated to the soil in this world. Nitrogen fixation didn't care that I was in Westeros instead of... wherever The Book's world was. Chemistry was chemistry. Biology was biology. The laws that governed plant growth were the same here as anywhere, and that meant everything else in The Book was real too. The crop rotation systems. The composting techniques. The selective breeding programs. The irrigation designs. The soil amendment strategies.

All of it. All real. All waiting.

I turned to a fresh page in my notebook and started writing. Not in my careful child's scrawl this time — there was nobody to see, the candle was guttering, and I needed to think on paper faster than I could maintain the disguise.

*Companion planting — confirmed. Beans improve wheat yield, visual observation, 6-week trial.*

Below that: *Next steps.*

The list that followed was longer than anything I'd written before. Crop rotation plans for different soil types. Composting systems scaled from garden to field. Seed selection protocols — saving the best seeds from the best plants, generation after generation, a slow artificial selection that would improve yields incrementally but consistently. Cover cropping for winter months. Green manure — growing plants specifically to plow them back into the soil. Drainage improvements for the wetter fields near the Mander. And on. And on.

None of it revolutionary. That was the beauty. Every item on the list was simple, proven, low-risk, and explainable without reference to any theory more complicated than "I tried it and it worked." No machines. No technologies. No inventions that would make people ask where a child learned to build something that didn't exist. Just better farming practices, introduced gradually, tested visibly, scaled slowly.

The boring revolution. The quiet one. The kind that changes everything and that nobody writes songs about.

I blew out the candle and lay in the dark and thought about the beans growing in their bed, roots tangled with wheat roots, the invisible bacteria doing their invisible work in the invisible soil, and I smiled.

Nobody noticed but me.

That was fine. For now, that was exactly right.
