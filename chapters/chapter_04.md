## Chapter 4 — The Library

The library at Highgarden smelled like dust and ambition.

Not the ambition of the lords who'd built it — they'd been dead for centuries, their contributions fossilized into leather and vellum. No, the ambition belonged to whoever had decided that a castle in the most fertile region of the continent needed a room full of books more than it needed another granary. I respected that person. I also suspected they'd been a second son with too much time and too little responsibility, but that was probably projection.

I found the library on a Tuesday. Or whatever they called Tuesdays here — the days blurred together when your primary obligations were eating porridge, napping, and not falling down stairs. I'd been exploring the castle in the way five-year-olds explore things: with purpose disguised as wandering. The servants had long since stopped worrying about me getting lost. The heir to Highgarden toddles where he pleases, apparently, so long as he toddles back before supper.

The door was heavy oak, iron-banded, and slightly ajar. I pushed it open with both hands because my arms had the structural integrity of cooked noodles, and stepped inside.

Three walls of shelves. Floor to ceiling. Maybe two hundred volumes, plus loose scrolls in wicker baskets and stacked parchments bound with twine. A reading table near the window, its surface scarred with candle wax and ink stains. A cushioned chair that had seen better decades. Morning light pouring through leaded glass, catching motes that swirled like tiny, literate ghosts.

Two hundred books.

In a world where a single volume might cost what a smallfolk family earned in a year, this was wealth. This was Highgarden saying *we can afford to let knowledge sit in a room and gather dust*. Most castles in Westeros probably had a shelf. Maybe two. The Citadel had tens of thousands, but they hoarded knowledge the way dragons hoarded gold — fiercely, jealously, and with the firm conviction that nobody else could be trusted with it.

Two hundred books. I wanted to cry, and I wasn't entirely sure if it was joy or grief.

Joy, because this was a starting point. Grief, because The Book — my perfect, impossible mental encyclopedia — contained more actionable knowledge in its section on soil chemistry alone than this entire room probably held in total. And I couldn't tell anyone that. I couldn't even hint at it. I was five, and I'd found a library, and the appropriate reaction was wonder.

So I wondered. Loudly.

"Books!" I said, because even a bright five-year-old would state the obvious. I ran my fingers along the spines of the nearest shelf, standing on tiptoe to reach the third row up. Leather, mostly. Some cloth-bound. A few that felt like they might disintegrate if I breathed too hard near them.

The maester found me there an hour later, sitting cross-legged on the floor with a treatise on Reach geography open across my lap. I'd chosen it deliberately — a lord's heir interested in his own lands was endearing, not alarming. The maps were crude but recognizable. The Mander River, the Honeywine, the Roseroad. Highgarden at the center like a spider in a very green, very fertile web.

"My lord Willas." Maester Lomys stood in the doorway, hands folded into his sleeves. He was a tall man running to softness at the middle, with a chain of many links and an expression that suggested he'd found a dog where he expected a book. "You've discovered the library."

"There are maps," I said, holding up the book like a trophy. "Of *our* lands."

The possessive pronoun was calculated. *Our* lands. Not just pretty pictures in a book — *mine*, or as close to mine as a five-year-old could claim. Let him see the heir taking interest in his inheritance. Let him report that to my father with a warm glow of approval.

Lomys smiled. It was the smile of a man who'd taught children of noble houses before and knew how this went: the initial enthusiasm, the gradual drift toward boredom, the inevitable return to wooden swords and riding lessons. He'd seen plenty of young lords "discover" the library. Most discovered it once and never came back.

"The Reach is well-mapped," he said, settling into the chair with the ease of long habit. "Lord Harlan Tyrell commissioned a full survey in the time of the Old King. Every river, every road, every holdfasting."

"Can you teach me to read them properly? The symbols, I mean."

He could. He did. And thus began the phase of my infiltration that I privately called Operation Look Natural.

***

The trick to hiding in plain sight is giving people a story they already want to believe.

Maester Lomys wanted to believe that his lord's heir was bright, studious, and eager to learn about the Reach. This was true. He also wanted to believe that this interest was normal — precocious, certainly, but within the bounds of what a clever child might do. This was less true, but I worked very hard to keep it plausible.

I came to the library three or four times a week. Never every day — that would seem obsessive. I read what a young lord should read: the geography of the Reach, the histories of House Tyrell, the lineages of the major bannermen. Lomys guided my reading with the gentle hand of a man who assumed he was in control of the curriculum.

He wasn't.

Every approved text was also a reconnaissance mission. When I read the history of House Tyrell, I was really cataloguing which lords had been innovative and which had been conservative, building a map of institutional attitudes that might help or hinder me decades from now. When I read the geography, I was cross-referencing river systems with what The Book knew about water-powered mills, irrigation networks, and seasonal flood patterns. When I read the lineages, I was looking for marriage alliances, trade relationships, and the invisible web of obligation and resentment that held the feudal system together.

The approved reading was the cover. The real work happened in the gaps.

I'd ask a question about the Mander's flooding patterns — innocent enough, rivers flood, children are curious about floods — and Lomys would explain the annual cycle. Snowmelt from the mountains, spring rains, the way the river swelled and deposited rich soil along its banks. I'd nod and ask another question, and another, and he'd mistake my systematic extraction of hydrological data for childish fascination.

"Why do the farmers plant after the floods?" I asked one morning, knowing exactly why, needing to know what *he* knew about it.

"The waters leave good soil behind," Lomys said. "Rich and dark. The fields nearest the river produce the finest harvests."

Alluvial deposits. Silt and organic matter carried downstream, spread across the floodplain, creating some of the most fertile agricultural land possible. He knew the *what*. He didn't know the *why* — didn't know about nutrient transport, mineral composition, the specific role of nitrogen and phosphorus in plant growth. He knew that river-mud grew good wheat the way a peasant knew that the sun rose in the east: as fact, not mechanism.

The gap between knowing *that* and knowing *why* was the gap between this world and The Book.

It was enormous.

***

I should talk about the books themselves, because they were fascinating in the way a time capsule is fascinating — you learn as much from what's missing as from what's there.

Highgarden's library was, by Westerosi standards, excellent. Two hundred-odd volumes spanning history, geography, heraldry, theology, some natural philosophy, a surprising amount of poetry (the Reach bred poets like it bred roses — prolifically and with an emphasis on appearance over substance), and a handful of practical texts on agriculture and animal husbandry.

The practical texts were what I was really after. And they were... well.

*On the Tilling of Fields*, attributed to a Maester Gareth who'd served Highgarden a century past, contained exactly three useful observations buried in forty pages of received wisdom and outright superstition. The useful bits: plant legumes and grain in alternating seasons (crop rotation, sort of, arrived at by accident and tradition rather than understanding), manure improves soil (true), and fields need rest (also true, though the reasoning was about the soil's "vital humors" needing to regenerate, which was not true in any way that mattered).

The rest was folklore wearing scholarly robes. Plant by the phases of the moon. Sing to the wheat at dawn. Bury a dead cat at the corner of a new field for luck. I read that last one three times to make sure I hadn't hallucinated it.

I had not.

*A Treatise on the Beasts of the Field*, covering animal husbandry, was marginally better. Whoever had written it had actually observed animals, which put them ahead of Maester Gareth and his dead cats. Breeding practices were described in enough detail to be useful, though the understanding of heredity was — unsurprisingly — nonexistent. They knew that breeding a strong bull to a strong cow tended to produce strong calves. They did not know why, and their guesses ranged from "the gods' favor" to "vital essences mingling in the womb" to one memorable passage that I think was trying to describe genetics but got lost somewhere around humoral theory and never found its way back.

Mendel wouldn't be born for centuries. Assuming Mendel existed in whatever version of reality I now inhabited, which was an assumption I tried not to think about too hard.

The point was this: the knowledge existed, but it was fragmented, unsystematic, and buried under layers of tradition and superstition like a diamond encased in mud. Every useful fact was surrounded by ten useless ones, and there was no framework for telling them apart. No scientific method. No controlled experiments. No understanding that you could *test* whether burying dead cats actually helped your harvest by burying a cat in one field and not in another and comparing the results.

They had observation without methodology. Data without analysis. Centuries of accumulated experience and absolutely no way to separate the signal from the noise.

And The Book — my perfect, crystalline mental encyclopedia — held the signal. All of it. Every agricultural technique humanity had ever developed, from the Neolithic revolution to modern industrial farming, organized and indexed and illustrated with the kind of clarity that made me want to weep with gratitude and scream with frustration in equal measure.

I knew how to build a seed drill. A winnowing machine. A proper crop rotation system based on actual soil chemistry rather than lunar phases and folk superstition. I knew about selective breeding, grafting techniques, irrigation systems, drainage, composting, nitrogen fixation, and a hundred other things that could transform the Reach from the breadbasket of Westeros into something the word *breadbasket* couldn't even begin to describe.

I knew all of this.

I was five.

I couldn't build a seed drill. I couldn't even lift a seed drill. I could barely lift the books that told me nothing I didn't already know, because my arms were the arms of a child who'd recently survived a fever that nearly killed him, and the heaviest thing I should be lifting was a spoon.

The frustration was physical. It lived in my chest like a second heartbeat, tight and persistent. Some nights I lay in bed and mentally paged through The Book — turning pages that existed only in my mind, studying diagrams for machines I wouldn't be able to build for years, reading agricultural tables for experiments I couldn't run, absorbing engineering principles I couldn't apply — and the gap between knowledge and capability felt like standing on one side of the Mander and seeing everything I needed on the other bank with no bridge and no boat and arms too short to swim.

But I was also, I reminded myself, five. I had time. Assuming I didn't die of some medieval childhood disease or get trampled by a horse or fall off a wall or succumb to any of the thousand things that killed children in a world without antibiotics, I had decades. The gap would close. Not all at once, not quickly, but it would close.

Patience. The most unnatural virtue for someone who could see exactly where they needed to go and couldn't take a single step.

***

The notebook started as an accident.

Lomys had given me parchment and a quill for practicing my letters — the approved curriculum for a five-year-old heir included basic literacy, and I performed the role with only slight exasperation at forming letters I could have written in my sleep. My handwriting was deliberately terrible. A child's scrawl, thick and uneven, the quill gripped in a fist because fine motor control at this age was genuinely difficult and also because a child who wrote in a clean, adult hand would raise questions I couldn't answer.

But I needed to write things down. Not because I'd forget — The Book never changed, never faded, never lost a page. My mental encyclopedia was as perfect today as it had been the day I woke from the fever, and I had every reason to believe it would stay that way. The problem wasn't memory. The problem was *planning*.

The Book was a reference, not a roadmap. It told me how to build a waterwheel but not when, or in what order, or which prerequisite technologies I'd need first, or how to convince the people around me that a waterwheel was worth building. For that, I needed to think. And thinking, for me, worked better on paper than in my head.

So I started a notebook.

The cover was plain leather, small enough to fit in the pocket of my tunic. I'd asked my mother for it — "for my letters, Mother, Maester Lomys says I should practice" — and she'd provided it with a kiss on my forehead and a smile that made me feel like the worst kind of liar. Which I was, technically. The best lies are the ones wrapped in truth, and I was practicing my letters. I was just also doing other things.

The first page was genuine practice. Wobbly letters, my name written six times in increasingly legible versions. Evidence, in case anyone looked.

The second page was a drawing of a flower. Also evidence. Also genuine — the gardens of Highgarden were spectacular, and drawing them was both good cover and good practice for the technical sketches I'd eventually need.

The third page was where the real work began.

I wrote in a code that wasn't really a code — just terrible handwriting combined with abbreviations that would look like a child's nonsensical scribbles to anyone who glanced at them. Which was, functionally, everyone. Nobody studies a five-year-old's notebook with cryptographic intent. The best cipher in the world is being underestimated.

The list started small.

*Soil.* Underneath, in my scrawl: *beans + wheat? compost. worms. test.*

*Water.* Below that: *mill? river. power. grinding.*

*Tools.* Just the word, followed by a sketch that might have been a wheelbarrow or might have been a very ugly dog. Even I wasn't sure, and I'd drawn it.

*Food.* This one got more detail: *preservation. salt. smoke. drying. vinegar?*

*Health.* Two words below: *wash hands.*

That last one might save more lives than everything else on the list combined. The single most impactful piece of knowledge in The Book wasn't a machine or an agricultural technique or an engineering marvel — it was the germ theory of disease, and its simplest application was *washing your hands*. Doctors in my... in the world The Book came from... had killed patients for centuries by going from autopsies to deliveries without washing up. The solution was soap and water. The barrier was convincing people that invisible things could hurt them when they lived in a world where the dominant medical theory involved imbalanced humors and bad smells.

I'd get to that. Eventually. When I was old enough that suggesting hand-washing wouldn't result in a concerned maester prescribing me more bed rest.

The list grew over weeks. Each addition was small, coded, and deliberately messy. Some pages I tore out and burned in my bedroom candle — not because they contained anything incriminating, but because building the habit of operational security at age five meant it would be second nature by the time it actually mattered.

I was, I reflected one evening while watching a page of notes curl and blacken in the flame, possibly the most paranoid kindergartener in the history of any world.

That seemed fine. Paranoid kindergarteners survive.

***

Lomys, to his credit, noticed that my interests were unusual. He didn't find them alarming — I was careful about that — but he noticed.

"You have a great interest in the land, my lord," he said one afternoon, watching me trace the course of the Mander on a map with my finger. I'd asked him about the river towns, the ones that handled grain shipments to Oldtown and beyond. Trade routes, volumes, seasonal patterns. The questions of a future lord, but a future lord about ten years ahead of schedule.

"Father says the Reach feeds the realm," I said. Simple words, a child repeating what he'd heard at supper. Mace did say things like that, usually while eating enough to feed a small village himself. "I want to understand how."

Lomys adjusted his chain — a nervous habit, the links clicking softly against each other. Tin, copper, iron, silver, and several I couldn't identify by sight. Each one representing a field of study, years of work at the Citadel. He was not a stupid man. Conservative, traditional, set in the Citadel's ways of thinking — but not stupid.

"Most boys your age want to understand swords," he said. Not a rebuke. An observation, delivered with the careful neutrality of a man who served at his lord's pleasure and knew that Mace Tyrell's heir showing interest in agriculture rather than martial pursuits might create complications.

"I like swords too," I lied. "But you can't eat a sword."

He blinked. Then he laughed — a short, surprised sound, quickly controlled. "No," he agreed. "No, you cannot."

I'd surprised him. That was dangerous but also useful. Surprising people makes them remember you, and being remembered as the boy who said clever things about farming was a much better reputation than being remembered as the boy who knew things he shouldn't.

"Could you teach me about the harvests?" I pressed, pushing exactly as far as a precocious child might push before losing interest. "How much wheat we grow? Where it goes?"

He could. He did. And the numbers he gave me were... educational.

Not the numbers themselves — I already had a rough sense of the Reach's agricultural output from context, from overheard conversations, from the sheer scale of Highgarden's kitchens and the endless wagons I'd seen on the roads from my window. No, what was educational was the imprecision. Lomys spoke in generalities. "The Reach produces more grain than any other kingdom." True, but useless. How much more? Compared to what baseline? Measured how? Tracked where?

Nobody was counting. Not really. The lords knew what their lands produced in the way that a man knows his purse is heavy or light — by feel, not by figure. Taxes were collected in kind: a portion of the harvest, delivered to the lord's granary, assessed by men who'd been doing it for decades and knew roughly what a good year looked like versus a bad one. It worked. It had always worked. It was also, from the perspective of anyone who'd ever seen a spreadsheet, absolutely insane.

I made a note in my notebook that evening, crouched by candlelight with my quill.

*Counting. Nobody counts. MUST count.*

Underneath, in slightly clearer handwriting than I should have used: *You cannot improve what you do not measure.*

The Book had a whole section on management theory. Most of it was irrelevant to a feudal agricultural society — I didn't need to know about quarterly earnings reports or stakeholder engagement frameworks. But the core principle translated perfectly: data precedes optimization. You measure, then you improve. You track, then you adjust. You count, because counting is how you turn "it seems like the harvest was good" into "the harvest produced twelve thousand bushels, which is eight hundred more than last year and four thousand less than we'd get with proper crop rotation and soil management."

The gap between this world and the one in The Book wasn't just technology. It wasn't just science. It was *methodology*. These people were smart — they'd built castles and ships and cities and a civilization that spanned a continent. But they'd done it without the single most powerful tool in the intellectual arsenal.

They didn't know how to know things systematically.

I did.

And I was going to introduce the concept of writing things down and comparing them to other things you'd written down, and it was going to be the most boring revolution in history, and nobody was going to see it coming because nobody worries about a five-year-old with a notebook.

***

That night, I sat in bed with the notebook on my knees and the candle burning low, and I read through my list.

Soil improvement. Water power. Better tools. Food preservation. Basic hygiene. Crop rotation. Animal husbandry. Record-keeping. Measurement. Composting. Seed selection. Drainage. Irrigation.

And those were just the agricultural entries. I hadn't even started on metalworking, construction, textiles, medicine, education, economics, or the hundred other categories where The Book held knowledge that could transform this world from the ground up.

The list was very long.

I was very small.

I closed the notebook, blew out the candle, and lay in the dark with the ceiling invisible above me, listening to the wind move through Highgarden's gardens and the distant sound of the Mander running south toward the sea.

Somewhere in this castle, my father was probably regaling some poor bannerman with the tale of his last tourney, or his plans for the next feast, or whatever occupied a man whose primary talents were eating and being loud. Somewhere, my mother was reading or embroidering or doing whatever graceful things Alerie Tyrell did in the evenings. Somewhere, my baby brother was sleeping the sleep of a two-year-old who'd spent the day putting things in his mouth and shouting at birds. And somewhere, I was certain, my grandmother was sitting in a chair and thinking thoughts that would terrify the people around her if they could hear them, the way grandmothers do.

And here I was. Five years old, with a child's body and an engineer's mind and a notebook full of ideas that wouldn't mean anything for years.

The list was very long. The night was very dark. And I had so much work to do that the only sane response was to close my eyes, go to sleep, and start again tomorrow.

So I did.
